3. Topics He Is an Expert In — Where You Must Be Airtight

This section is ordered by how dangerous a weak answer would be.

3A. LLM Continued Pretraining — HIGHEST RISK (his daily work)

He has run this experiment. You need to be able to discuss:

Why 10% of original LR? The e-Llama paper ablation (arXiv 2501.09706) shows:

  • LR = 3.0e-3 (10× original): catastrophic forgetting — general NLU drops from ~43 to 34.5
  • LR = 3.0e-4 (1× original): moderate forgetting
  • LR = 3.0e-5 (0.1× original): best balance — e-commerce improves ~25%, general shows some degradation, especially for the smaller 8B (NLU stays roughly comparable)

The mechanism: a large LR during continued pretraining moves parameters far from the pretrained optimum in parameter space, overwriting the prior distribution. Low LR allows the model to absorb domain signal without erasing the posterior from the original pretraining corpus.

Why 50% e-commerce / 50% general data mix? Ablation shows that going to 75% e-commerce delivers negligible incremental e-commerce gain (57.5 → 57.6 on e-com score) but starts degrading general NLU. The replay of general data acts as a regularizer against forgetting.

Checkpoint averaging. Simple mean of multiple checkpoint parameter vectors often outperforms the best single checkpoint. Explanation: SGD/Adam trace a noisy path through loss landscape; averaging smooths the noise and can approach a wider loss basin — empirically more robust. eBay uses it in LiLiuM.

Model merging (for deployment-time tuning). The eBay e-Llama paper (arXiv:2501.09706) shows that linearly interpolating between base Llama-3.1 and e-Llama gives a near-linear Pareto tradeoff between domain and general performance — effectively free tuning at deployment time (no retraining). Know: SLERP, DARE, TIES-merging, task arithmetic as the broader landscape; be able to say why simple averaging works here (models are close in parameter space because continued pretraining did not move them far).

Loss spikes during pretraining. Common causes: (a) data quality — a batch of anomalous examples; (b) learning rate too high; (c) gradient norm explosion (fix: clip at ~1.0); (d) in MoE models, router softmax logit drift → unconstrained gating → gradient variance spikes. Checkpoint averaging or mid-training restarts are typical mitigations.

3D parallelism. He used Megatron-LM on 480 H100s. Know conceptually: data parallelism (replicate model, split batch), tensor parallelism (split weight matrices across GPUs, used for single large layer), pipeline parallelism (split layers across stages, with micro-batches filling the pipeline bubbles). Flash-Attention-2 for memory (avoids materializing full attention matrix). Mixed precision BF16 (not FP16 for LLMs, because BF16 has larger exponent range, avoiding overflow on large activations).

3B. Tokenizer Design and Vocabulary

The vocabulary customization paper (Sept 2025 (arXiv preprint), arXiv 2509.26124) is Versley's most specific technical contribution at eBay. Know it cold.

The fertility problem. Standard BPE (trained on general web text) segments domain-specific terms into multiple subwords. For example, an e-commerce brand name or a product category term like "SwiGLU" or "sneakerhead" might split into 4–6 tokens. More tokens → more autoregressive steps → higher latency. LiLiuM's custom 65k vocabulary → 34% generation speedup vs. LLaMA-2 (which uses a 32k BPE vocabulary).

The monotone guarantee. Versley's 2025 paper presents an algorithm to extend an existing tokenizer (not replace it) with domain-specific tokens, with the formal guarantee that no input sequence is assigned more tokens after the extension than before. This is important because naive token addition can actually worsen tokenization for sequences that were previously handled efficiently. Result: up to 20% sequence length compression on e-commerce text with no accuracy degradation.

Tradeoffs of extend vs. replace. Extending adds new token IDs without invalidating the existing embedding matrix — you can initialize new token embeddings from subword compositions. Replacing from scratch requires retraining the embedding layer from random initialization and is only practical if you are training from scratch (as eBay did for LiLiuM). If you adapt a pretrained model (e-Llama path), extension is the right choice.

BPE vs. SentencePiece Unigram. BPE is greedy merge — builds vocabulary by iteratively merging the most frequent byte pair. Unigram (SentencePiece) starts from a large vocabulary and prunes by maximizing corpus likelihood under a unigram LM. Unigram handles subword regularization (sampling different tokenizations at train time) which can improve robustness. Both are widely used; LiLiuM uses SentencePiece.

3C. Vision-Language Models — HIS MOST RECENT PAPER (arXiv:2602.11733, 2026)

The VLM paper (arXiv 2602.11733) is Versley's most recent major work and is directly relevant to Tony's Human Intent World Model. Know it in detail.

Architecture. LLaVA-OneVision style: SigLIP2 or Qwen2.5 ViT vision encoder → lightweight projector (MLP) → text decoder (LLM). No cross-attention between vision and text; visual tokens are simply prepended to the text token sequence and the LLM attends over both.

Three-stage training:

  1. Vision-Language Alignment: freeze LLM, train projector only on caption data (BLIP-LAION 558k)
  2. Mid-Stage Training: partial LLM unfreeze; filtered diverse datasets
  3. Visual Instruction Tuning: full fine-tune on 4M e-commerce instruction examples (45% VQA, 30% Dynamic Attribute Extraction, 12.5% Instruction Following, 12.5% Listings)

Data pipeline. 15M raw marketplace listings → InternVL-2.5-26B for captioning → Mistral-Small-3-24B for visual-textual verification → 4M instruction tuning examples. This three-stage data curation removes seller-introduced noise (wrong images, misleading titles) before fine-tuning.

Key finding for Tony to reference. Using e-Llama3.1-8B as text decoder instead of generic Llama-3.1-8B gives 5-12% improvement on e-commerce tasks across benchmarks. The domain-adapted LLM decoder knows about product taxonomy, attribute semantics, and pricing structures — things a general model does not. This is a clean empirical demonstration of the value of the full stack: pretraining → domain adaptation → VLM alignment.

Efficiency point. Fine-tuned Gemma3-4B achieves 3.8× inference speedup vs. zero-shot Gemma3-27B while improving F1 from 44.8% to 50.5%. Same lesson as Tony's routing and pruning work: a smaller, specialized model beats a larger, general model on domain-specific tasks with lower cost.

3D. Classical NLP — His Academic Home (probe zone)

He will not test you on bridging anaphora theory. But he will drop classical NLP questions as probes of intellectual depth, especially if the conversation lags. Do not dismiss these as outdated.

HMMs and Viterbi have been reported as actual eBay interview questions (from candidates). Be ready to walk through:

  • HMM: states S, observations O, transition matrix A, emission matrix B, initial distribution π
  • Forward algorithm: α_t(i) = P(o_1...o_t, s_t=i) — computed recursively in O(N²T)
  • Viterbi: same structure but max instead of sum; backtrace to get the most likely state sequence
  • Applications in NLP: POS tagging (states = POS tags, observations = words), NER (BIO tagging), speech recognition

Coreference. He may ask how modern LLMs handle coreference vs. traditional systems. The honest answer: LLMs have dramatically improved entity tracking via in-context learning, but still fail systematically on bridging anaphora (indirect reference), long-distance reference, and cross-document coreference. His BART toolkit used SVM + tree kernels + semantic similarity features. Modern neural approaches use span-based models (e.g., Lee et al. 2018, SpanBERT for coref) or prompt-based LLMs. You can bridge this to your work by noting that multi-turn dialogue and multi-image product understanding require similar entity tracking — which is a real challenge in the VLM attribute extraction task he worked on.

MT metrics. He will ask about chrF vs. BLEU. The eBay RAG-for-MT paper uses chrF because product titles are short (avg ~11 words) — with such short sequences, 4-gram BLEU has sparse statistics and high variance. chrF computes character n-gram F-score — more robust for short texts, morphologically rich languages (DE, PL), and out-of-vocabulary product terms. Know this.

3E. Scaling Laws

He has trained at scale; he will expect you to be fluent.

Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al. 2022): For a compute budget C (FLOPs), the compute-optimal model size N and token count D satisfy N ∝ C^0.5 and D ∝ C^0.5, with D ≈ 20N tokens. GPT-3 (175B on 300B tokens) was significantly under-trained by this standard. Chinchilla 70B on 1.4T tokens matched or exceeded it.

eBay's situation. eBay trains smaller models (1B/7B/13B LiLiuM; 8B/70B e-Llama). The Chinchilla prescription is for maximally compute-efficient training — but eBay has other constraints: inference cost, latency, serving thousands of queries per second. A 7B model served at scale costs far less than a 70B model. Chinchilla is the training compute optimum; the serving compute optimum is often a smaller, better-trained model (this is the Llama philosophy). Be able to articulate this distinction.

Domain-specific scaling. At e-commerce scale (~1T domain tokens, not 100T), you are not on the Chinchilla frontier for a 70B model. The continued pretraining is not compute-optimal in the academic sense; it is pragmatically optimal given you start from a strong 70B checkpoint.

Design choice to anticipate: He will ask why U-Net diffusion rather than direct regression of future weights. What to say:

"Diffusion Weights connects directly to the kind of repeated domain adaptation your team does. Each time you update e-Llama for a new e-commerce data release, you pay the full continued pretraining compute cost. Diffusion Weights offers a way to predict where the weights will end up after training without running the full gradient descent path. The WMT16 evaluation was deliberate — machine translation was a concrete domain where we could measure both speed and downstream quality, and it maps to exactly the kind of MT task eBay runs at scale."

6. Ten Intelligent Questions to Ask Versley

Use 3–4 of these in Stage 1. Prioritize the ones that connect most naturally to where the conversation has gone.

Q1 — Checkpoint averaging and model merging geometry

"In LiLiuM you use checkpoint averaging, and in e-Llama you use linear parameter interpolation between base Llama-3.1 and the domain-adapted model. For checkpoint averaging to work well, the averaged checkpoints need to be in the same loss basin. But if the checkpoints come from stages of training on divergent data mixtures — say, a general-data phase and an e-commerce-heavy phase — the parameter trajectories may have diverged enough that arithmetic averaging is suboptimal. Has your team compared SLERP (spherical linear interpolation, which better preserves vector magnitudes when models have diverged) against simple linear interpolation for checkpoint or model merging, and at what degree of data-mixture divergence does SLERP start winning?"

Why good: SLERP is a specific, technically grounded question about a real engineering decision the team makes. It shows you understand the geometry of weight space, not just the high-level "averaging helps" result. Versley will either have a direct answer (they tested it) or will appreciate the precision of the question.

Q2 — Vocabulary customization in production (his Sept 2025 vocabulary paper (arXiv 2509.26124))

"The vocabulary customization paper gives a monotone-efficiency guarantee for extending an existing tokenizer. In practice, how did you handle the new token embeddings — did you initialize them from compositions of the subword embeddings they replace, or from random initialization? And after adding new tokens, how many continued pretraining steps does the model need before the new token embeddings converge to useful representations?"

Why good: The paper presents the algorithm; the practical initialization and convergence questions are the things that only come up when you actually run the experiments. This shows you are thinking about implementation.

Q3 — VLM multi-image generalization (his VLM paper, arXiv:2602.11733)

"Your VLM paper found that models trained on single-image data generalize to multi-image inputs without explicit training — which is a striking result. Is that zero-shot generalization robust across all benchmark tasks, or are there specific failure modes (e.g., tasks that require comparing two images explicitly rather than reasoning about each independently)? And is there a clear signal in the attention patterns for whether the model is actually attending across images or treating them independently?"

Why good: The paper reports the generalization result but does not fully characterize its limits. This question shows you are thinking about when the result would break down.

Q4 — Model merging in a live serving fleet

"The e-Llama paper uses simple parameter averaging for model merging as a zero-compute Pareto knob between domain and general performance. In a live serving fleet, how do you operationalize this? Do you maintain a library of merged checkpoints at different interpolation weights and serve the appropriate one by query type, or do you pick one fixed merge point for deployment? And have you compared simple averaging to methods like TIES-merging or DARE that handle parameter interference more explicitly?"

Why good: Connects published research to operational deployment questions he deals with every day.

Q5 — Evaluation benchmark design (he literally built five new ones)

"Building reliable domain-specific evaluation benchmarks is notoriously hard — LLM contamination, label distribution bias, annotation cost. For the five multilingual e-commerce benchmarks in e-Llama, what was the hardest part of ensuring the eval set is actually diagnostic of real e-commerce capability and not just a proxy for general NLU that already exists in the pretraining data?"

Why good: Directly relevant to his paper's contribution, opens a methodological discussion he has strong views on.

Q6 — LiLiuM vs. e-Llama strategy going forward

"LiLiuM was built 100% in-house for full data and licensing control. e-Llama is continued pretraining on Llama-3.1. Now that Meta has released models with open weights, is eBay's strategy converging toward the e-Llama track (adapt open models) and treating LiLiuM as the specialized from-scratch alternative for cases where licensing really matters? Or is there a plan to do a second-generation LiLiuM pretraining from scratch?"

Why good: This is a real strategic question that affects what Versley's team works on. Shows you understand the tradeoffs between the two tracks and have thought about where the field is heading.

Q7 — Cross-lingual transfer with domain-specific vocabulary

"One tension I see in the vocabulary extension approach: when you add e-commerce-specific tokens that are English-origin (brand names, product categories), how does that affect the multilingual capacity of the model? Does the token-level vocabulary divergence between English and non-English e-commerce text worsen alignment for the cross-lingual tasks — German/French/Italian listings in particular?"

Why good: Connects two of his papers (domain adaptation + vocabulary customization) and raises a real tension he has likely thought about.

Q8 — Versley's own research direction

"Looking at the arc from LiLiuM to e-Llama to the VLM work, the team's research has moved from language-only to multimodal. What does the next phase look like — are there open research problems in the VLM + knowledge graph combination (for Magical Listing) that are still genuinely unsolved? Or is the focus shifting more toward agentic pipelines like the Mercury platform?"

Why good: Open-ended, shows you have followed the arc of his team's research output, and gives him room to talk about what he is excited about (which tells you what skills will matter on the job).

Q9 — The role and team composition

"The JD mentions both pretraining and post-training responsibilities. In practice, where does this role spend most of its time — more on upstream data curation and continued pretraining experiments, or more on the downstream instruction tuning and alignment side? And is the team research-oriented (publishing is a goal) or are projects primarily driven by internal product needs?"

Why good: Practical and important for your own decision-making. It also signals you are thinking seriously about the fit, not just trying to get an offer.

Q10 — e-commerce / general data-mixture ratio over time

"The e-Llama ablation fixed the e-commerce/general data ratio at 50/50. But eBay's e-commerce corpus keeps growing — new listings, new languages, new product categories. As more high-quality e-commerce data has accrued since that initial run, has your team revisited the optimal mixing ratio? And is there a point of diminishing return where adding more e-commerce data stops improving e-commerce benchmarks because the model has already learned the key attribute and pricing distributions?"

Why good: This question goes beyond what is published (the paper fixes a ratio from one ablation) and probes the team's ongoing operational experience. It shows you understand that data strategy is not a one-time decision and prompts Versley to share what they have learned since publication.

7. Rapport and Etiquette for the Call

Opening

He is Amsterdam-based, you are Amsterdam-based. Open naturally:

"I'm actually already based in Amsterdam — hybrid three days a week would be very natural for me. I've been in the city since my TU Delft MSc and genuinely like the research ecosystem here."

This is a factual positive that costs nothing and removes any logistical ambiguity from the first minute of the call.

Pace and listening style

Versley is precise and probably speaks at a measured pace — he is a linguist, and linguists tend to be deliberate with word choice. Match his pace. Do not rush or over-explain. When he asks a technical question, it is fine to pause for 2–3 seconds to formulate a precise answer rather than immediately dumping everything you know.

If he asks a question you do not know the exact answer to, say so directly:

"I haven't run that specific experiment, but my intuition is X based on the mechanism, and I would want to verify by ablating Y."

He will respect intellectual honesty far more than a fabricated confident answer. He has run the experiments himself; he will know if you are guessing.

On your papers — be the author, not the explainer

He will ask design choice questions about your papers. The right posture is: you made those decisions, you have the reasons, you know the failure modes, you know what you would do differently. Avoid:

  • "We chose X because it seemed to work better." (Weak — no mechanism.)
  • "The paper shows that X outperforms Y." (Passive — he can read the paper.)

Instead:

  • "We chose X because of mechanism Z. The ablation [describe it] confirmed that was the right call. In hindsight, the one thing I would change is [specific limitation]."

Language and tone

He has a computational linguistics background — he values precise language. Avoid AI hype vocabulary: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "we leveraged synergies," "robust performance at scale" (as a vague claim). Prefer: "17.5% improvement," "3.2× speedup," "some degradation on general NLU, more pronounced on the 8B."

He will appreciate specificity about multilingual challenges — he has done them (AlexaTM multilingual, mATIS++ across 6 languages, eBay multilingual benchmarks). Do not wave your hands about "multilingual capability." Be specific about which language pairs, what the gap was, why.

Closing the call

End with genuine enthusiasm and a specific reason:

"I'm excited about this role specifically because the research agenda — building and adapting foundation models for a real product at scale — is exactly the systems-level work I've been building toward. The VLM + knowledge graph combination for Magical Listing is a particularly interesting problem space, and having e-Llama as the text decoder to build on is a real advantage over starting from a generic base."

Then ask one of the Stage-6 questions you have not yet asked.

8. Risk Areas and Mitigation

Risk What he might ask Your mitigation
Pretraining at scale (480 H100s, Megatron-LM) "Have you run multi-GPU pretraining? What parallelism strategy?" Be honest: "My projects have been research-lab-scale. Conceptually I can walk through 3D parallelism, FlashAttention, mixed precision. I know the engineering principles; I haven't operated the cluster. That's a skill I would ramp on quickly."
Classical NLP depth "Walk me through the Viterbi algorithm" or "What are the limitations of modern LLMs on coreference?" Prepare HMMs + Viterbi cold (see Section 3D). Do not be dismissive of these topics — they connect to real LLM failure modes he cares about.
Multilingual evaluation "Your WMT16 work — what does that tell you about German MT vs. low-resource languages?" WMT16 EN-DE is a high-resource pair. Be explicit about what it does and does not test. eBay cares about PL, SE, NL, IT — lower resource in some models. Acknowledge the gap and connect to the RAG approach for low-resource pairs.
Production MLOps "How would you monitor continued pretraining runs in production?" Connect to what you know: perplexity curves, validation loss on held-out domain data, general benchmark regression. Acknowledge you have not operated a production ML platform at eBay's scale, but show you know the metrics and tooling conceptually.
