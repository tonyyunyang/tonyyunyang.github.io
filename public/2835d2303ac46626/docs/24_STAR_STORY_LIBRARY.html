The hardware wall on the VR eye-tracking dataset

Fires for: P1 (challenging project delivered), program/project management, cross-functional delivery, lead-without-authority. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~30s.)

Say-it-like-this: "The hardest thing I've shipped end-to-end was a VR eye-tracking emotion dataset — and the thing that nearly sank it was a hardware limit, not the modeling."

S/T — As co-first author at TU Delft, I set out to read emotion from the eyes in VR using only sensors a headset already carries. The wall hit early: our VIVE Pro Eye's built-in Tobii tracker only exposed derived gaze — processed numbers — while the raw periocular video the whole idea depended on was locked behind a commercial SDK license.

A — Rather than buy our way out, I integrated a Pupil Labs add-on to pull the raw near-eye video directly — two infrared cameras at 120fps. I built the synchronization and labeling pipeline around it, plus an open-source Unity collection-and-labeling tool so the protocol was reproducible. I ran two participant studies, coordinated my co-first author and a senior external collaborator I couldn't direct, and built the benchmark fusion model — a ViViT video encoder fused with a time-series transformer over gaze and pupil.

R — Caveat first: it's a modest 26-participant set, shared on request under an agreement rather than as an open download, and the fusion actually loses slightly at the half-second window — 0.43 against periocular's 0.45 — which we reported rather than buried. With that said: across seven emotions, fusion lifts cross-session F1 from 0.54 to 0.70, the Unity tool is open-source, and the paper published at IMWUT 2025. [CONFIRM TONY — one concrete coordination moment: a point where you and co-first author Bishwas Regmi, or senior collaborator Andreas Bulling, disagreed on a design call and how you steered it — needed if you want this to also fire for "leadership/coordination."]

The measurement wall on nnU-Net pruning

Fires for: P1 (challenging project delivered), P4 (data to influence — proxy/ACDC curve), scoping under constraint, honesty about unfinished work. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~20s.)

Say-it-like-this: "A clean 'I hit a wall and engineered around it' story is my nnU-Net pruning paper — at one point I literally couldn't score my own results."

S/T — As first author, I wanted to know how much of a trained nnU-Net — the standard medical-segmentation model — is actually dead weight. The blocker hit immediately: these benchmarks don't release test-set labels, so I had no ground truth to measure pruning damage against.

A — I defined a proxy Dice — how much the pruned model agrees with the unpruned model's own predictions. A proxy can quietly lie, so I anchored it on ACDC, the one dataset with public labels, and checked that the real-Dice and proxy-Dice curves tracked together. I scoped the method tight — one-shot magnitude pruning, no fine-tuning — so I was measuring the network's own redundancy, not a retraining trick. And I pruned all five cross-validation folds independently across four datasets, so it wasn't one lucky split.

R — Caveat first: this is unstructured pruning, so there's no measured speedup — the footprint stays dense. The finding: over 80% of the weights come out while proxy Dice stays above 0.95, with an inverted-U pattern showing the deep, parameter-heavy layers are the most prunable. First-author paper, MIDL 2025. I frame real latency wins as the structured follow-up the redundancy map enables — not something I've already delivered. [CONFIRM TONY — if you use this for P4 (data-to-influence), name the real person the proxy/ACDC curve actually persuaded — advisor Qian Tao, co-author Yidong Zhao, or a reviewer — and what they changed their mind about; the data is grounded, the named recipient is not.]

Fitting weight-space diffusion onto a single GPU

Fires for: P1 (challenging project delivered), technical depth on generative models, resourcefulness under a hardware constraint. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~30s.)

Say-it-like-this: "A pure technical-challenge story is a paper I'm an author on, currently under review at ACL, where the whole method only became feasible because of one compression decision."

S/T — The idea was to treat fine-tuning as forecasting: instead of running SGD to convergence, train a diffusion model to predict a future weight state directly. The wall was raw scale — you simply cannot run a diffusion U-Net over 1.5 billion weights on the single RTX 4090 I had.

A — So the enabling move was to not diffuse over raw weights at all. I worked in a compressed latent: a chunk-wise beta-VAE vectorizes and compresses each layer's weights into a compact, smooth latent space, and the diffusion runs there — genuine machinery, a Gaussian forward process and a DDPM/DDIM reverse chain with an attention U-Net, but with a non-standard target: the denoiser predicts a future-epoch weight state, not the clean version of its own input. A conditioning stack tells it which task it's forecasting for.

R — Two caveats first: it's diffusion-inspired — real machinery, non-standard target — and the speedup is amortized, not free; you still pay to collect the training trajectories. The headline is a deliberately conservative roughly 3.2x wall-clock acceleration; my per-task table actually measures 3.8 to 4.3x, and I'd rather name that gap than paper over it. Demonstrated on GPT-2 up to 1.5B, MLP layers only, on one GPU. [CONFIRM TONY — if you want this to also serve a collaboration or disagreement facet, supply a real human element. The "ran it over a weekend to settle a baseline dispute" framing is NOT grounded — the Soup baselines are real, the dispute is not — so don't tell it as fact.]

Greedy-locking: making the TwinRouterBench corpus buildable

Fires for: P1, scoping to manage cost/risk, complexity & impact of a decision, technical judgment. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~30s.)

Say-it-like-this: "On TwinRouterBench my main contribution was a scoping decision — it turned a labeling job that was literally impossible to run into a buildable one."

S/T — TwinRouterBench is a step-level LLM-routing benchmark; I'm third of seventeen authors and I owned the labeling protocol. The problem: to label the cheapest model tier that still solves each step, the honest way is to re-verify every combination of tier choices across a trajectory. That's four-to-the-N reruns for an N-step trajectory — exponential — so the corpus simply could not be built that way.

A — I designed a greedy sequential-locking search instead. You lock each earlier step at its verified tier, then for the current step try tiers cheapest-first, and accept a tier only if the full trajectory still passes end-to-end with the same step count. That collapses four-to-the-N down to roughly four-times-N — linear — which is what made the 970-step corpus buildable at all. I knew greedy can miss steps that are each safe but jointly break a trajectory, so I deliberately scoped in a roughly 10% manual audit to catch exactly those interactions. [CONFIRM TONY — how you proposed this to the two lead authors, and whether anyone pushed back on greedy versus exhaustive.]

R — Caveat first: it's greedy, so it can miss cross-step interactions, and we list that as a limitation rather than hide it. The audit found one further-downgradeable step out of sixty-four, so the labels came back tight — and, more to the point, the corpus exists because the search was made tractable.

Scoping the nnU-Net experiment to isolate redundancy

Fires for: P1, scoping discipline, complexity & impact of a decision, data-driven rigor. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~25s.)

I chose unstructured pruning because it's the cleanest way to measure how much is removable, accepting up front that it gives no real speedup. R — Caveat first: because it's unstructured, there's no measured speedup — same dense footprint. The ranked idea-portfolio and the smallest-falsifying-experiment

The ground-truth curve behind the "80% removed" claim

Fires for: P4 (used data to influence), complexity & impact of decisions, communication. (Aim ~2 min)

Say-it-like-this: "When I claimed I'd removed over 80% of a model's weights, I built the evidence that my own measure wasn't lying to me."

S/T — I was pruning the nnU-Net medical-segmentation model, and I could remove over 80% of its weights while quality looked fine. But "fine" was measured by a proxy Dice score — agreement between the pruned model and the original model, not against real labels. That's circular: it could just be agreeing with the original model's mistakes. Before I'd let anyone trust the claim, I had to prove the proxy wasn't fooling me.

A — I used ACDC, the one cardiac dataset that ships with public ground-truth labels, as an anchor. There I could put two curves side by side as I pruned harder — proxy agreement and true Dice against real labels — and show the proxy tracked the ground truth. That justified trusting the proxy on the three datasets that had no test labels, across five cross-validation folds.

R — Caveat first: this is unstructured pruning with no measured speedup — I removed parameters, I haven't yet made it faster, and proxy Dice is agreement, not accuracy. But the ACDC ground-truth anchor is what makes ">80% removed at proxy Dice above 0.95" a credible finding rather than an artifact. When failure-aware accounting flipped the ranking

Fires for: P4 (used data to influence), P3 (technical advice), complexity & impact of decisions. (Aim ~2 min)

Say-it-like-this: "On our routing benchmark, the 'cheapest' router on paper turned out to be the worst deal — and a single accounting change in the data is what proved it."

S/T — On TwinRouterBench we rank LLM routers — systems that pick which model handles each step of an agent task — by how much money they save. The standard cost metric only counts the compute you spend; it ignores that a cheap model can fail a step and force an expensive retry. I own the cost-accounting slice, and I suspected the naive metric was rewarding exactly the wrong behavior.

A — I built a failure-aware version of the CostSave metric that charges a router for the cascades and retries its failures cause, scored on full-trajectory, execution-verified replay — so every step's success was actually checked, not assumed. Then I re-ran the leaderboard.

R — Caveat first: this is one benchmark variant, and the fact that the ranking is metric-sensitive is itself a finding we chose to publish, not hide. Under the four naive accounting variants the rule-based ClawRouter ranks on top; only the failure-aware variant flips it — the nearest-neighbor router SR-KNN at 56.18 versus Claw's 53.82. Separately, a frontier model used as the router flagged only 7 of 147 verified-hard steps as high-difficulty — evidence that "just ask a smart model to route" quietly fails. [CONFIRM TONY — did this data actually change a decision or a collaborator's mind among the 17 authors? Who pushed back on the failure-aware metric, and what did they do once they saw the flip?]

Owning and defending the benchmark's labeling protocol

Fires for: P3 (used technical expertise to advise), program/project management, managing technical issues. (Aim ~2 min)

Say-it-like-this: "On a seventeen-author benchmark, I owned the labeling protocol — and if the labels are wrong, every number built on top of them is wrong."

S/T — TwinRouterBench needs a difficulty label for each step of a multi-step agent task. The naive way explodes combinatorially — four model choices to the power of N steps — so you can't actually label it by brute force. I owned the labeling-and-cost-accounting slice and had to make that space buildable without fooling ourselves.

A — The design I built and defend is greedy sequential-locking: lock in the verified tier at each step before moving on, which collapses four-to-the-N reruns down to roughly four-times-N. Labels come from downgrade-and-cascade with full-trajectory, execution-verified replay — a step is only labeled "safe to downgrade" if the whole trajectory still passes with the same step count — plus a ~10% manual audit to catch interactions greedy might miss.

R — Caveat first: greedy locking is an approximation — it can miss two individually-safe downgrades that jointly break a run, and I list that as a limitation rather than claim otherwise. But it's what turned an impossible labeling space into the 970-step corpus the whole benchmark stands on. [CONFIRM TONY — the actual advising moment: who questioned the greedy shortcut or the label validity — a co-author, a reviewer — and how did you walk them through the trade-off?]
