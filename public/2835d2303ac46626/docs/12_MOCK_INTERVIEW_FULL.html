EXCHANGE 2 — Breadth Probe: Pretraining Fundamentals

VERSLEY: You mentioned training constraints. Let me test the fundamentals. Walk me through the key decisions you'd make when designing a pretraining run from scratch — say you have a compute budget for a 7B-parameter model. What's your checklist?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The first decision is data — it dominates everything else. Before touching architecture I'd define my data pipeline: quality filtering (FastText language detection, heuristic scoring on average line length and alpha-character ratio, perplexity filter against a reference LM), MinHash LSH deduplication at paragraph and document level, and domain mixing ratios decided by held-out perplexity on your target task categories. For eBay's case I'd heavily include product listing text, parallel MT pairs for the languages you serve — German, French, Italian, Spanish — and code, because code data has an outsized effect on reasoning per token.

Second is the Chinchilla budget question. Using the C = 6ND compute approximation (the 6ND form is Kaplan et al. 2020, not Hoffmann), Hoffmann et al. (Chinchilla, 2022) showed the loss-optimal allocation is roughly 20 tokens per parameter. So for 7B that's 140B Chinchilla-optimal tokens. But in 2025–2026, Llama 3 and Qwen 3 are deliberately training beyond Chinchilla — 15T and 36T tokens respectively — because inference cost needs to be amortized over billions of queries. For eBay, where you're serving at e-commerce scale, inference efficiency matters more than minimal training cost, so I'd overtrain toward 1–2T tokens for the 7B.

Third is architecture. Today it's essentially settled: decoder-only, RMSNorm pre-norm, SwiGLU FFN, RoPE positional embeddings, GQA instead of full MHA to cut KV cache memory. For vocab I'd use domain-specific BPE — your own paper showed that a custom e-commerce vocabulary gives 34% generation speedup on downstream tasks, which is a massive serving-cost win.

Fourth is training stability: warmup LR over 1000–2000 steps, cosine decay schedule, gradient clipping at norm ≤ 1.0, BF16 compute with FP32 master weights to avoid overflow. For MoE I'd add Z-loss on the router. QK-Norm is worth adding to prevent attention logit explosion. Checkpoint averaging at the end gives you a free quality improvement.

Fifth is evaluation — I'd design held-out task sets for the target domain before training starts, so you're not trying to define success after the fact.

VERSLEY: You mentioned 20 tokens per parameter from Chinchilla. But eBay's e-Llama paper trained on 1T tokens for an 8B model — that's 125 tokens per parameter, six times beyond Chinchilla-optimal. Why?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

Two reasons, and your team's e-Llama paper makes the point well. First, inference-aware scaling: if you expect a model to serve K tokens over its production lifetime, the breakeven shifts. A smaller, more thoroughly trained model costs less per inference token than a larger, Chinchilla-optimal model that needed fewer training tokens. At eBay's query volume — millions of e-commerce requests per day — you're firmly in the regime where it pays to overtrain a smaller model.

Second, domain absorption: continued pretraining for domain adaptation is not the same as a random walk through the loss landscape. The model needs enough exposure to e-commerce data patterns, e-commerce terminology, and multilingual catalog content to genuinely internalize the distribution. Your ablations showed that below a certain token budget the model hasn't actually absorbed the domain — the improvement plateaus, and general capability starts degrading if you push the domain fraction too hard. The 1T token budget at 50/50 domain-general mix was validated by ablation, not arbitrary.

COACHING NOTE: Versley has run 70B pretraining on 480 H100s. He knows this cold. Your job is to show you understand the tradeoffs and can cite numbers — specifically the 1T token e-Llama training, the 50/50 data ratio, and the Chinchilla vs inference-optimal distinction. He will probe if you say anything vague.

EXCHANGE 3 — Tokenization Deep Dive

VERSLEY: You mentioned custom vocabulary. Let me push on that. What exactly causes the 34% speedup in LiLiuM's generation speed, and why doesn't every model just do this?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The core mechanism is token fertility reduction. A standard general-domain tokenizer trained on web text has never seen "18k yellow gold diamond tennis bracelet" as a recurring pattern, so it splits it into 12–15 subword tokens. With a domain-specific vocabulary that includes common e-commerce n-grams and product terms as single tokens, the same phrase might be 6–8 tokens. Every extra token is an extra autoregressive decoding step — one forward pass, attention over the full KV cache, one sampling step. Cut the sequence length by 34% and you cut the number of forward passes by roughly the same fraction.

Your vocabulary customization paper from Sept 2025 (arXiv preprint) takes this further with a monotone efficiency guarantee — the extended vocabulary only adds tokens such that no input is ever segmented into more tokens than before. That's the right property: you want domain compression without any pathological case where a rare domain term gets segmented worse than the original tokenizer.

Why doesn't every model do this? Three costs. First, you have to train the embedding matrix for new tokens from scratch or warm-initialize from the mean of the constituent subword embeddings — you can't just reuse a pretrained general embedding. Second, you lose drop-in compatibility with any pretrained model that uses the standard vocabulary; you're on your own for weight initialization. Third, it requires continued pretraining with the new vocabulary to let the model learn the token meanings. All three costs are substantial for an organization that doesn't control the full training stack — but for eBay, with an in-house LLM, it's absolutely the right call.

COACHING NOTE: The mechanistic explanation (token count → forward passes → latency) is the key insight. The vocabulary-paper (arXiv 2509.26124) reference shows you read Versley's actual work, not just the LiLiuM arXiv. The "why not everyone" framing shows systems thinking.

EXCHANGE 4 — Domain Adaptation and Catastrophic Forgetting

VERSLEY: Let's go to domain adaptation. In e-Llama we used continued pretraining — 1T tokens at 10% of the original learning rate, 50/50 domain-general mix. Walk me through the catastrophic forgetting risk and how you mitigate it. What would happen if you just cranked the learning rate?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

Catastrophic forgetting in continued pretraining is a weight interference problem. The model's weights encode the general distribution from 15T tokens of pretraining. When you continue training on a narrow domain, the optimizer updates weights to reduce loss on domain data, overwriting the general representation. The higher the learning rate, the more violent the overwrite per step.

The e-Llama ablation makes this vivid: at 10x the LR — 3.0e-3 for the 8B versus the intended 3.0e-5 — e-commerce benchmark performance was 50.6 but general NLU dropped to 34.5. At the intended LR of 3.0e-5, you got 59.9 on e-commerce and 42.0 on general — better on both axes. Crucially, the high-LR run didn't even win on the domain task — 50.6 versus 59.9 — because the catastrophic overwrite destabilizes the very representations the domain task depends on. The optimizer's first and second moment estimates overcorrect toward the domain gradient direction, effectively erasing structure that domain performance also relies on.

The mitigations are: first, keep LR at about 10% of the original pretraining LR — enough to move in weight space but not enough to erase the prior. Second, maintain the data mixture — the 50/50 general-domain ratio from the e-Llama work acts as a regularizer, continuously reminding the model of the general distribution during the run. Third, model merging as post-hoc interpolation: the e-Llama paper shows that averaging the final e-Llama weights with the original Llama 3.1 base gives a nearly linear interpolation between domain performance and general performance, with near-zero compute cost. That's an elegant way to offer different operating points in production without retraining.

An alternative approach is EWC (elastic weight consolidation) or LoRA — freeze most weights and only update a low-rank subspace, which structurally prevents the general representation from being overwritten. But at your scale, with the infrastructure to do full continued pretraining, data mixing plus careful LR is simpler and more controllable.

EXCHANGE 4b — Context Window Pushback (VERSLEY CHALLENGES)

VERSLEY: What about the context window? You trained e-Llama at 8k context even though Llama 3.1 supports 128k. Did that limit the model? And you said "the weights don't actively forget long-context handling" — is that actually the right mechanistic explanation?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

You're right to push on that. My phrasing was imprecise. Let me give the correct mechanism.

The reason e-Llama largely retains long-context capability despite training at 8k is not that "weights don't forget" — that's too vague and basically circular. The real mechanism is RoPE frequency extrapolation. RoPE encodes position via sinusoidal functions at different frequencies across the head dimensions. Low-frequency components encode long-range structure; high-frequency components encode local structure. During the original Llama 3.1 pretraining at 128k context, the model learned to use all of those frequencies — including the low ones that matter beyond position 8k. When you continue pretraining at 8k, you're providing training signal only for positions 0–8k. The high-frequency RoPE components get updated because they're exercised. The low-frequency components — the ones encoding positions 8k–128k — receive no gradient signal and therefore don't change much. So you accidentally preserve long-context capability not by any active mechanism, but by never touching the weights that encode it.

The risk is that if the distribution of your 8k training data systematically differs from the long-context distribution in a way that affects shared low-frequency components, you'd see degradation. That's why including some long-document examples even in a short-context continued pretraining run — as Llama 3's own continuation did — is the safer approach.

If long-context were critical for the application — say, processing a full seller catalog in one shot — the right fix is a small fraction of long-document examples in the training mix, or running YaRN after training to extend the context explicitly.

The correct explanation is RoPE frequency extrapolation: low-frequency sinusoidal components encoding long-range positions receive no gradient at 8k, so they're preserved by default. EXCHANGE 5 — VLM Architecture Probe

VERSLEY: Your VLM paper (arXiv:2602.11733) — we found that using e-Llama as the text decoder instead of the base Llama 3.1 gave a consistent improvement on e-commerce attribute tasks. You mentioned a VLM project of your own. How would you explain that gain mechanistically?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The gain comes from two sources. First, vocabulary alignment: the e-Llama tokenizer or at minimum the LLM's representation space has been shaped by 1T tokens of e-commerce data. When the VLM projects visual features from the SigLIP encoder through the MLP projector into the LLM's input space, the LLM needs to map visual representations onto semantic concepts in its vocabulary. If those vocabulary entries for "aspect," "material," "condition," "listing specifics" are already sharp and well-separated in e-Llama's embedding space — whereas in base Llama they're part of a more diffuse general representation — the projector has a much easier mapping problem. It's the difference between landing on a precise target versus an approximate one.

Second, world knowledge: e-Llama has seen the actual attribute ontology that eBay uses — categories like "Aspect Prediction" in your benchmark reflect eBay-specific taxonomies. A model that has read millions of eBay product descriptions during continued pretraining knows that for "Sports Cards" the key aspects are "Player," "Year," "Card Grade," "Set." The base Llama has to infer this from general web knowledge, which is noisier.

Looking at the actual numbers from the paper: on the eComMMMU public benchmark, the base Llama 3.1-8B decoder gives 46.9% and e-Llama gives 52.2% — a 5.3 percentage-point gain. On internal tasks the gains are larger: Aspect Prediction goes from 37.7% to 44.4%, Dynamic Attribute Extraction from 51.9% to 60.4%. So the gain is concentrated on the knowledge-intensive and taxonomy-aware tasks, which is consistent with the world-knowledge hypothesis over the pure vocabulary-alignment hypothesis.

I faced a related question in my Human Intent World Model work. We had to decide whether to fine-tune a general VLM on our synthetic sales-intent dataset or to first domain-adapt the text decoder. We took the fine-tuning path and found that the quality of the synthetic reasoning traces — how well they captured the structure of sales intent — mattered more than the choice of base LLM. This may differ from your finding because your task (attribute extraction from product images) is more knowledge-intensive, while ours was more reasoning-structure-intensive. I'd be curious whether in your ablations the e-Llama advantage was primarily concentrated on knowledge-heavy tasks like Aspect Prediction or also on structural tasks like Dynamic Attribute Extraction — the numbers suggest the latter actually benefited just as much, which is interesting.

VERSLEY: Interesting. What VLM architecture did you use, and why?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

We used a LLaVA-style architecture — a pretrained vision encoder, a two-layer MLP projector mapping visual features to the LLM's token space, and a fine-tuned text decoder. We chose LLaVA because it's modular: you can swap text decoders without retraining the vision encoder, which let us ablate different LLM bases quickly. The alternative was Q-Former from InstructBLIP, which uses learned query tokens that cross-attend to visual features and compress them to a fixed-size representation — more elegant for very high-resolution images because you avoid the sequence length explosion, but it introduces an information bottleneck that can drop fine-grained visual details.

For customer intent inference, the fine-grained visual details — posture, gaze direction, hand position — matter, so we didn't want a bottleneck. The LLaVA projector passes the full patch-level feature grid, which preserves that spatial detail.

One finding from your paper that I found compelling was the bounding-box-enhanced labeling strategy for multi-image inputs: using Qwen2.5-VL-32B to produce precise bounding box annotations, then re-annotating with GPT-4 using those boxes, gave Gemma3-27B 58.8 F1 on the Item Intelligence task versus 44.8 F1 for the same model in zero-shot. And the finetuned Gemma3-4B at 50.5 F1 with 6.7 seconds per example represents a 3.8x speedup versus the 27B zero-shot at 25.5 seconds — better quality and substantially faster. That quality-of-labels-over-model-size insight is one I'd borrow for any future multi-image work.

EXCHANGE 5b — VLM Isolation Follow-Up (VERSLEY PUSHES DEEPER)

VERSLEY: You say the gain in switching to e-Llama as the text decoder comes from world knowledge and vocabulary alignment. But how do you actually know? Could it just be that e-Llama is a stronger LLM overall on these tasks regardless of the visual input? What experiment would isolate whether the gain is in the LLM side or the ViT side?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

That's a real confound and you're right to press on it. The clean isolation experiment is: run the same e-Llama decoder on the attribute extraction tasks with text-only input — no images, just the product title and category — and compare against the same tasks done by the base Llama 3.1 with text-only input. If the e-Llama text-only gap matches the e-Llama VLM gap, the visual encoder is contributing nothing beyond what the language model already knows from text. If the VLM gap is larger than the text-only gap, that excess is genuinely from better visual grounding via the vision encoder.

A second experiment is to freeze the LLM decoder and train only the MLP projector, comparing the base Llama projector versus the e-Llama projector on the same vision encoder. If the frozen e-Llama outperforms frozen base Llama, you've isolated the LLM contribution while holding the visual encoding and projection training equal.

My guess based on the data is that the gain is mostly LLM-side: the ~5.3pp gain on eComMMMU and the larger gain on Aspect Prediction look like knowledge-intensive wins, not visual grounding wins. Dynamic Attribute Extraction's gain is more interesting — that task requires reading specific product details from images, so if the visual path were contributing, you'd expect it there. The fact that it also improves with e-Llama could mean the LLM's better product-term vocabulary makes it easier to decode the attribute values from visual tokens, even if the ViT representation is identical. But I'd want to run that ablation before claiming it. I don't know if that experiment is in the paper — is it?

EXCHANGE 6 — Deep Dive: LLM Router Paper

VERSLEY: Let's talk about your LLM Router work. You claim more than 90% cost reduction versus a single SOTA model per step. That's a big number. Walk me through the setup and what the routing signal actually is.

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The core insight is that in complex multi-step tasks, not every step requires frontier-model capability. A step might be "reformat this JSON" or "extract dates from this text" — tasks where GPT-4o or Claude 3.7 are comically over-specified. The router's job is to look at each individual step in a task and assign it to the cheapest model that can handle it correctly.

The routing signal is a learned quality predictor built on top of a lightweight bi-encoder. Concretely: the input representation is a concatenation of the step text embedding and the preceding context summary (both encoded by a sentence encoder at ~50ms latency). The quality predictor is a shallow MLP that outputs the probability that model tier M produces output meeting the downstream acceptance criterion on this step. The training signal comes from task-level success labels backpropagated to step-level: we run the full multi-step task using the strong model, collect its trajectory as step-level silver labels, then train the predictor on those (step input, model tier, step quality) tuples. This avoids needing manual step-level ground truth — the strong model's trajectory provides it.

We also incorporate explicit features for task type (structured extraction, reasoning, code, creative generation) and step dependency complexity (how many prior context tokens the step requires), because these are strong priors for model difficulty before you ever look at the content.

The 90%+ cost reduction comes from the benchmark composition: many real-world agentic workflows have a long tail of simple deterministic steps mixed with a handful of genuinely hard reasoning steps. On those simple steps — which dominate by count — you can route to GPT-4o-mini or a comparable small model at 50–100x lower API cost, and you get the same output quality because the task is well within the smaller model's capability. The overall cost drops dramatically while aggregate task performance stays matched to the single-SOTA baseline.

I want to be honest about the benchmark conditions and what they do and don't generalize to: we're measuring on agentic coding and structured-task workflows. If your task distribution is heavily skewed toward complex multi-hop reasoning with deep interdependencies — where cheap intermediate steps cascade into hard recovery problems — the gain would be smaller. For eBay's workloads, which include many high-volume, structured tasks like attribute extraction, product categorization, and translation, I'd expect the cost reduction to be substantial but would need to benchmark on your specific task mix.

EXCHANGE 6b — Router Cost Reduction Pushback (VERSLEY CHALLENGES)

VERSLEY: That 90%+ cost reduction — you said it comes from benchmark composition. So isn't it largely benchmark-specific? If I take that router and run it on a task mix with more complex steps, the number collapses. What's actually the generalizable claim?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

You're right, and I should be clearer about this. The 90%+ figure is not a universal property of the router — it's a property of the router applied to that specific task distribution, which has a lot of simple formatting and extraction steps. On a task mix of exclusively hard multi-step reasoning, the gain would shrink substantially because there are fewer cheap steps to exploit.

The generalizable claim is more modest and also more interesting: the router identifies, within any given workflow, which steps are within a cheaper model's capability envelope, and exploits that heterogeneity. The cost reduction you observe is a function of the heterogeneity of step difficulty in your task distribution — which is an empirical property of your specific workload, not something the router guarantees in the abstract.

For eBay, I'd say the honest expected range is somewhere between 40–70% cost reduction on typical catalog-management and product-understanding tasks, based on the step-difficulty distribution I'd expect in those workflows. That's still highly valuable at eBay's query volumes — millions of requests per day. But claiming 90%+ without benchmarking on eBay's actual workload would be overreaching.

The other generalizable claim is the methodology itself: the framework for decomposing multi-step tasks and assigning routing decisions per step, the training signal from strong-model trajectories, and the cascade-fallback mechanism — these transfer to any heterogeneous task distribution. The specific numbers don't.

COACHING NOTE: This is a genuine concession delivered with intellectual confidence. You're not backing down from the work — you're clarifying what the claim actually is. Versley values precision over defensiveness. The move from "90%+" to "40–70% on eBay's likely workload" is honest and shows you can calibrate numbers to context.

VERSLEY: How do you handle the cold-start problem? When you see a new task type you haven't trained the router on, what happens?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

Cold start is the hardest problem in routing, and I want to be honest about it. At launch with a new task type, the router has no task-specific quality signal to rely on. Our approach has two parts.

First, fallback-to-strong: for unknown task types, the router defaults to the strongest model tier and begins collecting trajectories. This is expensive at the start but safe — you don't degrade task quality while bootstrapping signal.

Second, similarity-based transfer: we embed the new task's step descriptions using the same bi-encoder and find the most similar task type in the router's training distribution. The routing policy for the nearest neighbor serves as a warm start. In practice this gets you to reasonable routing quality within a few hundred examples of the new task type, after which the specialized policy takes over.

There's a harder variant of cold start specific to agentic tasks: multi-step tasks where you can't evaluate the routing decision until the full task completes — you only find out at the end whether a cheap intermediate step caused a cascade failure downstream. Our Cost-Adaptive Routing work addresses this by collecting full task-level trajectories from the strong model and using them to supervise step-level quality labels after the fact.

VERSLEY: How is your routing approach different from MoE routing during pretraining?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

Great distinction — it's a genuinely different regime. In MoE pretraining, routing is a learned gating function inside a single model — typically a linear layer → softmax → top-K selection over a fixed set of expert FFNs that are co-trained with the router. The routing signal is learned end-to-end from the same language modeling objective. The experts are always co-present in memory; the router is cheap (one matmul); and the whole system is trained jointly so the router and experts develop a consistent division of labor.

My routing setup is at the inference/system level: different physical models, potentially from different providers, different API latencies, different cost points, different architectural families. The routing signal isn't learned from a shared objective — it's learned from outcome labels (did this step produce quality-acceptable output?). The model selection is a combinatorial allocation problem with latency and cost constraints that simply don't exist inside a single MoE model.

The deeper conceptual difference is heterogeneity. MoE experts are trained together and have identical interfaces. My router handles models that have different tokenizers, different context limits, different few-shot behaviors. That requires a richer routing signal and more robust fallback logic.

The similarity is that both face the load-balancing problem — MoE needs to avoid expert collapse (all tokens going to one expert) and I need to avoid always routing to the expensive model when a cheap one would do. But the mechanisms for preventing this are completely different: MoE uses an auxiliary load-balancing loss during training; I use explicit cost-constraint optimization at inference time.

COACHING NOTE: Exchange 6 now has a technically precise routing signal description: bi-encoder input representation, shallow MLP architecture, and task-level-to-step-level training signal via strong-model trajectory. The 90%+ framing is honest about benchmark dependency. The MoE comparison correctly identifies the key differences. Exchange 6b adds the pushback and concession.

EXCHANGE 7 — Deep Dive: Diffusion Weights

VERSLEY: Let's look at Diffusion Weights. You frame fine-tuning as generative weight prediction. What's the theoretical justification for that framing?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The framing comes from one observation: standard fine-tuning throws away almost everything it computes. EXCHANGE 9 — Classical NLP Probe (Versley's Home Turf)

VERSLEY: You haven't mentioned much classical NLP. Let me ask you a foundational question: Hidden Markov Models and the Viterbi algorithm — walk me through them, and then tell me where they still matter today.

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

A Hidden Markov Model assumes a Markov process over hidden states — the true underlying sequence, say POS tags — that generates observations — the surface tokens. The model has three sets of parameters: the transition matrix A where A[i,j] = P(state j | state i), the emission matrix B where B[i,k] = P(observation k | state i), and the initial state distribution π.

The inference task is: given a sequence of observations O = o1,...,oT, find the most likely state sequence Q* = q1,...,qT. This is what Viterbi solves.

Viterbi is a dynamic programming algorithm. Define δ_t(i) = max over all paths up to time t that end in state i, weighted by the probability of the observed sequence o1,...,ot. The recurrence is:

δ_t(j) = max_i [ δ_{t-1}(i) × A[i,j] ] × B[j, o_t]

You track the argmax using a backpointer ψ_t(j) = argmax_i [ δ_{t-1}(i) × A[i,j] ]. After a forward pass through the sequence, you backtrack from the best final state to recover Q*. Complexity is O(T × S^2) in states S and sequence length T. In practice you work in log space to avoid numerical underflow.

The natural extension from HMMs into NLP sequence labeling is CRFs — Conditional Random Fields. Where HMMs model the joint P(y, x) through factored emission and transition terms, CRFs model P(y|x) discriminatively, and critically, both the emission and transition factors can condition on the full input sequence — not just the current observation. This is the key advantage for NLP: you can use rich bidirectional features over the whole sentence rather than just the current token. And the Viterbi algorithm transfers directly to CRF decoding: the DP structure is identical, you just replace the HMM's local emission-transition factoring with CRF's globally conditioned factors. CRFs dominated NER, POS tagging, and chunking benchmarks through the early 2010s precisely because they had the same efficient inference as HMMs but a much richer feature model.

Where do HMMs still matter today? Surprisingly, they're not entirely extinct. In ASR pipeline stages, HMMs are still used as the acoustic model prior in hybrid CTC-Attention systems where the alignment model benefits from an explicit transition structure. In computational biology, HMMs are the foundation of profile-HMM tools like HMMER for protein family detection — the sequences are long, the state space is biologically meaningful, and the interpretability matters more than raw accuracy. In multilingual tokenization research, HMM-style segmentation models are sometimes used as unsupervised morpheme segmenters for low-resource languages where you can't train a BPE tokenizer on enough data.

And conceptually, understanding HMMs and CRFs is useful for reasoning about modern sequence models: the decoding dynamics in autoregressive generation — beam search, greedy decoding — share the same dynamic programming structure with Viterbi, just over a distribution modeled by a neural network rather than explicit A × B factoring.

EXCHANGE 9b — HMM Parameter Estimation Follow-Up (VERSLEY PUSHES DEEPER)

VERSLEY: You've described Viterbi for inference. How do you actually estimate the HMM parameters from unlabeled data? What algorithm, and how does it relate to EM?

TONY [MODEL ANSWER]:

The algorithm is Baum-Welch, and it is literally an instance of Expectation-Maximization applied to the HMM.

The E-step computes two quantities: the forward variable α_t(i) = P(o1,...,ot, qt=i | λ), the probability of observing the prefix o1,...,ot and being in state i at time t; and the backward variable β_t(i) = P(o_{t+1},...,oT | qt=i, λ), the probability of observing the suffix from t+1 onward given state i at time t. These are computed efficiently with the forward algorithm (left-to-right DP) and backward algorithm (right-to-left DP) respectively, both O(T × S^2).

From α and β you derive ξ_t(i,j) = P(qt=i, qt+1=j | O, λ) — the probability of being in state i at t and state j at t+1, given the whole observation sequence. This is the "soft" assignment over state transitions. And γ_t(i) = Σ_j ξ_t(i,j), the marginal probability of being in state i at time t.

The M-step re-estimates the parameters by taking expectations over these soft assignments. The new transition probability Â[i,j] = expected number of transitions from i to j, divided by expected number of transitions from i. The new emission probability B̂[i,k] = expected number of times in state i observing symbol k, divided by expected number of times in state i. The new π̂_i = γ_1(i).

Why does this converge? Because each M-step strictly increases the likelihood of the observations P(O|λ) — or holds it equal at a fixed point. This is the EM guarantee: the ELBO is tight at the E-step, the M-step maximizes the expected complete-data log-likelihood, and the result is a monotone increase in observed-data likelihood. It's not guaranteed to reach the global maximum — the likelihood surface has local optima — so in practice you run multiple restarts and take the best.

The key difference from Viterbi is that Viterbi does hard assignment — it finds the single best state sequence and commits. Baum-Welch does soft assignment — it maintains a distribution over all state sequences simultaneously via the forward-backward variables. For parameter estimation from unlabeled data, soft assignment is essential because there's no single correct path to supervise; you want to accumulate gradient signal from all plausible paths weighted by their posterior probability.

CODING EXAMPLE 1: Implement k-means Clustering from Scratch

PROBLEM: Given a 2D numpy array X of shape (N, D) and an integer k, implement k-means clustering. Return cluster assignments and centroids after convergence. Use L2 distance. Initialize centroids randomly from the data points.

THINK-ALOUD APPROACH:

"Let me break this into parts: initialization, assignment step, update step, convergence check.

For initialization, I'll sample k rows from X without replacement as starting centroids.

For the assignment step: for each point, find the closest centroid. This is a pairwise distance computation. If I do it naively it's O(N × k × D) per iteration. I can vectorize it with broadcasting.

For the update step: average all points assigned to each centroid. Need to handle empty clusters — if a centroid has no assigned points, I'll re-initialize it randomly.

For convergence: stop when centroid positions change by less than a tolerance, or after max_iter iterations.

Let me code this up."

import numpy as np

def kmeans(X: np.ndarray, k: int, max_iter: int = 100, tol: float = 1e-4, seed: int = 42) -> tuple:
    """
    X: (N, D) array
    Returns: (labels, centroids) where labels is (N,) and centroids is (k, D)
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)

    # Initialization: sample k points from X without replacement
    indices = rng.choice(len(X), size=k, replace=False)
    centroids = X[indices].copy()  # (k, D)

    labels = np.zeros(len(X), dtype=int)

    for iteration in range(max_iter):
        # Assignment step: compute pairwise distances
        # X: (N, D), centroids: (k, D)
        # diff: (N, k, D)  via broadcasting
        diff = X[:, np.newaxis, :] - centroids[np.newaxis, :, :]  # (N, k, D)
        dists = np.sum(diff ** 2, axis=2)  # (N, k), squared L2
        new_labels = np.argmin(dists, axis=1)  # (N,)

        # Update step: recompute centroids
        new_centroids = np.zeros_like(centroids)
        for c in range(k):
            mask = new_labels == c
            if mask.sum() == 0:
                # Empty cluster: re-initialize to random point
                new_centroids[c] = X[rng.integers(len(X))]
            else:
                new_centroids[c] = X[mask].mean(axis=0)

        # Convergence check
        centroid_shift = np.linalg.norm(new_centroids - centroids)
        centroids = new_centroids
        labels = new_labels

        if centroid_shift < tol:
            break

    return labels, centroids

CODING EXAMPLE 2: BPE Tokenizer Merge Step

PROBLEM: Given a vocabulary (mapping from string token pair to frequency), perform one BPE merge step. Return the new vocabulary after merging the most frequent pair.

THINK-ALOUD APPROACH:

"BPE merge: find the most frequent adjacent pair in the corpus, merge them into a single token, update the corpus. The tricky part is representing the corpus efficiently — usually as a list of word-frequency tuples where each word is a tuple of current tokens."

from collections import defaultdict
import re

def get_stats(vocab: dict) -> dict:
    """Count all adjacent pair frequencies in the vocabulary."""
    pairs = defaultdict(int)
    for word, freq in vocab.items():
        symbols = word.split()
        for i in range(len(symbols) - 1):
            pairs[(symbols[i], symbols[i + 1])] += freq
    return pairs

def merge_vocab(pair: tuple, vocab: dict) -> dict:
    """Merge a pair of tokens across all words in the vocabulary."""
    merged = ' '.join(pair)
    replacement = ''.join(pair)
    # Use regex to replace ONLY adjacent occurrences of this exact pair
    pattern = re.compile(r'(?<!\S)' + re.escape(merged) + r'(?!\S)')
    new_vocab = {}
    for word, freq in vocab.items():
        new_word = pattern.sub(replacement, word)
        new_vocab[new_word] = freq
    return new_vocab

def bpe_step(vocab: dict) -> tuple:
    """
    Perform one BPE merge step.

    vocab: dict mapping space-separated token strings to their frequency.
    Example: {'l o w </w>': 5, 'l o w e r </w>': 2, 'n e w e s t </w>': 6}

    Returns: (new_vocab, merged_pair)
    """
    pairs = get_stats(vocab)
    if not pairs:
        return vocab, None
    best_pair = max(pairs, key=pairs.get)
    new_vocab = merge_vocab(best_pair, vocab)
    return new_vocab, best_pair

First, the headline redundancy result: over 80% of the weights could be removed while a proxy Dice — agreement between the pruned and unpruned model — stayed above 0.95, across four datasets. Second — the critical layer for a skeptical clinical audience — I was explicit that proxy Dice is fidelity to the baseline, not accuracy, so I anchored on ACDC, the one dataset with ground-truth labels, where true Dice stayed essentially flat through about 80% pruning; and I pruned each of the five cross-validation folds independently so the finding wasn't one lucky split. Third, I showed the per-layer inverted-U redundancy map — the bottleneck and deep layers are the most prunable while the encoder and decoder ends hold the critical weights — which told the group exactly where capacity was safe to remove rather than asking them to trust an aggregate number.

I was also careful about what I did not claim: because the pruning is unstructured, it demonstrates the redundancy but not a wall-clock speedup, so I framed the deployment payoff as the next step — convert the redundancy map into structured channel removal to get a real latency win — rather than overselling a number we hadn't measured.

Result: The PI approved including the pruning results as the primary finding for the MIDL submission. The clinical collaborators accepted the model after reviewing the proxy-Dice fidelity and the ACDC true-Dice analysis. The paper was accepted at MIDL 2025. The broader lesson I took away is that when you're pushing back on a conservative default, you have to meet the stakeholder's risk model directly, not replace it with your own.

BEHAVIORAL STORY 4: "A difficult stakeholder situation" — Tencent LLM-for-Optimization

INTERVIEWER: Tell me about a difficult stakeholder or vendor situation you navigated.

TONY [STAR]:

Situation: In the LLM-for-Optimization work with Tencent, we were building a system where an LLM acts as a heuristic optimizer for a complex combinatorial problem. The Tencent team had strong prior beliefs about the structure of the solution space based on years of experience with classical solvers, and they wanted the LLM to produce solutions in a very specific JSON format that mapped to their existing solver infrastructure.

The problem was that the LLM's performance degraded significantly when constrained to that exact output format — the JSON structure required precision about nested constraint satisfaction that the model couldn't reliably produce when it also had to reason about the optimization objective.

Task: I needed to convince the Tencent team to allow a two-step approach: LLM generates a natural-language solution plan, then a lightweight rule-based parser converts that to the JSON format, rather than requiring direct JSON output.

Action: Instead of arguing abstractly, I ran a head-to-head benchmark. I showed them: (a) the failure rate of direct JSON output (too high for production), (b) the performance on the optimization objective when the model could reason freely in natural language, (c) the success rate of the parser on converting natural-language solutions to JSON (>95% for valid solutions). The quality of the solutions in option (b) was measurably better — lower objective function values — because the model could express reasoning in prose before committing to the structure.

I also framed the parser as an interface contract, not a workaround. "You own the contract between our system and your infrastructure. The parser is maintained by us and guarantees the output format. The LLM's reasoning is internal."

Result: They agreed to the two-step approach. The system shipped with the natural-language-then-parse architecture. Solution quality improved by ~18% on the benchmark objective. The framing of "interface contract" turned a technical disagreement into a collaboration on who owns what part of the system.

SECTION 5: Stage 3 — Team Fit Dialogue

TEAM MEMBER: Hey Tony, I'm one of the researchers on the pretraining team. Informal one here. What's your take on the current direction of open-source LLMs — do you think models like Qwen 3 and Llama 4 are eating into the competitive advantage of teams like ours that are doing in-house pretraining?

TONY:

Honestly, I think the relationship is complementary rather than competitive, but it requires a clear-eyed view of what in-house pretraining is actually for.

For a company like eBay, the value of in-house pretraining isn't to have the strongest general language model — Qwen 3 235B or GPT-5 will beat anything eBay can train on general benchmarks, and eBay can't win that arms race. The value is the domain specialization that only eBay's data can provide, the vocabulary optimization that gives 20–34% inference speedups on e-commerce text, the data governance of knowing exactly what went into the training corpus, and the ability to fine-tune and adapt without waiting for a vendor's permission.

Where open-source models do change the equation is in the baseline. When Llama 4 or Qwen 3 is already a strong base model, you spend less of your in-house compute budget on general capability and more on domain adaptation and efficiency — which is exactly what e-Llama does by continuing from Llama 3.1 rather than training from scratch.

The risk I'd flag is that continued pretraining assumes the open-source base model is a good starting point for the domain shift. For e-commerce, it generally is — but if eBay's specific needs diverge significantly from what the base model architecture supports, you might hit a wall. The vocabulary customization paper is interesting in that context because it shows you can retrofit domain efficiency on top of a general base — that's a nice middle ground.

I think the team doing in-house pretraining is in a stronger position than a team purely prompt-engineering on top of an API, because you have the ability to actually understand and modify what's inside the model. The competitive advantage of that capability grows over time.

TEAM MEMBER: What's your working style like? Do you prefer to work deeply alone or do you like working in a close pair or group setting?

TONY:

I tend to be structured-independent: I do my best deep work when I have blocks of uninterrupted time for coding and experimentation, but I actively seek out research discussions when I'm at a decision point or when I'm formulating a new idea. For me, research discussions are most valuable at two moments: early, when you're trying to figure out if an idea has a fatal flaw before you've invested time in it; and late, when you have preliminary results and you want pushback on the interpretation before you commit to a story.

In the MSCA fellowship at IMDEA, I was the only person working on my specific project, which meant I had to be disciplined about seeking out external feedback loops — I'd schedule a weekly check-in with the principal investigator even if I had nothing to present, just to stay calibrated on what questions the broader community cared about.

At TU Delft and in the collaborations with Gradient Networks and Tencent, the work was more naturally collaborative and I found that pair-debugging experiments or co-designing ablations was more efficient than working alone and then syncing asynchronously.

One thing I'd specifically value on your team: being close to the people running the actual pretraining runs. My research background is in how models behave after they're trained; I want to develop stronger intuition for what the training dynamics look like while the run is happening. That's a knowledge gap I'd actively work to close.

TEAM MEMBER: Where do you see LLM research going in the next two to three years? What are you most excited about?

TONY:

Three things I'm most excited about.

First, test-time compute as the new training-time scaling. The trajectory of o3, DeepSeek-R1, and the GRPO results suggests that you can get significant capability gains by giving the model more compute at inference — extended chain-of-thought, process reward models, verifiable feedback. What's exciting to me from an efficiency perspective is that this changes the routing problem: rather than routing to a bigger model, you can route to more compute for the same model. The cost-quality tradeoff becomes richer.

Second, multi-modality becoming truly native. LLaVA-style connector models have improved enormously but they're still bolt-on. Models like InternVL3 and Llama 4 are starting to treat image tokens as first-class citizens in the same vocabulary as text. When that's done at pretraining scale with truly interleaved image-text data, I expect qualitative improvements in visual grounding and multi-image reasoning that are hard to achieve with connector models. For eBay's use case — understanding product images with the same depth that the model understands product text — this is directly relevant.

Third, agentic reliability. We're at the point where agents can do impressive things in controlled settings, but production reliability — knowing when to ask for clarification, avoiding tool hallucination, recovering gracefully from errors — is still the bottleneck. The GRPO/RLVR results suggest that verifiable rewards are a powerful training signal for reasoning reliability. I'd expect to see similar progress applied to agentic task success as a training signal.

Attention Formula (must know cold)

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( QK^T / sqrt(d_k) ) V

where Q, K, V ∈ R^{seq_len × d_model}
d_k = d_model / num_heads

Multi-head: concat heads, project:
MultiHead(Q,K,V) = Concat(head_1,...,head_h) W_O
head_i = Attention(QW_i^Q, KW_i^K, VW_i^V)

Complexity: O(n^2 × d) per layer for attention over sequence length n.

Key Design Tradeoffs (one-line version)

  • GQA vs MHA: GQA always in production (fewer KV heads → smaller KV cache → more throughput)
  • RoPE vs ALiBi: RoPE with YaRN for known long-context fine-tuning; ALiBi extrapolates better zero-shot; YaRN target length set at training time, not ad-hoc
  • DPO vs PPO: DPO for simplicity; PPO if online RL and reward hacking are critical
  • LoRA vs full FT: LoRA always for budget/speed; full FT for max quality on large domain shifts
  • AWQ vs GPTQ quantization: AWQ needs no per-task calibration; GPTQ is task-specific but sometimes marginally better
  • Continued pretraining vs RAG: continued pretraining for distribution shift (need model weights to know domain); RAG for knowledge retrieval (domain knowledge fits in retrieved context)
  • BPE vs SentencePiece Unigram: BPE with byte fallback for universal coverage; Unigram for morphologically rich multilingual
