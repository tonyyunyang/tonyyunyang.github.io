eBay Company and AI Dossier — Stage 1 Prep

1. eBay Business Model and Scale

What eBay Is (and Is Not)

eBay is a pure marketplace: it owns no inventory and runs no warehouses. Revenue comes from transaction fees (final value fees, ~10–14% of GMV depending on category), promoted listings / advertising, and ancillary services (eBay Money / payment processing in some markets, shipping integrations). This asset-light model means eBay's unit economics are dominated by software, AI, and trust infrastructure — not logistics. Every percentage point of AI-driven improvement in listing quality, search relevance, or conversion flows directly to the bottom line.

Scale as of 2025–2026: - ~132 million active buyers globally - ~17 million active sellers - Present in 190+ markets - Full-year 2025 GMV: ~$80 billion (up from ~$73B in 2024) - Full-year 2025 Revenue: ~$11.1 billion - Q1 2026 GMV: $22.2 billion (+14% YoY) — strongest quarterly growth in years - Q1 2026 Revenue: $3.09 billion (+19.5% YoY) — first time exceeding $3B since Q1 2021 - Advertising revenue FY2025: ~$2 billion (1P ads grew 17%+)

Revenue Streams

Stream Mechanism AI leverage point
Transaction fees % of closed sale value Better search/recommendations → higher conversion → more closed sales
Promoted Listings (1P ads) Sellers pay for placement Keyphrase relevance ranking, LLM-as-judge, distillation
eBay International Shipping Cross-border fulfillment MT quality → more cross-border listings discovered and bought
Subscription / Store fees Seller stores Listing AI → more sellers onboarding

Focus Categories

eBay's focus category strategy concentrates investment on high-engagement enthusiast verticals where eBay has structural advantages (rare inventory, C2C liquidity, enthusiast communities) over Amazon or Walmart:

  1. Motors / Parts & Accessories (P&A) — largest single category; repair-over-replace tailwind; 2.3 billion parts listings; AI-driven fitment matching is critical
  2. Trading Cards / Collectibles — authentication (eBay Authenticate), graded card pricing models; strong AI image analysis use case
  3. Luxury — watches, handbags, jewelry; AI authenticity signals + brand detection in VLMs
  4. Refurbished Electronics — eBay Refurbished program; condition grading AI
  5. Fashion / Apparel / Sneakers — "Shop the Look," visual search, sneaker authentication
  6. Pre-loved / Secondhand (Recommerce) — ~90% of eBay GMV is non-new items; cross-category

Focus category GMV grew 16%+ in Q4 2025; five focus categories each exceeded $10B in annual GMV in 2025.

Recommerce and Sustainability Positioning

eBay's differentiator against Amazon is recommerce: the pre-owned and refurbished economy. Over 40% of GMV in 2025 came from pre-owned/refurbished goods (depending on exact classification). eBay is leaning into this as both a growth driver and a sustainability/ESG narrative: - Net-zero carbon emissions target for 2045, validated by Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) - Every sale of a pre-owned item extends product lifespan, reducing new manufacturing demand - AI enables the recommerce economy to scale: automated condition assessment, value estimation, smart listing generation from seller-uploaded photos

Buyer / Seller Dynamics

  • Sellers: range from casual C2C (single items) to large Power Sellers and business accounts (B2C). The listing funnel is a major investment area — reducing friction = more inventory = more GMV
  • Buyers: bifurcated between enthusiasts (deep category knowledge, long sessions) and transactional buyers (price-driven, search-led). eBay is shifting to serve enthusiasts first
  • Trust gap: unlike Amazon (guarantees, standardized products), eBay sells unique items with variable condition — AI for item identification, authentication, condition grading, and pricing is structurally more important here than on any other major marketplace

2. eBay AI/ML Stack — Deep Detail

2.1 LiLiuM — eBay's In-House LLM Family

Paper: arXiv 2406.12023 (Jun 2024, ACL Workshop)

LiLiuM (eBay's own name) is the clearest statement of eBay's AI philosophy: build the foundation from scratch when the upside in efficiency, customization, and data governance justifies the cost.

Architecture: - Decoder-only transformer - Three sizes: 1B, 7B, 13B parameters - Context: 4,096 tokens - Positional encoding: RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings) - Activation: SwiGLU (gated linear unit — standard post-PaLM efficiency choice) - 1B required additional experimental tuning vs. 7B/13B

Training: - 3 trillion tokens, multilingual - Sources: RedPajama-V2 (general web), eBay marketplace listings (proprietary e-commerce), parallel MT data, code, encyclopedic sources - Language emphasis: DE, ES, IT, FR (for cross-border MT capability) - Filtering: heuristic deduplication + fuzzy matching - Checkpoint averaging applied at end of training (stabilizes final model — cheap but effective)

Custom Tokenizer: - Vocabulary: 65k tokens (versus LLaMA-2's 32k) - Three goals: (1) full control over special tokens; (2) better multilingual representation; (3) e-commerce term specialization - Key insight: common e-commerce terms (brand names, product categories, model numbers, part numbers) that are split into multiple BPE sub-words in a general tokenizer become single tokens in LiLiuM's vocabulary → fewer autoregressive steps → up to 34% generation speedup vs LLaMA-2 on eBay-specific tasks - This is not a marginal optimization — 34% faster generation at eBay's serving scale translates to significant infrastructure cost reduction

Performance: - English NLU: comparable to LLaMA-2 - Non-English NLU, machine translation, e-commerce tasks: LiLiuM beats LLaMA-2 - E-commerce benchmarks: Aspect Prediction, Item Selection tasks

Strategic rationale for building 100% in-house: - No licensing constraints (LLaMA has usage restrictions for >700M MAU products) - Full data governance: eBay marketplace data contains buyer/seller PII, pricing data, transaction history — can't train on it via external providers - Custom vocabulary → inference efficiency at scale - Foundation for downstream fine-tuning and VLM extension (e-Llama, eBay VLM)

Talk track: "The 34% speedup from tokenizer design alone is essentially free efficiency — no architectural change, no model compression, just better vocabulary alignment to the domain. My routing work approaches the same problem from the other side: assign cheaper models to simpler queries. The two are composable — a well-tokenized LiLiuM serving as the 'cheap specialist' in a routing system would multiply these gains."

2.2 e-Llama — Domain Adaptation of Foundation LLMs

Paper: arXiv:2501.09706 (eBay). Know this paper cold — Versley leads the org behind this work.

eBay's dual-track strategy: (A) LiLiuM = build from scratch; (B) e-Llama = adapt Meta's existing Llama 3.1.

Method: - Base: Llama 3.1 8B and 70B (Meta) - Approach: continued pretraining on 1T domain-specific tokens - Data mix (ablation-determined): 50% e-commerce / 50% general domain; 10% of total is non-English - Infrastructure: 480 NVIDIA H100 80GB GPUs, 60 nodes, NVLink intra-node + InfiniBand inter-node - Framework: Megatron-LM with 3D parallelism (data + tensor + pipeline) - Attention: Flash-Attention-2 - Context during continued pretraining: 8,192 tokens (Llama 3.1 native is 128k, but they reduce this for continued pretraining efficiency) - Training scale: 70B on 1T tokens ≈ 340,000 GPU-hours over ~one month - Batch: ~11.8M tokens; 85,000 update steps

Key design choice — learning rate: - Final LR: 10% of original Llama pretraining LR (3.0e-5 for 8B; 1.5e-5 for 70B) - Too high → catastrophic forgetting; too low → slow domain absorption - Ablation:

LR multiplier E-com score General score
0.1× (chosen) 59.9 42.0
1× 58.6 40.5
10× 50.6 34.5

Key design choice — data ratio: - 50% e-com is optimal; above 50% gives no further e-com gain but degrades general capability

Results: - English e-commerce tasks: ~25% average improvement (e-Llama 70B Aspect Prediction: 59.2 vs 42.8 baseline) - Non-English e-commerce: ~30% average improvement across DE/ES/FR/IT - General domain: some degradation, especially for the smaller 8B; 70B retains general capability better (catastrophic forgetting largely mitigated by the 50/50 data mix and conservative LR)

Model merging: - Parameter averaging of base Llama-3.1 + final e-Llama checkpoint gives near-linear trade-off curve - Zero additional compute cost; lets you dial domain vs. general balance at deployment time - This is a cheap engineering trick with real production value

Tony connection: The model merging technique used in this eBay work is simple parameter averaging. The broader landscape includes SLERP, DARE, TIES-merging, and task arithmetic (Ilharco et al. 2023). If Versley asks about merging, you can note you know the broader landscape but their choice of simple averaging was validated by the ablation showing near-linear trade-off — so more complex methods aren't necessary here.

2.3 Vocabulary Customization for Efficient Deployment

Paper: arXiv 2509.26124, Sept 2025 (arXiv preprint) — Versley is co-author.

  • Problem: "Token fertility" — general BPE tokenizers over-split domain-specific terms. "iPhone 14 Pro Max 256GB Space Gray" becomes many subwords in LLaMA's tokenizer; in a domain-adapted vocabulary, this can be 3–4 tokens.
  • Algorithm: Extend existing tokenizer with domain-specific tokens with a monotone efficiency guarantee — no input is segmented into more tokens than before. This is the principled follow-up to LiLiuM's vocabulary design.
  • Results: Up to 20% input sequence compression on e-commerce text; reduced inference latency; no accuracy loss.

Why this matters to you: Token count reduction = fewer autoregressive forward passes = directly reduced latency and cost. This is the vocabulary-level analog of your routing work (reducing compute per query). Mention this connection explicitly if the topic comes up.

2.4 Vision-Language Models for E-commerce

Paper: arXiv:2602.11733 (2026) — Versley is a co-author; this is his most recent major paper and the best one to engage him on directly.

This is eBay's full recipe for building production-grade VLMs for e-commerce. Study it carefully because it directly overlaps with your Human Intent World Model work.

Architecture (modular LLaVA-style): - Vision encoders: SigLIP2-SO400M-Patch14-384 or Qwen2.5 ViT (tested both) - Text decoders: Llama3.1-8B, e-Llama3.1-8B, LiLiuM 1B/4B/8B, Qwen3 4B/8B, Gemma3 4B/12B/27B

Three-stage training: 1. Vision-Language Alignment — BLIP-LAION 558k corpus; aligns visual features to text decoder space 2. Mid-Stage Training — filtered datasets 3. Visual Instruction Tuning — 4M internal e-commerce instructions + general data

Data pipeline scale: - ~15M raw marketplace listings processed - InternVL-2.5-26B used for captioning; Mistral-Small-3-24B for visual-textual verification - ~4M instruction-tuning examples surviving quality filter - 100k items for multi-image "Item Intelligence" fine-tuning (median 5 images/item) - Distribution: VQA 45%, Dynamic Attribute Extraction 30%, Precise Instruction Following 12.5%, Product Listings 12.5%

Key results:

Task Best F1/Acc Best model
Aspect Prediction 56.2% SigLIP2 + Qwen3-8B
Deep Fashion Understanding 60.1% SigLIP2 + Qwen3-8B
Dynamic Attribute Extraction 68.5% SigLIP2 + Qwen3-8B
eComMMMU (public) 58.3% LiLiuM-8B
Item Intelligence F1 58.8% Gemma3-27B + better labels

Efficiency finding: Fine-tuned Gemma3-4B achieves 3.8× inference speedup vs. zero-shot Gemma3-27B while improving F1 from 44.8% → 50.5%. This is a concrete data point that fine-tuning smaller domain-adapted models dominates zero-shot prompting large general models — relevant to your efficiency thesis.

Key technical findings: - Domain-adapted text decoder (e-Llama) gives 5–12% improvement on e-commerce tasks vs. generic Llama - Multi-image generalization: models trained on single-image data generalize to multi-image without explicit training - Vision encoder choice (SigLIP2 vs. Qwen2.5ViT) was task-dependent, not conclusively better overall - Bounding-box labeling strategy improves Item Intelligence F1 to 58.8

Tony connection: Your Human Intent World Model took a similar approach — synthetic data + fine-tuned VLM for domain-specific inference. Your specific angle was customer intent (behavioral/visual cues → purchase intent), while eBay's is attribute extraction (product properties). The synthetic reasoning traces in your work are analogous to eBay's Visual Verification Pipeline. Prepare to draw this parallel clearly.

2.5 Machine Translation

Business context: Cross-border trade is one of eBay's structural advantages. A German buyer browsing US listings, or a US buyer finding a rare part from a Japanese seller, can only transact if the listing is comprehensible. AI-driven MT directly increases GMV from international transactions. Historical data point: NBER study showed eBay MT increased US exports to Spanish-speaking Latin America by 17.5%; ITIF found ~18% international trade increase from eBay's AI MT.

LiLiuM MT role: Pretrained on parallel MT data (included in 3T token corpus); outperforms LLaMA-2 specifically on MT tasks. Multilingual tokenizer (65k vocab, with DE/ES/IT/FR emphasis) was explicitly designed to support MT.

eBay's Translation API: Developer-facing API offering MT for marketplace listings (item titles, descriptions); helps inventory reach new markets. eBay acquired AppTek's MT talent for this purpose.

RAG for Product Title Translation (arXiv 2409.12880, ACM Workshop 2024):

The standard MT problem for eBay is product titles — extremely short (avg 11.3 words), contextually sparse, domain-specific jargon, brand names, model numbers. Standard MT systems fail on these.

Solution: - Retrieval: BM25 (Lucene 8.11.3, OKAPI BM25) over bilingual product catalog - Index types: titles (TTL), bullet points (BP), product descriptions (PD), combined (T.B.D.) - Scale: >10 million bilingual pairs (EN-NL, EN-PL, EN-DE, IT-DE indexed) - Model: Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct (few-shot prompted with retrieved bilingual examples) - Configurations: 1-shot and 5-shot

Results (chrF over zero-shot baseline):

Pair 1-shot 5-shot
EN-PL +10.9% +15.3%
EN-SE +11.2% +14.0%
EN-NL +7.4% +12.0%
DE-CS +5.9% +5.9%
IT-DE +2.7% +3.6%
EN-DE +1.5% +2.0%
TR-DE +2.3% +1.6%

chrF chosen over BLEU: Titles are short → 4-gram BLEU statistics too sparse to be meaningful. chrF (character n-gram F-score) is more appropriate for short domain-specific strings. (If Versley asks about MT metrics, this is the right answer.)

Key insight from results: RAG helps most for language pairs where the backbone LLM (Mixtral) has low proficiency (EN-PL, EN-SE). High-resource pairs (EN-DE) benefit less — the LLM already knows the domain well. RAG fills in the gaps.

Tony connection: Your Diffusion Weights paper was evaluated on WMT16 (machine translation benchmark). This is a natural bridge. You can mention that WMT16 is one of your evaluation benchmarks and ask Versley about the specific challenges of product-title MT vs. sentence-level MT — the sparsity and jargon issues are fascinating from an NLP perspective.

2.6 Recommendation Systems

Scale context: >2 billion active listings; hundreds of millions of users; real-time serving with hundreds-of-milliseconds latency constraints. Recommendations drive a large fraction of sessions that don't start with a specific search query.

Mercury — Agentic AI Platform for LLM-Powered Recommendations:

eBay's internal agentic framework for scaling LLM-based recommendation experiences.

Design principles: - "Prompts as code" — all prompt changes go through PRs, code reviews, unit tests (rigor over speed) - Modular Agents: each encapsulates a prompt + input/output transformation layer - Chains: sequential agent steps with consistent interfaces (analogous to LangChain/LlamaIndex patterns but custom)

Listing Matching Engine (the retrieval + ranking subsystem inside Mercury): - Query expansion: topic → multiple specific queries via LLM - Exact match retrieval: eBay's internal search engine (precision-optimized) - Semantic similarity: KNN with vector databases, BERT-based + custom deep learning - Anomaly detection: category validation, price range validation, title alignment - Hard filters: NSFW, condition requirements, user preferences (size, location) - Personalized ranking: user behavior signals + round-robin for diversity

Infrastructure: - Distributed queue-based message passing (near-real-time) - Auto-retry with self-healing - On-the-fly priority adjustment - Multi-model: custom fine-tuned Llama, Mistral, LiLiuM (internal) + GCP/Azure/OpenAI (external APIs) - RAG with user/listing data, Common Crawl, Google Search for real-time signals - Safety: content moderation, LLM safety mechanisms, prompt injection detection

BERT-Based Recommendations (foundational layer): - eBay pre-trained NLP/BERT model on 3 billion item titles - Cosine similarity between item-title embeddings as ranking feature - Now using Vertex AI Vector Search for embedding-based retrieval (scans billions of items)

Shop the Look (April 2024, iOS): - GenAI-powered outfit recommendation carousel - Personalized based on browsing history (requires 10+ fashion views in past 180 days) - Interactive hotspots revealing similar items and inspirations - Uses decades of behavioral data + billions of images

Tony connection: Mercury's multi-model architecture (Llama, Mistral, LiLiuM, external APIs) is exactly the deployment pattern your LLM Router addresses. Mercury currently handles model selection by business logic rules; a learned router (your work) would dynamically optimize cost/quality. Frame your routing research as directly applicable to the Mercury platform.

2.7 Search and Ads Ranking

Search Ranking — "Progressive Refinement" (SIGIR 2025): - Machine-learned ranking (MLR) baseline trained on 2 weeks of eBay search log data - Incorporates sequences of clicked items as short-term contextual signals - Uses eBERT embeddings (eBay's BERT variant) + cosine similarity between query and item - Progressive refinement: in-session buyer activity updates rankings in real-time

LLM for Search Relevance (2024–2025): - "From Reasoning LLMs to BERT: A Two-Stage Distillation Framework for Search Relevance": LLM-generated rationales guide distillation into lightweight cross-encoders for production serving - "LREF: Novel LLM-based Relevance Framework for E-commerce Search" (2025): LLM judge for relevance labels at scale

Advertising / Keyphrase Recommendations:

"To Judge or not to Judge" (ECAI 2025): LLM-as-judge for advertiser keyphrase relevance. - Task: recommend keyphrases to sellers for bidding; must be relevant to prevent search quality degradation - Problem: click/sales signals are biased — "irrelevant but clicked" scenarios pollute training - Solution: LLM-as-judge at scale as a proxy for seller relevance judgment - Three-way optimization: seller judgment ↔ advertising system ↔ search system

LLMDistill4Ads (arXiv 2508.03628, 2025): Knowledge distillation pipeline. - Architecture: LLM teacher → cross-encoder assistant → bi-encoder EBR student - Goal: LLM-quality signal at production bi-encoder latency - Addresses click-induced bias - Result: more varied, seller-aligned keyphrase suggestions

This distillation pipeline is relevant to Tony's cost-efficiency work: using LLM signals (expensive) to train cheap production models is exactly the "buy good labels from large model, serve with small model" pattern.

2.8 Product Knowledge Graph

eBay's product knowledge graph is the structured backbone for listing quality and Magical Listing: - Encodes product taxonomy, ontology, attribute-value structures, and product relationships - Used by AI agents to identify specific products and fill structured item specifics (category, brand, model, condition, color, size...) - Published research: "Enabling Explainable Recommendation in E-commerce with LLM-powered Product Knowledge Graph" (arXiv 2412.01837) — LLMs querying/augmenting the KG for recommendation explainability

The KG is the key asset that eBay's AI has that general-purpose LLMs don't: deep, structured, proprietary knowledge about tens of millions of specific product SKUs, categories, and attributes.

2.9 Magical Listing and GenAI Products

Magical Listing (fully AI-native, launched 2024, next-gen Q4 2025): - Not "AI layered on legacy flow" but architecturally AI-first - Flow: seller photographs item → AI guides which additional photos to take → VLM + LLM agents process photos → autonomously generate title, category, item specifics, price estimate - Leverages: advanced VLMs + LLMs + product knowledge graph - Impact (as of Q1 2026): - >25% decrease in average listing time - >50% increase in new listing creation rate - Double-digit % increase in sold items and GMV per lister - 500 million listings created using Magical Listing (cumulative as of Q1 2026) - 50%+ increase in new listing creation rate in U.S. rollout - 10M+ unique sellers have used eBay's AI features - Won AI Breakthrough Award for "Best Overall Generative AI Solution" (2024) - Rolled out to all C2C sellers in US, UK, Germany

System design implication: Magical Listing is a multi-step agentic pipeline: (1) image ingestion + processing via VLM; (2) product identification via knowledge graph lookup; (3) attribute extraction; (4) title generation via LLM; (5) pricing suggestion. Each step could theoretically be served by a different model — a routing problem.

Shop the Look: Described in §2.6 above.

AI Video Generation (2025): Tool for US sellers to generate short-form social media videos from product listings.

eBay AI Shopping Agent (May 2025 beta): - Real-time hyper-personalized recommendations + expert guidance - Conversational interface - Tested in limited beta; agentic search beta showed 50% more search engagement and double-digit purchase behavior increases

eBay + OpenAI (Operator) Partnership: - eBay was a launch partner for OpenAI's Operator (autonomous web agent, early 2025) - Operator is approved to interact with eBay on behalf of users for product discovery - eBay built a "unified agentic commerce platform": eBay's hybrid cloud + LLM ecosystem + MCP server + proprietary data layer - Separate: eBay + OpenAI "AI Activate" in UK — helping 10,000 small businesses access AI tools

Third-Party Agent Restrictions (from February 20, 2026): - eBay updated user agreement to ban unauthorized third-party AI agents from autonomously placing orders (targeting "buy-for-me" bots) - Strategy: eBay wants to control the agent experience to protect ad revenue attribution and user trust - Exception: partners with prior express permission (OpenAI Operator) - This reveals eBay's agentic commerce philosophy: controlled, first-party agentic UX rather than open API free-for-all

Internal AI Productivity: - eBay internally using Claude Code for developer tooling (bug triage: 2–3 days → 1–2 hours) - AI email subject lines → 40%+ increase in quality visits - CEO Jamie Iannone declared 2025 "a watershed year" — from GenAI pilots to "scalable agentic experiences"

5. Why eBay — Authentic Talking Points for Tony

These are genuine reasons grounded in Tony's actual research interests. Do not use them as canned answers; use them as anchors for authentic conversation.

5.1 The Routing Problem Is Alive Here

eBay's Mercury platform already runs multiple LLMs in parallel (LiLiuM, e-Llama, Llama fine-tuned variants, Mistral, external OpenAI calls) for different tasks. There is no learned router deciding which model handles which query — it's currently rule-based or priority-queue-based. Tony has three papers on exactly this problem. The fit is not coincidental; it is structural. eBay is the environment where routing research becomes real infrastructure.

5.2 The Efficiency Thesis Finds Its Testing Ground

Tony's thesis is "AI is moving from a scaling race to a systems race." LiLiuM's 34% speedup from tokenizer design, the vocabulary customization paper's 20% sequence compression, and Gemma3-4B beating Gemma3-27B zero-shot after fine-tuning — eBay's team is building the same thesis into production. The difference is that at eBay, "more efficient" means serving billions of queries per month, not just a benchmark number. The stakes make the work real.

5.3 Machine Translation as a First-Class Citizen

Tony's Diffusion Weights paper was evaluated on WMT16. eBay uses machine translation as a direct revenue driver for cross-border commerce. The RAG title translation paper shows there are still open technical problems in domain-specific, short-text MT that are genuinely unsolved. Tony's interest in multilingual NLP (WMT16 evaluation + multilingual eye-tracking work at IMWUT) connects directly to eBay's core MT infrastructure.

5.4 VLMs for Intent, Not Just Attributes

Tony built the Human Intent World Model: a VLM that infers customer intent from visual/behavioral cues. eBay's VLM (arXiv:2602.11733, Versley co-author) focuses on attribute extraction from product images. These are complementary: attribute extraction tells you what the item is; intent modeling tells you why the buyer wants it. A combined system (item attributes + buyer intent signals → personalized search re-ranking) is a natural research extension. This is a genuine intellectual contribution Tony could bring to the team.

5.5 Agentic Systems That Ship, Not Just Demo

The chance to work on agent infrastructure that is used at this scale, with real users and real GMV stakes, is qualitatively different from academic agent benchmarks.

5.6 The Research-Production Balance

6. Talking Points That Show You Did Your Homework

These are specific, non-obvious things Tony can say in the interview to signal genuine engagement. Each references something not on the company homepage.

On e-Llama (eBay team, arXiv:2501.09706):

"The data ratio ablation in the e-Llama paper was striking — going from 50% to 75% e-commerce data in continued pretraining gave essentially no additional e-commerce gain but started hurting general capability. That's a good argument that the benefit of domain data saturates early, and after that you're just paying in catastrophic forgetting risk. I'd be curious how sensitive that curve is to the specific mix of e-commerce sources — whether eBay listing text is interchangeable with product reviews."

On the vocabulary customization paper (Versley, Sept 2025 (arXiv preprint)):

"The monotone efficiency guarantee in your vocabulary extension paper is elegant — you're not just adding tokens, you're proving that no sequence gets longer. That's the kind of guarantee that makes infrastructure teams trust a research result. The 20% sequence compression number is directly translatable to serving cost. I'm curious whether this interacts with the 50% e-com / 50% general pretraining data split — if the new tokens only appear in the e-com half, how well do they generalize at inference for multilingual queries?"

On the VLM paper (Versley co-author, arXiv:2602.11733):

"The finding that Gemma3-4B fine-tuned on e-commerce beats Gemma3-27B zero-shot, with a 3.8× inference speedup, is the strongest argument I've seen for the 'fine-tune small, don't prompt large' paradigm in a domain-specific setting. My Human Intent World Model work reached a similar conclusion — the synthetic reasoning traces we used for fine-tuning mattered more than the base model size. I'm curious whether the eBay result holds when the query requires reasoning over multiple images from the same listing — your multi-image Item Intelligence task."

On LiLiuM tokenizer:

"The 34% speedup from tokenizer design in LiLiuM is essentially free efficiency — no architecture change, no compression, just better vocabulary alignment. That's the same fundamental logic as my routing work but at a different layer: instead of routing the query to a cheaper model, you're making the model itself cheaper per token by reducing fertility. These two approaches are composable — a routing system that routes to a well-tokenized domain-specific model would multiply both effects."

On MT and WMT16:

"I evaluated Diffusion Weights on WMT16 because MT is a natural stress test for a training dynamics method — you can measure translation quality before and after fine-tuning with chrF or BLEU and the signal is clean. Reading the RAG title translation paper from your team, I see the e-commerce MT problem is harder than WMT16 in a specific way: 11-word product titles are so sparse that standard sentence-level MT metrics break down. The chrF choice over BLEU makes sense — character n-gram F-score is much more robust at that title length."

On Magical Listing:

"The 500M listings milestone is a striking number not just as a product metric but as a data flywheel signal — every listing created by AI with user acceptance or rejection is labeled training data for the next generation of the VLM. That's a self-improving system at a scale very few teams get to build."

7. Concrete Things to Try on ebay.com Before the Interview

The recruiter explicitly asked what excited Tony about eBay. These are specific product experiments to do beforehand so Tony can speak from experience, not abstraction.

Must Do (direct interview relevance)

  1. List an item using Magical Listing. Go to ebay.com (or app) → Sell → upload a photo of any physical object. Observe: how does the AI generate the title? What attributes does it extract? Where does it fail (wrong category, wrong brand)? Can you see the product knowledge graph working (specific model number vs. generic category)? Time how long it takes vs. how long manual listing would be. Note: you'll need an eBay seller account.

  2. Search for something niche in a focus category. Try a specific car part (e.g., "BMW E46 rear subframe bushings"), a trading card (e.g., "1986 Fleer Michael Jordan RC PSA 9"), or a vintage luxury watch (e.g., "Rolex Submariner 5513"). Observe: how does search ranking work? Are results personalized? Does the search understand your query semantically or just keyword-match? Notice any AI-generated summaries or product comparisons.

  3. Use Shop the Look (if you have iOS app, browse fashion items). Look at the outfit carousel personalization. Note what behavioral signals it uses (requires 10 fashion views — browse 10 clothing items first). This is the GenAI recommendation system in production.

  4. Browse eBay in a non-English language (switch browser to German, or use the German eBay site de.ebay.com). Search for something in English and observe: does the search work cross-language? Are German listings surfaced for English queries? See the MT system at work (or observe where it fails).

  5. Check the agentic shopping feature (if available in your region). Look for any "Shop with AI" or conversational search interface. Note the difference between keyword search and intent-driven agentic search.

Good to Do (context and talking points)

  1. Find a cross-border listing (e.g., a Japanese automotive part, a German collector item) and observe: is the translation natural? Does the item specifics box contain structured attributes (color, size, compatibility) or just free-text? This is where the knowledge graph + VLM pipeline either works or breaks.

  2. Look at promoted listings vs. organic listings in a search result. This shows you the advertising stack (keyphrases, bid-based placement) that the LLM-as-judge keyphrase recommendation paper is trying to improve.

  3. Check eBay's "authenticity guarantee" flow for a sneaker or luxury watch listing. This shows the authentication pipeline (AI + human verification) at work — another AI-heavy system.

  4. Read 2–3 posts on the eBay Innovation Blog (innovation.ebayinc.com), especially "AI Trends to Watch in 2026" and any Magical Listing or Shop the Look posts. These are public statements of strategic direction that Tony can reference naturally.

  5. Search your own niche interest (e.g., running gear, tennis equipment, cooking equipment — from Tony's personal interests) and evaluate: does eBay's search understand enthusiast-level queries? What is missing that AI could fix? This gives Tony a genuine personal angle on why eBay's AI work matters.
