Hiring-Manager Behavioral Playbook — Versley Round (Story Bank + Why-eBay)

This is your spoken story bank for the 60-minute round with Yannick Versley. Read it aloud in the days before. Every number here is the verified one; where the human/interpersonal part of a story isn't in your record, you'll see a [CONFIRM TONY — …] gap you must fill before the room. Don't paper over those with invention — an honest "I'd have to check the specifics" beats a confident fabrication every time.

New to STAR? Read doc 22 (STAR Method — Learn It First) before this one. That doc teaches the shape (what goes in S/T/A/R, how long each part runs); this doc loads the ammunition (the word-for-word stories). Come here once the method makes sense.

1. How this round works

Versley is the Hiring Manager (Senior Tech Lead Manager, LLM pretraining, eBay CoreAI Amsterdam), so the 60 minutes is part technical deep-dive and part behavioral/competency (STAR) conversation — he is evaluating both whether you can do the work and how you operate with people. Compensation is OFF-LIMITS with him. It's a recruiter-only topic, so if pay comes up you redirect ("I've been keeping comp with the recruiter"). Be concise and let him cut in: he is measured, he wrote the neighbors of your papers, and a short honest answer lands far better than a long one.

2. Your 4 core stories (primary + backup)

Format reminder. Situation + Task ≤ 45 seconds; whole answer ~2 minutes; then stop and let him follow up. Bolded S/T · A · R are just your internal signposts — don't say "situation" out loud. The scripts below are deliberately short (~150–200 words, comfortably under the 2.5-min cap) so you don't pad them under nerves. Each one is tagged with a rough target.

Caveat placement — read this once, it resolves a real tension. You have two different "caveats" and they go in different places: - A caveat on an over-claimable NUMBER (in-distribution only / no measured speedup / "80% removed") goes first, in the opening sentence of your Result. Leading with it reads as rigor. - Role / byline humility (3rd of 17, no direct reports) goes once, in the Result, said plainly — not as your opening line. Opening a story by deflating your own contribution reads as under-confident. So: lead with the win or the hook, put number-caveats first in the Result, and keep byline humility to one clean sentence near the end.

P1 — A challenging project you delivered

PRIMARY · "The hardware wall on the eye-tracking dataset"

Fires for: P1 (challenging delivery), project management, cross-functional, lead-without-authority. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~30s.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "The hardest thing I've shipped end-to-end was a VR eye-tracking emotion dataset. The part that nearly sank it was a hardware limitation, not the ML."

S/T — As co-first author at TU Delft, I set out to read emotion from the eyes in VR using only the sensors a headset already has. The catch surfaced early. Our headset's built-in Tobii tracker only gave us derived gaze: processed numbers, not the raw signal. The whole idea depended on having the raw periocular video, and that was locked behind a commercial SDK we'd have had to buy.

A — Rather than buy our way out, I integrated a Pupil Labs add-on to capture the raw eye video directly. Then I built the synchronization and labeling pipeline around it, including an open-source Unity tool so anyone could reproduce the protocol. I coordinated a co-first author and a senior external collaborator I couldn't direct, ran two participant studies, and built the fusion model: a ViViT video encoder fused with a time-series transformer.

R — Caveat first: it's a modest 26-participant set, shared on request under an agreement rather than as an open download, and our fusion actually loses slightly at the half-second window — which we reported rather than buried. With that said: seven emotions, the Unity tool released open-source, and the paper published at IMWUT 2025.

BACKUP · "The measurement wall on nnU-Net"

Fires for: P1 (challenging delivery), scoping, honesty about unfinished work. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~20s.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "A cleaner 'I hit a wall and designed around it' story is the nnU-Net pruning work — at one point I literally couldn't score my own results."

S/T — As first author, I wanted to know how much of a trained nnU-Net — the standard medical-segmentation model — is actually redundant. The blocker hit immediately: these benchmarks don't release test-set labels, so I had no ground truth to measure pruning damage against.

A — I defined a proxy Dice: how much the pruned model agrees with the unpruned model's own predictions. A proxy can quietly lie, so I anchored it on ACDC, the one dataset that does have public labels, and checked the two curves tracked together. I scoped it tight — one-shot magnitude pruning, no fine-tuning — so I was measuring redundancy, not a retraining trick. And I pruned all five cross-validation folds independently, so it wasn't one lucky split.

R — Caveat first: this is unstructured pruning, so there's no measured speedup — same dense footprint. The finding: over 80% of the weights came out while proxy Dice stayed above 0.95, across four datasets, with an inverted-U showing where the redundancy lives. First-author paper, MIDL 2025. I frame real latency wins as the structured follow-up the redundancy map enables, not as something I've already delivered.

Read this first — it's your thinnest real material. You have never managed a vendor or an end customer in a commercial role, and there is no documented difficult interpersonal episode in your record. So you have two options below, and they are not equal: - The MeetaVista primary is a scaffold: the collaboration is real, but the conflict and resolution are reconstructions you must replace with a real memory. - The peer-review backup is your safer, genuinely-grounded fallback: you published at MIDL and IMWUT, so you provably did receive real reviewer pushback. Only the specifics need recall, not invention. If you can't fill the MeetaVista gaps, lead with the peer-review story. Also: do not tell the embellished MeetaVista version that appears in doc 10 (the "two investors praised the honest scoping" detail is unverified — do not state it as fact). Use the BRIDGE below to frame whichever one you use honestly.

PRIMARY (scaffold) · "MeetaVista — scoping a claim the product side wanted broader"

Fires for: P2 (difficult stakeholder), research-to-business translation, technical advice to a non-technical audience. (Aim ~2 min.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "My most honest 'difficult stakeholder' story is the MeetaVista pilot, where the product side wanted a broader claim than the model could honestly support. The difficulty was managing expectations without damaging the relationship."

S/T — I collaborated with MeetaVista on an NDA pilot, fine-tuning a VLM to infer customer purchase intent — internally the "Human Intent World Model." The collaboration is real. Here's the tension I'd describe: [CONFIRM TONY — was there an actual disagreement where the business/product side pushed for rule-based sales scripts, or for a "works in any retail setting" claim, while you wanted to protect honest generalization? What did they literally say and ask for?]

A — [CONFIRM TONY — what did you actually do? e.g. walked them through where the model held up vs broke, showed the in-distribution vs out-of-distribution gap, proposed a narrower "validated use cases" scope?] The defensible technical anchor you can state: our strong number, 91.3%, is in-distribution only, so any "works anywhere" claim would have been over-reach — and flagging exactly that is the honest move.

R — [CONFIRM TONY — how did it resolve? Did they accept the narrower scope? What was their reaction / the human outcome?]

BACKUP (real, low-confirmation) · "A reviewer I had to win over on a published paper"

Fires for: P2 (difficult external party), defending methodology under challenge. (Aim ~2 min.)

This is the safer P2 option, because the event is real — you published nnU-Net (MIDL) and the eye-tracking paper (IMWUT), so you genuinely received reviewer pushback and answered it in a rebuttal. Treat that reviewer as the difficult external party you had to win over. Only the specific objection and response need recall.

Say-it-like-this opener: "The most real 'difficult external party' I can point to is peer review. On my published papers I got hard pushback from reviewers I had to win over on the merits, not by authority."

S/T — One genuine, recurring objection on this kind of work is circular evaluation: if you score a method using the same model's own predictions, the result can look self-fulfilling. I've had to answer exactly that. [CONFIRM TONY — pick ONE concrete reviewer objection you actually received — nnU-Net at MIDL, or the eye-tracking paper at IMWUT — and state what the reviewer challenged.]

A — [CONFIRM TONY — what did you do in the rebuttal: add an ablation, anchor on ground truth, run a control, tighten the scope?] The anchor you can state for nnU-Net: you answered the "the proxy just measures self-agreement" worry by showing the real-Dice curve on ACDC tracking the proxy-Dice curve, so the proxy was calibrated against ground truth wherever it could be checked.

R — The honest, grounded landing: the rebuttal held and the paper published (MIDL / IMWUT 2025). [CONFIRM TONY — add the specific outcome: did that reviewer concede, change their score, or ask for the exact control you ran?]

The "difficult customer" BRIDGE (how to reframe honestly)

If Versley frames it as a customer or vendor, don't pretend you've had that role. Say it plainly, in short pieces he can absorb one at a time:

  • "I want to be upfront: I haven't managed external vendors or end customers commercially. My one conventional industry role was a support-engineering role at NXP."
  • "My closest honest analog is the MeetaVista pilot. There, the hard part wasn't a personality clash. It was holding the line on what the model could truthfully claim when the business wanted a bigger promise."
  • "The discipline still transfers: read their real goal, show the data behind the limit, and offer a narrower thing you can actually defend."
  • "But I'll be honest — that was a research collaboration, not a customer account."

That reframe is both true and exactly the competency they're probing.

P3 — A time you used technical expertise to advise

PRIMARY · "Owning the TwinRouterBench evaluation methodology"

Fires for: P3 (technical advice), defending a method, scoping a buildable problem. (Aim ~2 min; S+T ~30s.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "On TwinRouterBench, the slice the rest of the team relied on me for was the evaluation methodology — how each routing step actually gets labeled and costed."

S/T — TwinRouterBench scores routing per step of a multi-step agent task. The measurement problem I owned: to label which model tier each step needs, the naive approach reruns every tier at every step. That's on the order of 4^N configurations — you simply can't build that corpus.

A — I'll separate the three pieces, because they're often blurred. First, the labeling protocol — we call it downgrade-and-cascade. Greedy sequential-locking makes the search tractable: fix the best tier at one step, lock it, move on, which collapses 4^N to roughly 4·N. Then each candidate downgrade is accepted only if the full trajectory still passes end-to-end — execution-verified replay, not a local guess — with each tier checked as a pool of models, not a single one. That's how a step actually gets labeled. Second, separately, I pushed for failure-aware cost accounting, so a cheap router that breaks a step gets charged for the failure rather than quietly rewarded for being cheap. (You've probably seen this pattern at eBay scale — I'd be genuinely curious how you label step difficulty in production.)

R — The payoff: that protocol is what made the 970-step corpus actually buildable, and it's the slice the team leans on me for. To be precise on credit: I'm 3rd of seventeen, listed as an independent researcher — I own the labeling and cost-accounting, not the project framing. [CONFIRM TONY — name who actually received/adopted this advice (a co-author? the lead?) and what they changed, so this answers the "advise a person" prompt and not just "I designed a method."]

BACKUP · "The ML voice on a physics-led MRI team"

Fires for: P3 (advising across a boundary), cross-functional, honesty about a negative result. (Aim ~2 min.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "A different flavor of advising-with-expertise is the Reverse Imaging project, where I was the machine-learning voice on a team led by MR physicists."

S/T — Reverse Imaging is a cardiac-MRI reconstruction project led by MR physicists, with NIH/NHLBI and Microsoft Research collaborators. I was 4th of eight, the CS/ML contributor advising across the ML-to-physics boundary, where the two sides genuinely speak different languages.

A — My contribution was on the learning side, and I'll be honest about a real negative we hit: one variant, RI-T2S, actually underperformed the baseline on short-echo-time GRE data. Rather than paper over it, I treated that failure as the signal — it's precisely what motivated the next method, the RI-Aug augmentation approach.

R — It's a preprint with eight authors; my role was the ML advisory slice, not leading the physics. The thing I'd point to is that we diagnosed the failure mode honestly and turned it into the follow-up, which is how cross-disciplinary advising should actually work.

P4 — A time you used DATA to influence a customer / colleague / manager

Before you pick: P4 is an INFLUENCE prompt — it needs a real person whose mind changed. In both options below, the data is fully grounded; the named person is the gap. Making naming that person your top P4 prep task is what turns these from "I defended a method" into "I changed someone's mind."

PRIMARY · "Making an 80%-removed claim believable with the ACDC curve"

Fires for: P4 (data to influence), credibility under skepticism. (Aim ~2 min.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "The clearest time I used data to win an argument was making an aggressive compression claim believable. 'You removed 80% of the network and it still works' invites disbelief unless your measurement is airtight."

S/T — When I claimed over 80% of a trained nnU-Net could be removed, the natural objection is: "Your proxy Dice just measures the model agreeing with itself. That's circular." It's a fair objection, and it's the first thing a careful reviewer raises. [CONFIRM TONY — if a real collaborator or reviewer actually voiced this doubt, name them; that upgrades the story from "I answered the anticipated objection" to "I changed a specific person's mind," which is what the prompt rewards.]

A — So I didn't lead with the proxy. I led with ACDC, the one dataset with public ground-truth labels. I showed the real-Dice curve sitting right alongside the proxy-Dice curve, and they tracked each other. That let me say: the proxy is calibrated against ground truth everywhere we can check it, so trust it where we can't. I also showed the inverted-U redundancy map, so the claim had a mechanism, not just a headline number.

R — Caveat first: it's unstructured pruning, so no measured speedup. The win: that reframed it from a suspicious headline to a measured finding, and it's what carried the paper through MIDL review.

BACKUP · "The accounting choice that flipped a router ranking in TwinRouterBench"

Fires for: P4 (data changing a decision), rigor, defending a metric on first principles. (Aim ~2 min.)

Say-it-like-this opener: "A sharper 'the data changed a decision' example: on TwinRouterBench, one accounting choice flipped which router we treated as best."

S/T — On the static track we were ranking routers by cost savings, the CostSave metric. The subtlety: if you only count what a router saves and ignore the cost of the steps it gets wrong, a reckless cheap router looks like the winner.

A — There are five ways to do that accounting. Four of them rank a rule-based router, ClawRouter, on top. Only the failure-aware version — where a router that breaks the task is charged for the failed steps instead of credited for being cheap — flips it: SR-KNN at 56.18 over ClawRouter at 53.82. I argued the failure-aware version is the correct one on first principles: a router that saves money but breaks the task hasn't saved anything. I backed it with a second signal — a frontier model used as the router predicted "high difficulty" on only 7 of 147 verified-hard steps, so a naive frontier router wasn't doing the work we'd assumed.

R — That changed which router the team treated as the reference. Caveat I keep attached: it's a benchmark, and the labels are tied to a frozen 11-model pool and a price snapshot, so the ranking is only valid under that snapshot. [CONFIRM TONY — name the colleague who initially favored the other ranking, to make this "influenced a person" rather than "influenced the team's decision."]

If he pushes "variant E is cherry-picked — you picked the metric that makes your router win" (he likely will — this is his day job): answer on first principles, not on the ranking. Charging failures is the correct accounting regardless of which router comes out ahead; and the fact that four of five variants rank ClawRouter on top is itself the finding — the ranking being metric-sensitive is something we chose to publish, not bury. The full line-by-line defense is in doc 20 (TwinRouterBench → "How Versley will probe it"). Don't improvise this one cold.

3. Leadership style (~150 words spoken)

Say-it-like-this opener: "I'll lead with the honest caveat — I've never had formal reports or line-managed anyone — so when I say 'leadership,' I mean three concrete things."

"First, leading without authority through co-first-authorship. On the eye-tracking paper I coordinated a co-first author and a senior external collaborator, people I couldn't direct, so the job was alignment, not instruction. Second, leading by example and by teaching. I was a TA and MSc mentor across about five courses at TU Delft, and the way I lead a mentee is to make the problem crisp and then let them own it. Third, self-direction: my independent program is a solo-prioritized backlog of ten to fifteen ideas, ranked by 'what would I learn?' and 'what's the smallest experiment that could prove me wrong?' If I compress it to one line: get to clarity fast, design the smallest falsifying experiment, and let the evidence — not seniority — settle it. I haven't managed people yet; that's a growth edge, not a claim."

4. Two more they often ask

Failure / setback — "MERA's null result, and a regret about engaging production late"

(Honest, spoken. Aim ~2 min.)

Opener: "The failure I'm most comfortable owning is MERA's model-evolution result — it simply didn't work the way we hoped, and we chose to report that."

S/T — MERA has two halves: a routing half, and a model-evolution half where the system is meant to improve cumulatively as it sees more tasks.

A/R — The routing half held up: about 87.3% router-decision accuracy on the held-out set, at roughly 52% of always-large serving cost. But the evolution half showed no cumulative gain — pass rate stuck near 47%, essentially flat. We could have quietly dropped that half; we reported it instead. The deeper lesson connects to a real regret: I didn't engage with production realities early enough. At the MeetaVista pilot, the gap between lab accuracy and real-world usefulness was genuinely humbling — a number that looked great in-distribution, 91.3%, told you much less about whether the thing was actually useful in the wild. So my takeaway is to measure the thing that matters, in the setting that matters, even when it makes the headline weaker.

Ambiguity / pivot — "Leaving the Marie-Curie fellowship to go independent"

(Honest, spoken. Aim ~2 min.)

Opener: "My biggest deliberate step into ambiguity was leaving the Marie-Skłodowska-Curie fellowship mid-stream to go independent."

S/T — I'd been an MSCA fellow at IMDEA Networks in Madrid: a prestigious, funded, secure track. But the work pulling at me — LLM systems, routing, efficiency — was moving far faster outside the academic timeline than inside it.

A — So in early 2026 I made a deliberate bet. I left the fellowship and went independent, to work directly with industry collaborators — Gradient Networks, Commonstack, MeetaVista, Tencent — on the systems problems I actually wanted to solve, on a faster clock.

R — It's genuinely high-ambiguity, no institutional safety net, and I won't pretend it's risk-free. But it's consistent with how I've always moved: electrical engineering, then computer systems, then medical imaging, then wireless sensing, now LLM systems — each pivot toward where I'd learn the most. Honestly, an Amsterdam team doing exactly this systems-race work, but at production scale, is where I'd like that trajectory to land.

6. Smart questions to ask Versley (5–6, no comp)

These are framed as open problems and trade-offs, not as recitations of facts you already quoted in §5 — so they read as curiosity, not as performing your research twice.

  1. Team & roadmap: "On the Foundation Models / pretraining team, how much of the roadmap is in-house model pretraining versus adapting and routing across model sizes for specific shopping-agent tasks?"
  2. How research ships: "When applied research produces a result, what's the path from a finding to something that touches buyers — is there a platform path it rides, or is it more bespoke per project?"
  3. Where routing breaks down: "In your agentic stack, where does the model-routing decision break down most today — is it labeling step difficulty, the cost accounting, or keeping the policy stable as the underlying models change beneath it?"
  4. Success in 6–12 months: "What would a strong first six-to-twelve months look like for someone in this role — a shipped model improvement, a published result, infrastructure, or some mix?"
  5. Who owns the trade-off: "When a task-specialized small model and a bigger general one both clear the quality bar, who actually owns that call — the researcher, or a platform-level policy — and what tips the decision in practice?"
  6. Ways of working: "How is the Amsterdam team split between research and engineering, and how hands-on in the modeling do you like to be with the people you lead?"

7. Behavioral do / don't quick-card

DO DON'T
Lead with the caveat on any over-claimable number ("This is in-distribution only…", "no measured speedup"). Bury that number-caveat until a follow-up drags it out.
Put role/byline humility once, plainly, in the Result. Open a story by deflating your contribution ("I'm just a regular co-author…").
Lead clean, defensible wins with the win or the hook. Sandwich every achievement between two apologies — it reads as under-confident.
Keep Situation + Task ≤ 45s, whole answer ~2 min, then stop. Over-talk — he's measured and will cut in; pause 2–3s and match his pace.
If a number isn't in your head, say "I don't have that figure in front of me — directionally it's…" Invent a number under pressure (your #1 historical failure).
Own unfinished work plainly: Cost-Adaptive Routing has no results yet; MeetaVista's 91.3% is in-distribution only; MERA's evolution half shows no cumulative gain; nnU-Net has no measured speedup. Over-claim, smooth over, or imply these are finished wins.
On routing/cost-accounting stories, tell them as "I decided / I did", keep them tight, and hand the floor back. Lecture Versley about his own papers, authorship, or what routing is — highest relational risk.
Redirect comp to the recruiter if it surfaces. Discuss compensation with Versley at all.
For P2, bring one real memory (the peer-review backup is your safest) and use the honest BRIDGE. Tell the embellished MeetaVista version (no "investors praised us" claim).

8. Note on consistency

Two projects can legitimately serve more than one prompt. The risk is telling the same anecdote three contradictory ways in one hour. Lock these:

  • MeetaVista is canonical to P2 — the "difficult stakeholder" prompt, framed as honest research-to-business scope translation (the product side wanting a broader claim than the model could defend). Use it there. Do not also deploy MeetaVista as your P4 "data-to-influence" story or your leadership story in the same conversation. The 91.3% number stays attached to one honest caveat ("in-distribution only") wherever it appears.
  • nnU-Net appears twice on purpose: the P1 backup (delivering against a measurement wall) and the P4 primary (using the ACDC curve to make a claim credible). Genuinely different facets — fine to have both, but in one interview pick the facet the question asks and don't tell both back-to-back.
  • TwinRouterBench also appears twice: the P3 primary (the labeling/cost-accounting methodology you advise on) and the P4 backup (the failure-aware accounting choice that flipped a ranking). Same project, different facets. If you've already used TwinRouterBench for P3, reach for the nnU-Net P4 primary first and keep the TwinRouterBench accounting story as the second example only if he asks for another — don't run two TwinRouterBench stories back-to-back.
