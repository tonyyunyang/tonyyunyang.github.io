Story 1 — Pruning nnU-Net: Delivering a Research Goal Under Severe Technical Constraints

Competencies proved: Delivering under constraints, Ownership, Data-driven decision-making

Situation. In January 2025 I joined TU Delft Imaging Physics as an AI Research Engineer on a short-term contract (Jan–Jul 2025, 6 months). The goal set by the lab was to investigate whether the flagship medical-image segmentation model nnU-Net could be substantially compressed without harming clinical accuracy. The context made this genuinely hard: (1) medical images are 3-D volumetric scans where each voxel matters — you cannot afford the kinds of accuracy drops that are acceptable in NLP benchmarks; (2) I was new to the codebase, which is large, bespoke, and auto-configures itself per dataset with little external documentation; (3) the compute budget was a shared GPU cluster with no guaranteed allocation, meaning runs could queue for days; (4) the lab had active clinician collaborators at a partnering hospital who needed reproducible, reliable results.

Task. Own the full research arc end-to-end: literature review, methodology design, implementation, ablation study, and a published output — all within the 6-month contract window, with the MIDL Short Papers deadline as the hard external target.

Action. I started with a literature scan across network pruning for medical imaging and made a few early calls: (a) unstructured magnitude pruning (zeroing individual weights below a threshold) is the cleanest way to measure how much of a trained network is redundant, even though it leaves dense matrix operations unchanged, so any real speedup would be a later, structured step; (b) most of these segmentation benchmarks (the Medical Segmentation Decathlon tasks especially) do not release public test-set labels, so I could not score against ground truth directly; (c) no prior work had mapped nnU-Net's redundancy layer by layer across sparsity levels. So I scoped the question tightly: how much of an already-trained nnU-Net can you remove, and where does the slack live? I built one-shot, post-training magnitude pruning on top of the standard nnU-Netv2 pipeline — train each model to convergence, then set every weight below a fixed global threshold τ to zero, with no retraining and no fine-tuning afterward — and swept τ to trace the sparsity-versus-fidelity curve. To measure damage without test labels, I used a proxy Dice: the overlap between the pruned and the unpruned model's predictions, with ACDC (the one dataset that has real labels) as a ground-truth anchor. I pruned each of the 5 cross-validation folds independently so the result wasn't an artifact of one lucky split, and I documented every sweep in a shared lab notebook so the clinician collaborators could track decisions without reading code. The result that mattered wasn't just the headline compression — it was a per-layer inverted-U redundancy pattern: the bottleneck and deepest layers, which hold the most parameters, turned out to be the most prunable, while the shallow encoder and decoder ends held the critical weights, and that pattern held across all four datasets.

Result. Published at MIDL Short Papers 2025 (first author). Showed that over 80% of a trained nnU-Net's weights can be removed while proxy Dice stays above 0.95 across four 2D/3D medical datasets, plus the inverted-U map of where that slack lives. I was deliberate that this measures redundancy, not speed: unstructured pruning leaves a sparse tensor with the same dense footprint, so the paper reports no speedup — I framed real latency/memory wins as the structured-pruning follow-up that the redundancy map enables, not as a delivered result. The Imaging Physics lab now has a reproducible compression recipe applicable to future nnU-Net variants. The work was completed on time for the MIDL deadline with two weeks to spare for revisions.

What I would do differently. I would push the evaluation further than a four-page short paper allowed — in particular, get a true ground-truth Dice on held-out labels for every dataset rather than leaning on proxy Dice for three of the four, and add per-layer scale normalization before treating the inverted-U as a settled importance result rather than a consistent empirical finding. I also underestimated how much the auto-configuration of nnU-Net would interact with my pruning hooks; a short spike to understand the config DAG before writing pruning code would have saved me a refactor.

Connection to eBay. eBay's LiLiuM team (which Yannick leads) faces the same tension: a custom e-commerce tokenizer achieves 34% generation speedup while maintaining quality. The same principle of domain-aware compression — compress carefully, measure the real downstream metric, not a proxy — applies at LLM scale. My pruning mindset maps directly to the kind of efficiency-quality Pareto thinking that is central to LiLiuM's design.

Story 2 — VR Eye-Tracking Dataset: Coordinating Cross-Team Human-Subjects Research

Competencies proved: Cross-team coordination, Dealing with ambiguity, Ownership, Leadership

Situation. In mid-2023 I joined TU Delft Embedded Systems as an HCI Research Assistant with Bishwas Regmi (co-first author) to build a multi-faceted eye-tracking dataset for emotion recognition in VR — the first to capture raw high-frame-rate periocular video alongside higher-frequency gaze with discrete Ekman emotion labels, using only the kind of sensors a headset already has on board (no external physiological rigs). This was not a typical ML dataset project — it required: ethics approval from TU Delft's human-subjects board (IRB equivalent); recruiting and scheduling healthy participants; running them through a 7-emotion VR protocol at 240 Hz gaze sampling; coordinating with the VR equipment lab (a separate group with its own booking calendar); wrangling synchronised multimodal eye streams (raw periocular video, gaze, and pupil data); and doing all of this while both Bishwas and I were also carrying coursework and TA responsibilities. At the start, no protocol existed; the target sample size was undefined; and neither of us had run a human-subjects study before.

Task. Co-lead the full study — split responsibilities clearly with Bishwas, navigate IRB approval, recruit and run participants, and produce a clean, publishable dataset with an associated recognition method ready for IMWUT submission.

Action. Bishwas and I began by agreeing on a division of labour that matched our respective strengths: I owned the data pipeline (synchronisation of 240 Hz gaze streams with head-pose and event triggers, storage format, anonymisation) and the ML recognition method; Bishwas owned the VR stimuli design and participant interaction protocol. We formalised this split in a shared document after our first working session — I had seen co-author attribution disputes in other lab projects and wanted to pre-empt any ambiguity. For IRB approval I read every prior TU Delft study in our area that had been approved, extracted the key elements the board required, drafted the consent form, and submitted a tight protocol description that framed the study as minimal-risk (no deception, voluntary withdrawal at any time). This cut the typical review time from 8 weeks to approximately 5 weeks because we had anticipated the standard objections. Participant recruitment was slower than expected — reaching participants purely through student channels was hard, so I proposed extending to staff and community members via a departmental newsletter, which the PI approved. We reached our final sample of 26 participants (a first study of 20, then a second round of 6 with new stimuli) by adding extra recording sessions. The 240 Hz data synchronisation was technically the hardest part: the eye-tracker's timestamp domain was different from the VR headset's clock, and drift accumulated over a 20-minute session. I implemented a clock alignment step using shared trigger events at the start and end of each VR scene, which reduced synchronisation error to under 5 ms — well within a single gaze frame. When Bishwas and I disagreed on how to handle two participants whose data was partially corrupted, we resolved it by pre-specifying an exclusion criterion (>10% of frames missing in any emotion block = exclude that block, not the participant) so the decision was rule-driven, not post-hoc.

Result. Published at IMWUT/UbiComp 2025 (co-first authorship). The dataset covers 7 Ekman emotions across 26 participants and pairs raw high-frame-rate periocular video with 240 Hz gaze and discrete emotion labels — the genuinely novel combination in this space — though it is shared on request under a data-sharing agreement rather than as an open download (the public artifact is the open-source Unity collection/labeling tool). The benchmark fusion model we developed is efficient and scalable. The IRB process completed without a request for major revision, which the PI noted was uncommon for a first submission. The cross-team logistics (equipment lab, ethics board, two co-equals with overlapping schedules) were completed without a single missed participant session after we established the shared calendar.

What I would do differently. I would push for a pilot cohort of 5 participants before full data collection to catch the clock-drift issue earlier — it emerged only at session 12, partway through data collection. The fix was straightforward once found, but it required retroactively reprocessing all prior sessions.

Connection to eBay. eBay's Magical Listing product uses VLMs to infer product attributes from images — a form of intent and attribute recognition from visual signals. The structured approach I took to building a clean multimodal dataset (synchronisation, exclusion criteria, public release standards) maps directly to the data-quality discipline required when training or fine-tuning VLMs on e-commerce images.

Story 3 — LLM Router with Gradient Networks: Influencing a Technical Direction with Data

Competencies proved: Influencing with data, Stakeholder management (industry partner), Technical expertise to advise

Situation. In February 2026, working as an independent researcher in collaboration with Gradient Networks, I proposed building a rigorous benchmark and routing strategy for LLM inference cost reduction (the paper now under NeurIPS 2026 review). The initial framing from the Gradient Networks side was straightforward — they wanted to show their infrastructure could switch between models cheaply, and a simple heuristic router demonstrating cost savings would suffice for their marketing. I believed that heuristic routing without a systematic evaluation framework would produce inflated and non-reproducible cost claims that would not survive peer review and would undermine the scientific credibility of the collaboration.

Task. Persuade the Gradient Networks technical contacts that a rigorous benchmarking framework was worth the additional build time, and co-produce a result strong enough for a top-tier venue.

Action. Rather than arguing the point abstractly, I ran a quick set of preliminary experiments in the first two weeks: I took three existing routing heuristics from the literature (confidence-based, length-based, and a simple learned classifier), ran them on a shared subset of tasks from three established benchmarks, and tabulated the cost-versus-performance results. The key finding was that the heuristics showed wildly inconsistent cost savings across task types — 60% cost reduction on easy factual queries, but sometimes negative utility (routing to the cheaper model when the harder one was actually needed) on multi-step reasoning tasks. I presented this as a one-page data summary — not a slide deck, just a table with a clear headline: "Our current baseline gives 60% cost reduction on factual tasks but degrades performance on reasoning tasks by 8 percentage points. Without a principled benchmark, we cannot know which claim to believe." This reframed the question from "is our router good?" to "what is a router actually good at?" — which is the scientifically interesting question. The Gradient Networks contacts agreed to extend the project scope to include a multi-axis benchmark covering diverse task types and difficulty levels. We also agreed to test routing at the step level for multi-step agentic tasks (assigning individual steps to cheaper models rather than routing whole tasks), which was the key insight that drove the final results.

Result. The final paper demonstrates greater than 90% cost reduction versus using a single SOTA model per step, at matched performance, across a range of agentic task types. The step-level routing insight was the differentiating contribution over prior work. The paper is under NeurIPS 2026 review. Gradient Networks now has a defensible, externally reviewable cost claim rather than an anecdote. The decision to invest in rigorous benchmarking also directly produced a second paper (TwinRouterBench, an arXiv preprint with code released via CommonstackAI, co-authored with equal-contribution leads Yang P. and Chen W. — I'm a regular 3rd author of 17) which provides a fast static track and a live dynamic track for agentic LLM routing evaluation.

What I would do differently. I should have negotiated the authorship and IP terms with Gradient Networks more explicitly before starting the technical work. We resolved it amicably, but it required a conversation mid-project that would have been cleaner to have on day one.

Connection to eBay. This maps directly to eBay's need to serve LiLiuM (1B/7B/13B) efficiently at scale. A routing layer that directs easy queries (simple product category questions) to the 1B model and hard ones (cross-lingual attribute extraction) to the 13B is exactly the cost-performance Pareto that my work quantifies. The "influencing with data" framing also matches Yannick Versley's rigorous-empiricist profile — he will recognise the value of a well-designed ablation.

Story 4 — Human Intent World Model: Difficult Stakeholder — Navigating a Product Team's Conflicting Requirements

Competencies proved: Difficult stakeholder situation, Technical advice to non-technical audience, Dealing with ambiguity

Situation. In March 2026, I began a collaboration with MeetaVista on the Human Intent World Model project — fine-tuning a VLM to infer customer purchase intent from visual cues and respond with sales reasoning. MeetaVista is a small product company with a non-technical leadership team. Early in the project, there was a significant mismatch between what the product team wanted (a demo they could show investors within four weeks that "reads a customer's mind from a photo") and what was technically deliverable in that window (a prototype VLM fine-tuned on a small synthetic dataset with measurable but narrow generalisation). The product lead pushed hard for a wide claim ("works on any retail setting") despite the dataset being scoped to a specific sales context derived from classical sales literature.

Task. Deliver a technically sound prototype within the four-week window, correct the scope of the claims without damaging the relationship, and set expectations that would survive contact with real investor scrutiny.

Action. I scheduled a one-hour technical walkthrough with the product lead, using concrete language, no jargon, and a visual analogy: "Our model right now is like a new sales rep who has read every sales psychology book but has only shadowed real calls in a furniture showroom. It is extremely good at reading intent in that context, and it will generalise partially to similar retail settings, but we shouldn't promise a CEO demo in a car dealership yet." I then prepared a two-slide data summary: slide one showed the benchmark accuracy on the synthetic held-out set (strong); slide two showed the performance on a small real-world interaction sample I had run as a quick test (weaker, with a clear error analysis showing which visual cues the model missed). The key action was volunteering the second slide — showing the limitation proactively rather than waiting for an investor to find it. I proposed a revised demo scope: show the model working on three carefully chosen retail scenarios where we knew it would perform well, label these as "validated use cases," and describe the path to broader generalisation (more training data, domain expansion, active learning loop) as the product roadmap. The product lead initially resisted — "why not just show the best cases and call it general?" — and I held the line: "Because if an investor tests it on their own examples and it fails, we lose credibility and it makes the technology look worse than it is. Showing three scenarios we can defend is stronger than claiming ten we cannot." They agreed.

Result. The investor demo went well. The product lead told me afterward that two investors specifically praised the "honest scoping" of the demo as a sign of a mature technical team — the opposite of the risk they had feared. The project continued into a second phase with an expanded data collection plan. The VLM fine-tuning achieved strong downstream real-world interaction results in the validated scenarios, and the paper is in progress.

What I would do differently. I should have established a weekly written status update cadence from the start. The scope conflict arose partly because verbal discussions with the product team had left both sides with different mental models of what "the system can do." Written status notes with explicit assumptions listed would have surfaced the mismatch two weeks earlier.

Connection to eBay. This maps directly to eBay's Magical Listing product — a VLM-driven agentic system that takes product images and generates listings. The challenge of scoping VLM capabilities honestly to non-technical product stakeholders is identical. At eBay scale, this matters even more because false capability claims translate into production failures visible to millions of buyers and sellers.

Story 5 — Leaving the MSCA PhD Fellowship: A Values-Based Decision Under Uncertainty

Competencies proved: Dealing with ambiguity, Failure and retrospective, Values alignment

Situation. In July 2025 I joined IMDEA Networks in Madrid as a Marie Skłodowska-Curie Fellow — one of the most prestigious early-career research fellowships in Europe, with a clear track to a PhD and academic career in privacy-preserving wireless sensing. By early 2026, after roughly seven months, I made the decision to leave the fellowship and transition to independent applied AI research. This was not a crisis departure — the work was intellectually interesting, the funding was secure, and the institutional support was real. The decision was made from a position of clarity, not desperation.

Task. Make a well-reasoned decision about whether to continue a prestigious but increasingly misaligned path or take the harder, riskier route toward the applied systems work I believed was more important — and do so without burning bridges.

Action. I gave myself a structured self-audit over six weeks before taking any action. I wrote down, concretely, what I had published and built in seven months at IMDEA versus what I had shipped and published in applied LLM work I was doing on the side (the early foundations of what became LLM Router and Diffusion Weights). The contrast was stark: the wireless-sensing work was producing interesting academic results but the deployment gap to real impact was 5–7 years; the applied LLM work was producing results that were immediately applicable to production AI systems and was attracting industry collaboration. I also honestly assessed my intrinsic motivation: I am energised by systems that work in the real world under constraint, not by publishing in academic venues for their own sake. I raised this with my IMDEA supervisor directly and honestly — I did not fabricate a personal reason or give a vague excuse. I explained that my research interests had consolidated around applied LLM systems and that I did not believe I could give the wireless-sensing work the depth of commitment it deserved. We agreed on a clean handover plan: I documented all work in progress, handed over code and data, and left the fellowship with the relationship intact. I then committed fully to the independent research path with the collaborations (Gradient Networks, MeetaVista, and other industry and academic co-authors) that I had been building.

Result. In the five months following the departure (Feb–Jun 2026): five papers either published or under review at venues including NeurIPS, ACL, EMNLP, and an RL-Eval workshop; active industry collaborations producing artefacts (LLM Router, Agentic Coding Bench, Cost-Adaptive Routing, Human Intent World Model); and a positioning as an independent researcher with a clear thesis ("AI systems race") that is generating interview interest at companies like eBay. The IMDEA supervisor remains a professional reference. There was no reputational damage.

Retrospective. The honest retrospective is that I waited slightly too long. The misalignment was clear by month four; I spent months five and six in a holding pattern partly from risk aversion and partly from the sunk-cost pull of the fellowship prestige. I would make the decision faster next time, with the same deliberateness but less delay.

Connection to eBay. This story demonstrates that I make decisions based on impact alignment, not credential accumulation. The eBay role is a research-to-product position — it requires someone who cares whether the system ships and performs, not just whether it produces a publication. My decision to leave an academic path for applied systems work is the clearest signal I can give on that front.

Story 6 — MSCA Wireless Sensing: Using Technical Expertise to Reframe a Problem

Competencies proved: Technical expertise to advise, Dealing with ambiguity, Ownership

Situation. At IMDEA Networks my initial project brief was defined loosely: "use machine learning for privacy-preserving wireless sensing — detect humans or obstacles from RF signals without cameras." The problem was underspecified in a technically important way: the literature at the time framed wireless sensing primarily as a classification problem (person present / not present), but the IMDEA team's longer-term ambition was dynamic predictive reasoning — inferring what someone is doing and what they might do next. These two framings require fundamentally different architectures: a classifier can use a simple CNN over a spectrogram; a world model for dynamic prediction requires representation learning over temporal sequences with an internal state. The team did not have a clear view of which framing was right for the long-term goal.

Task. Advise the team on the right technical framing before committing significant compute and time to an approach that might be dead-end for the stated ambition.

Action. I spent two weeks doing a structured literature review across three adjacent areas: wireless sensing ML (the obvious area), world models in robotics and autonomous driving (where dynamic prediction is a solved research problem), and privacy-preserving representation learning (the constraint the project added on top). I synthesised this into a two-page technical memo — not a slide deck, a written document with citations — addressed to my supervisor and one senior lab member. The memo made three claims: (1) pure classification is insufficient for the stated 5-year goal; (2) the representation-learning framing (learn a latent representation of the RF environment's state, then train a predictive model on top) was technically feasible with existing architectures (borrowing from Dreamer/RSSM-style world models); (3) the privacy-preserving constraint was actually easier to satisfy in the representation-learning framing than in the raw-signal classification framing because the latent space naturally discards identifying information if trained with the right objectives. I presented this at a lab meeting and specifically invited pushback. The senior lab member raised a valid concern: "World models need a lot of data; RF datasets are small." I acknowledged this directly and proposed a mitigation — use transfer learning from larger RF datasets in adjacent domains (radar gesture recognition, where public datasets exist) and treat domain adaptation as a research contribution in itself.

Result. The team adopted the representation-learning framing as the working direction. My memo was circulated to the broader IMDEA Networks group as a problem-framing document. While I left the fellowship before the full project matured, the technical direction I articulated became part of the team's roadmap. More importantly for my own trajectory, the exercise of building world models for non-visual sensory inputs directly informed the Human Intent World Model project I pursued later.

Connection to eBay. Yannick Versley's team does domain adaptation of foundation LLMs for e-commerce. The exact same "representation learning + domain adaptation" thinking is the core intellectual problem there. The memo I wrote at IMDEA is structurally the same as the reasoning needed to decide whether to do continued pretraining (representation adaptation) versus task-specific fine-tuning (classification-style domain adaptation).

Story 7 — TU Delft TA and MSc Mentoring: Leadership and Leveling Up Others

Competencies proved: Leadership and mentoring, Technical advice, Delivering under constraints

Situation. Across my time at TU Delft (2022–2025) I was a Teaching Assistant on four courses: Supercomputing for Big Data, Embedded Systems Lab, Software Fundamentals, and Advanced Computing Systems. I also served as a formal MSc mentor to one or more graduate students. The courses ranged from systems-level (distributed computing, HPC) to ML-adjacent to fundamentals. TA roles at TU Delft are often treated as box-ticking support roles, but I decided from the start that my impact should be on student understanding, not just on marking completion. One concrete instance: in Supercomputing for Big Data, I noticed that a cohort of roughly 30 students was consistently failing the same type of distributed data pipeline question on lab assignments — not because they did not understand the concepts, but because they had no mental model for what "data locality" meant in a Spark context. Their code was logically correct but performatively wrong, and they could not explain why.

Task. Diagnose the root cause of a recurring comprehension failure in a large cohort and fix it within the constraints of the TA role (no control over lectures, limited lab hours, no ability to change the assignment).

Action. I built a 45-minute supplementary tutorial session — a "debugging a slow Spark job" live walkthrough using a real dataset from a prior year's assignment. I ran it as an optional session in the lab hour slot that was officially "open office hours." I used the profiler output from a deliberately slow student submission (anonymised) to show, step by step, where data was shuffling across nodes unnecessarily and what the fix looked like in code. I made the session interactive: I would stop at each step and ask students to predict what the profiler would show before I revealed it. Attendance was voluntary; 22 of 30 students came. On the next lab submission, the specific error type I had targeted dropped by approximately 70% in the cohort (I tracked this through my marking notes). I wrote up the tutorial as a two-page "common mistakes" document and shared it with the course lead, who added it to the course materials for the following year.

Result. Direct improvement in student performance on a specific, measurable error type. The tutorial document was retained in the official course materials — a small but concrete institutional impact. Several students mentioned the session in their course evaluations. My MSc mentee successfully completed their thesis and I continued as a reference for them.

What I would do differently. I wish I had done a more systematic pre-tutorial diagnostic — a short survey or quiz — to confirm that data locality was actually the root cause before building the tutorial. I inferred it from my marking observations, which turned out to be correct, but I was lucky. A two-question pre-test would have confirmed the hypothesis and potentially identified a second error cluster.

Connection to eBay. eBay's Amsterdam team is a research-to-production team where seniority involves leveling up colleagues and contributing to technical culture, not just shipping personal output. This story shows I already think that way and act on it without being asked.

Story 8 — Cross-Cultural Collaboration: China / NL / Spain / Remote

Competencies proved: Be For Everyone / diversity, Conflict and stakeholder management, Communication

Situation. My entire research career has operated across significant cultural and linguistic contexts simultaneously. My most structured example of managing cross-cultural friction is the MERA project (Model Evolution and Routing with Skill Adaptation, RL-Eval 2026 workshop), an internationally distributed collaboration: co-authors at US universities (Carnegie Mellon, UCLA), at Chinese universities (Soochow, Shanghai Jiao Tong), and at the startup Gradient, while I was in Madrid as an MSCA fellow, with additional collaborators in the Netherlands. We worked across three time zones, two primary research cultures (European academic norms around experimental rigour versus a faster-iteration startup culture on the Chinese side), and with English as the working language for all written communication but Mandarin used informally in video calls when it was faster.

Task. Maintain scientific rigour and clear technical communication across a geographically and culturally distributed team under the time pressure of a workshop deadline.

Action. The most concrete conflict I managed was about evaluation methodology. The Chinese collaborators preferred a faster ablation approach — run the router on the strongest available benchmark at the time and report the best headline number. I felt this was insufficient for a credible workshop paper: we needed to show performance across multiple task types and difficulty levels, and we needed to report cost alongside accuracy (not just accuracy). This was a genuine values difference, not a misunderstanding — the collaborators' publication incentive structure in their institutional context rewarded fast turnaround and strong absolute numbers; mine rewarded rigour and novelty. I did not argue by appealing to norms. Instead, I proposed a concrete experiment: run both evaluation approaches in parallel for two days and compare what we learn from each. The multi-axis evaluation revealed something the headline-number approach would have hidden — the router performed differently on agentic tasks versus single-turn tasks, which became a key finding in the paper. When I shared this finding the case was made empirically: "Look, the single headline number would have missed this. The multi-axis approach found something new." The collaborators agreed to the fuller evaluation framework. I managed the time zone logistics by establishing an asynchronous-first norm: all decisions documented in a shared Google Doc within 24 hours of any verbal discussion, with a 48-hour window for objections before proceeding. This respected the schedule constraints of colleagues 7–8 hours ahead.

Result. The paper was accepted at the RL-Eval 2026 workshop. The skill-differentiation finding (that routing quality differs by task type) became one of the three headline contributions. The relationship with Yao and Wang remains active — we are continuing the collaboration. Mandarin fluency was a genuine asset in informal calls when we needed to think through technical ideas quickly; English literacy meant all written artefacts (code, docs, paper drafts) were accessible to all collaborators equally.

Connection to eBay. eBay operates a genuinely global product — cross-border trade is core to its business model, and the Amsterdam team is international. The LiLiuM multilingual pretraining, the machine translation work, and the collaboration structure across Versley's team all require exactly this kind of cross-cultural technical communication facility.

Story 9 — Agentic Coding Bench: Prioritization Under Competing Pressures

Competencies proved: Prioritization, Ownership, Delivering under constraints

Situation. In May 2026, I co-led the design of Agentic Coding Bench with Gradient Networks — a benchmark to evaluate whether LLM agents can plan and build complete software projects from scratch, using real OSS challenges with hidden reference solutions and grading. This was a complex build: the benchmark required (a) curating real OSS challenges at the right difficulty level (not trivial, not impossible); (b) building a hidden grading harness that could evaluate diverse codebases automatically; (c) designing an agent interaction protocol that was fair across different agent architectures (some agents use tool calls, some generate code in one shot); (d) running the benchmark against the current strongest coding agents to produce initial results. All four tracks were interdependent but not sequential — we could work on them in parallel, but each had dependencies on shared design decisions. At the same time, I had two other active projects (Cost-Adaptive Routing and Human Intent World Model) at critical stages.

Task. Prioritize within the Agentic Coding Bench project to hit a meaningful milestone in four weeks while maintaining the other two projects, given that I was sole technical lead on all three.

Action. I applied a simple but explicit dependency-and-criticality triage. I mapped the four tracks of the benchmark against two axes: (1) how long does it block other tracks if delayed? and (2) how long does it take? The hidden grading harness blocked everything — without it, we could not evaluate any agent results and the entire project had no output. I spent the first week on nothing else. The agent interaction protocol had medium blocking (we needed it before running agent evaluations but could draft challenge curation in parallel). Challenge curation had the most uncertainty in duration but was parallelisable — I delegated the initial curation pass to a Gradient Networks team member, giving them explicit filtering criteria (must be solvable in under 4 hours by an expert human, must have a clean reference solution with automated tests). This freed me to finish the grading harness while curation ran in parallel. Running agent evaluations was the last-mile step, deliberately pushed to week 3 so it only ran once the infrastructure was solid rather than iterating on buggy early results. For my other two projects, I established a strict time-blocking discipline: Agentic Coding Bench got mornings (deep work, focus); Cost-Adaptive Routing got afternoons (currently at the analysis and writing stage, so lower cognitive load); Human Intent World Model got late afternoons (async communication with MeetaVista only, no deep technical work).

Result. The first version of Agentic Coding Bench was operational in four weeks. Initial evaluation results covering the strongest coding agents (Claude Code, Codex, and others) were produced. All three concurrent projects remained on track; no deadline was missed. The challenge curation delegation worked well — the criteria I had written were specific enough that the resulting curation required only minor revision from me.

What I would do differently. I should have built the grading harness to be more modular from the start. We later wanted to add a new challenge type (multi-file refactoring) and had to refactor the harness to accommodate it. A slightly more abstract harness design upfront — two extra days of work — would have saved four days of refactoring later. This is a classic "pay now or pay more later" engineering tradeoff that I over-optimised on the "pay now" side.

Story 10 — Diffusion Weights: A Failed Approach and What It Taught Me

Competencies proved: Failure and retrospective, Dealing with ambiguity, Delivering under constraints

Situation. The Diffusion Weights project (January 2026) reframes neural network fine-tuning as generative prediction — a U-Net diffusion model learns to forecast future weight states from earlier checkpoints, and multi-horizon predictions are merged to produce the fine-tuned model. The eventual result was a 3.2× wall-clock speedup on MMLU, OpenBookQA, and WMT16 translation tasks with comparable downstream performance. But the first two weeks of the project were a near-complete failure that I had to explicitly diagnose and reverse course on.

Task. Implement the core idea: train a generative model over neural network weight trajectories to predict future fine-tuned states.

Action — the failure. My initial implementation treated the weight trajectory as a time series and used a vanilla denoising diffusion model (DDPM-style) operating directly in full weight space. The model learned essentially nothing — loss did not decrease meaningfully and predicted weights produced random downstream performance. I spent four days debugging what I assumed was a hyperparameter issue. It was not a hyperparameter issue.

Diagnosis. I stepped back and did a proper failure analysis rather than continuing to tweak learning rates. The core problem was dimensional: a typical model's fine-tuning checkpoint is a tensor with millions of parameters. Treating this as a single high-dimensional data point for a standard DDPM was computationally intractable and statistically hopeless — there was no way to collect enough checkpoint samples to learn a useful distribution in that space. I had conflated "the idea works conceptually" with "this particular implementation of the idea is tractable." The fix was architectural: rather than operating in full weight space, operate in the space of low-rank perturbations (the LoRA delta matrices), which are 1–2 orders of magnitude smaller. This is not a trivial change — it required rethinking the data pipeline, the model architecture, and the loss function — but it was the right fix.

Action — the recovery. I wrote a one-page retrospective for myself documenting exactly what assumption had failed and why I should have caught it in week one (the dimensionality argument is basic — I could have done a back-of-envelope calculation on day one). I redesigned the approach around LoRA deltas, implemented a U-Net-style architecture over the delta trajectory (treating checkpoints at different training steps as a temporal sequence), and added multi-horizon prediction with learned merging weights. This version converged quickly and produced the 3.2× speedup result.

Result. Paper submitted to ACL 2026 (under review). The failure cost two weeks but the recovery produced a cleaner and more principled result than the original design would have. The retrospective note I wrote is now part of my personal "lessons" document that I review before starting any new project.

Retrospective lesson (explicit). The single most useful habit I have developed from this failure is the "back-of-envelope before you build" rule: before implementing any new ML idea, spend 30 minutes estimating whether the approach is computationally and statistically tractable. This is not glamorous, but it would have saved two weeks in this case.

Connection to eBay. This story is directly useful for the "tell me about a failure" question in any interview. It shows I can diagnose failure honestly, articulate the root cause clearly (not blame hyperparameters or randomness), and recover with a principled redesign — exactly what a senior research engineer on a pretraining team needs to do when an experiment does not work as expected.

Story 11 — MIDL Deadline: Delivering Research Under a Hard External Deadline

Competencies proved: Delivering under constraints, Prioritization, Ownership

Situation. The MIDL Short Papers deadline was fixed and external — it could not be moved. When I joined TU Delft Imaging Physics in January 2025, the lab's prior work had not yet produced a clean result on nnU-Net pruning; there were some preliminary experiments but no publishable conclusion. I had approximately four months to design the methodology, run all experiments, analyse results, and write a Short Paper (4-page limit, tight format). Short Papers at MIDL are competitive and require a complete, self-contained contribution, not a progress report.

Task. Produce a complete, publishable research contribution on nnU-Net pruning within a fixed four-month window, starting from a partially-explored problem with no established methodology.

Action. I front-loaded the methodology decision: in the first two weeks I read the existing preliminary experiments, identified what they had and had not tested (they had tested magnitude pruning at low sparsity ratios but never pushed to extreme sparsity, and had never mapped the redundancy layer by layer), and committed to a specific experimental design rather than exploring open-endedly. Committing early was a deliberate strategy — open exploration is appropriate when the deadline is distant; when it is fixed, you need a clear path to a result even if it is not the globally optimal path. I then set internal milestones at the 6-week and 10-week points (full ablation complete; writing complete with figures), which gave me a two-week buffer before the actual deadline for revisions. The main risk I identified was GPU queue delays. I mitigated this by scheduling the most compute-intensive runs (training each model to convergence before the pruning sweep, then the highest-threshold settings) first and running them over weekends when the queue was shorter. I also precomputed baselines that I could include in the paper regardless of the high-sparsity outcome, so the paper would have publishable content even if the most ambitious result did not land cleanly.

Result. Paper submitted on time. Accepted at MIDL Short Papers 2025. The headline result did land — over 80% of weights removable with proxy Dice above 0.95, plus the inverted-U redundancy map — and became the contribution. The two-week buffer was used productively to improve the figures and tighten the writing, not to rescue a late result. No extension was required and no content was cut for time.

Connection to eBay. Product research roles at eBay operate on product-cycle deadlines, not open academic timelines. The discipline of front-loading methodology decisions, setting internal milestones with buffer, and precomputing fallback results is directly applicable to delivering LLM experiments on a product team's schedule.

Story 12 — Cost-Adaptive Routing: Influencing with Data at Industry Scale

Competencies proved: Influencing with data, Technical expertise to advise, Stakeholder management

Situation. The Cost-Adaptive Routing with Specialist Models project (March 2026, targeting EMNLP 2026) addresses a specific inefficiency in LLM deployment: even after routing between strong and weak general models, many production workflows repeat the same task types daily (e.g., a company always classifies customer emails, always translates product titles into the same five languages, always generates summaries of the same document type). For these repeated workflows, the routing target itself can be replaced by a small specialist model fine-tuned on trajectories from the strong model — creating a cost reduction that compounds over time as usage data accrues. The challenge was that the collaborating industry partner (in this case connecting to the Tencent LLM-for-optimization context and additional contacts) had an intuitive scepticism: "fine-tuning a small model on synthetic trajectories from a large model is just distillation — we already do that." They were not wrong that the mechanism was related to distillation, and their pushback was technically legitimate.

Task. Demonstrate empirically that cost-adaptive routing with specialists is meaningfully different from — and superior to — static distillation, and use that data to redirect the collaboration toward the more general framework.

Action. I ran a controlled experiment comparing three approaches on a set of repeated-workflow tasks: (1) static distillation (fine-tune a small model on large-model outputs once, then deploy); (2) the proposed cost-adaptive routing (start with general routing, collect real deployment trajectories, fine-tune a specialist, re-evaluate routing targets as the specialist improves, repeat); (3) a cost-efficiency baseline of always using the large model. The key metric was cost-at-parity: at what point does each approach reach the same performance level as the large model, and what is the cumulative API cost to get there? The result was clear: static distillation required a large upfront data collection cost (sampling the large model heavily before training), and the specialist plateaued at slightly below large-model performance because the fixed training set did not capture the long tail of task variation. Cost-adaptive routing with specialists started cheaper (uses the general router immediately), converged to comparable performance, and its cost dropped continuously as the specialist was updated on real deployment data — including the long-tail edge cases that a static distillation set misses. I presented this as a cost-efficiency curve over time (a simple plot with three lines), not a table of numbers. The visual made the argument immediately clear: the adaptive approach was better in every direction except the first two days of deployment, where static distillation (if you had a large pre-existing dataset) could be competitive. That exception was exactly the condition under which the industry partner had been applying distillation — which is why their prior experience had been positive. Once they saw the curve, their question became "how do we build this into our deployment pipeline?" rather than "why is this different from distillation?"

Result. The collaboration redirected successfully. The paper is targeting EMNLP 2026. The cost-efficiency curve visualisation is now part of the paper's main contribution framing. The industry partner has committed to providing real deployment trajectory data for an extended evaluation — which strengthens the paper significantly.

What I would do differently. I would have front-loaded the experiment comparing against distillation from day one, before scoping the project. It would have strengthened the initial proposal to the collaborator rather than being something I had to prove mid-stream.

Connection to eBay. eBay serves millions of product listings, translations, and customer interactions daily. Many of these are highly repetitive (translate a product title from English to German for a specific category of electronics). The cost-adaptive specialist model concept maps directly to eBay's multilingual machine translation and LiLiuM inference budget. This is also a story that directly connects to Yannick Versley's pretraining and serving concerns — the cost reduction compounds exactly when the deployment volume is high, which is eBay's situation.
