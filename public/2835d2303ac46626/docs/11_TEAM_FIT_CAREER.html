Stage-3 Team-Fit & Career Conversation Prep — eBay ML Engineer (Amsterdam)

Priority: This document is for Stage 3 (team-fit / career conversation) but several sections are also useful for the Stage 1 HM screen opener and behavioral components. Read Sections 1–2 before every interview as a refresher; use Sections 6–7 actively in the team-fit round. Section 8 is a hard rule, not advice.

Section 1 — Tony's Career Narrative: The 2–3 Minute Story Arc

The goal is a crisp, coherent arc — not a CV recitation. The narrative should feel like a thesis with evidence, not a timeline. Practice it until it takes under 2.5 minutes and feels conversational.

The Core Arc (structure)

EE fundamentals → CS systems → research depth → the "systems race" thesis → why industry now, why eBay

Draft Script (memorise the shape, not the words)

"I started in electrical engineering — hardware, signals, embedded systems at NXP — because I wanted to understand how computation works at the physical layer. That gave me a strong intuition for resource constraints: when something is slow or expensive, it matters. During my MSc in Computer Systems Engineering at TU Delft I moved into ML, first as a research assistant on eye-tracking and emotion recognition in VR, then as an AI Research Engineer in Imaging Physics where I pruned over 80% of a trained nnU-Net's weights with minimal segmentation-quality loss. That was formative: it was the first time I saw clearly that the interesting engineering problem is not just 'can we train a bigger model' but 'what is the minimum model that is good enough, and how do we get there.'

That instinct deepened when I became a Marie Skłodowska-Curie Fellow at IMDEA Networks in Madrid, working on privacy-preserving wireless sensing — a domain where you have no public data, you cannot collect data openly, and you have to build representation learning and world models that generalise from almost nothing. That experience made me care intensely about data-constrained, resource-constrained settings.

Then I took an unusual step: I left the fellowship to pursue what I actually found most interesting, which was the question of LLM deployment at real cost and scale. I spent the last year as an independent researcher — collaborating with Tencent, Gradient Networks, McGill, Tsinghua — and produced three papers on LLM routing (one under review at NeurIPS), a method for generating future model weights with a diffusion model that delivers a 3.2× training speedup, and a VLM trained on synthetic customer-intent data for AI-assisted sales.

The thesis behind all of it is: AI has moved from a scaling race to a systems race. The frontier models are extraordinarily capable, but what separates production winners from lab demos is how you train efficiently, route intelligently, compress appropriately, and deploy under real constraints. That is where I want to build my career — and eBay's work on LiLiuM, e-Llama, and domain-adapted VLMs is exactly the kind of applied-but-rigorous systems work I want to be part of."

Key Beats to Hit (regardless of exact phrasing)

MSc TU Delft → pruning paper First concrete evidence of efficiency work; connects to eBay's vocab/inference efficiency work

The "Left a PhD Fellowship" Framing

Do not be defensive about leaving MSCA. The right frame is:

"The fellowship was a rare opportunity and I am proud of the work — but I found myself more energised by the questions of how to deploy and optimise models in real systems than by academic publication timelines. I wanted to work at the speed of the field, not the speed of a grant cycle. Leaving was a calculated bet on what I wanted to become."

This resonates with Versley, who himself made the academic-to-industry transition multiple times (Heidelberg visiting professor → Amazon → eBay).

Section 2 — Motivation, Values, and the 5-Year Arc

What Tony Actually Wants Next

Be concrete and honest — interviewers recognise rehearsed platitudes. Tony's genuine motivations, framed as answers:

Short-term (12–24 months): Join a team that is building LLMs — pretraining, domain adaptation, or efficient serving — for a real product at scale. Want to be a contributing researcher who is also close enough to production to see the feedback loop. Not looking for a pure academic-publishing role or a pure MLOps role.

Medium-term (2–5 years): Grow into a technical lead role — the kind of person who can shape research direction, mentor newer researchers, and have an opinion on what the team should build next. Role model: Yannick Versley's trajectory (deep technical plus team leadership). Also want to keep publishing — not for the prestige, but because publishing forces rigour.

Longer-term: Want to have built something that durably affects a product millions of people use, and to have developed the judgment that comes from having been close to scale: data at petabyte-level, inference at millions of requests per day, model training that costs real money.

Why eBay Fits This Arc

Four genuine, specific reasons (not generic):

  1. The problems are genuinely hard and novel. LiLiuM — building 1B/7B/13B models 100% in-house on 3 trillion tokens with a custom tokenizer — is not "put GPT-4 on it." The e-Llama work (480 H100s, Megatron-LM, 1T tokens, systematic ablations on LR and data ratio) is the kind of serious engineering that builds real skills. Tony wants to work on problems at this level of difficulty.

  2. The domain creates uniquely interesting NLP challenges. E-commerce product titles are ambiguous, multilingual, short (avg 11 words), terminology-dense, and high-stakes (translation errors cost GMV). Machine translation for cross-border trade is a core revenue function, not a side project. The RAG-for-MT work (chrF gains up to +15.3% for EN-PL), the custom tokenizer (34% generation speedup), and the multilingual evaluation benchmarks are all examples of problems that only exist at the intersection of e-commerce scale and linguistic diversity.

  3. The team publishes at top venues while shipping real products. ACL, EMNLP, EACL Industry, NeurIPS, SIGIR — this is a team that takes research seriously. But Magical Listing has 500M listings created and a 50%+ new listing rate increase. That combination — publish AND ship — is exactly what Tony wants.

  4. Amsterdam is where Tony already lives. Not a trivial point: Tony is already integrated, already has an NL network, has no relocation friction. Hybrid 3 days/week in a central Amsterdam office is immediately workable.

Section 3 — Working Style, Collaboration, and What Tony Wants in a Manager and Team

Tony's Working Style

How to describe it honestly:

"I am most effective when I understand the end-to-end system — from why the data looks the way it does, to what the model does, to how it is evaluated, to how it is served. I tend to go deep before going broad: I'd rather understand one paper or one system thoroughly than skim ten. But I also move fast when I have that foundation — I built five research projects in the last 15 months while collaborating with four external organisations.

I am AI-native in the sense that I use tools like Claude Code and Codex as force multipliers — but I was trained to write code, debug systems, and do math on a whiteboard before those tools existed, and I do not depend on them for understanding.

I work well across timezones (Tencent, McGill) and across disciplines (my collaborators have included physicists, clinicians, and social scientists). I am comfortable with ambiguity — independent research requires defining your own problem and own evaluation — but I also know when I need to ask for guidance to avoid going in circles."

What Tony Wants in a Manager

Frame this as genuine preferences, not flattery targeted at Versley:

"I do my best work for managers who have technical depth — not because I need constant technical direction, but because I want my ideas to be challenged at the right level. If I say 'we should use a lower learning rate for continued pretraining,' I want a manager who can ask 'why 10%, not 5%? Did you ablate?' rather than just nodding. I also value managers who shield their team from random organisational churn so that researchers can think in long enough cycles to actually complete something. And I appreciate direct feedback — I would rather know quickly if I am going in the wrong direction than find out six months later."

Why this maps to Versley: He does exactly this. His papers include systematic ablations on every hyperparameter. He came from academia where rigour is the baseline. He is a TL Manager who still co-authors papers — meaning he stays close enough to the work to be a real technical peer.

What Tony Wants in a Team

"I want a team where people disagree on technical grounds, not on hierarchy or politics. I learn most from colleagues who know something I do not — which in this team means colleagues with deep pretraining experience (which I have less of) and strong multilingual NLP background (which Versley and his team clearly have). I also want to be in a team that ships — pure research groups where I cannot see the impact of my work on a real product are not where I do my best work."

Connection to Amsterdam Applied Research culture: The eBay Amsterdam team sits at exactly this intersection — publishing at ACL/EACL/SIGIR while shipping Magical Listing and e-Llama. The eBay-UvA collaboration (Cees Snoek as co-author on the VLM paper) suggests the team is also plugged into the academic Amsterdam ecosystem, which is an additional draw.

Honest Growth Areas (how to frame them)

The key is to be specific, genuine, and show active learning — not to present weaknesses as secret strengths.

1. Pretraining at industrial scale (honest gap) "My research has been at academic-lab scale — I have trained models with up to a few billion parameters on single or a few multi-GPU setups, but I have not run Megatron-LM on 480 H100s or managed a training run that costs hundreds of thousands of GPU-hours. I understand the architecture of 3D parallelism and mixed-precision training conceptually, but I have not yet owned one of those runs from start to finish. That is something I am actively seeking to learn — it is a big part of why I find the eBay LLM pretraining team interesting."

2. Classic production MLOps at internet scale "My work has been research-oriented — the production path for my models has been publication, not real-time serving to millions of users. I have done research-to-prototype work (the Human Intent World Model was deployed in a real sales context at MeetaVista) but not full production MLOps with canary releases, drift monitoring, and on-call rotation. This is a genuine gap I want to close."

3. Classical recommender systems and ranking (eBay's core business) "My background is more NLP/LLM/multimodal than recommendation systems or learning-to-rank. I understand the concepts (collaborative filtering, two-tower retrieval, NDCG), but I have not built or maintained a large-scale recsys pipeline. At eBay this would be a learning curve for me, especially on the ads-ranking side."

How to deliver these in an interview: Be specific, keep them short (one sentence per weakness), and pivot immediately to what you are doing about it or how you would tackle it.

Section 5 — Why eBay / Why This Team / Why Now

This is the most important section for final-round team-fit interviews. The answer must be specific, grounded in research, and not sound like it was written for any company.

Why eBay (not a competitor)

Three tiers of answer:

Tier 1 — The research is genuinely interesting "eBay is one of the few companies outside the pure frontier AI labs that is doing real LLM pretraining from scratch — LiLiuM is not a fine-tune of an existing model, it is a custom vocabulary, custom architecture, 3 trillion token training run that they own end-to-end. The reason is sensible: full data governance, licensing control, and the ability to optimise the tokenizer for e-commerce fertility. That 34% generation speedup from vocabulary design is exactly the kind of systems-level insight that I find more interesting than the headline benchmark number. Then the e-Llama work on top — systematic ablations on learning rate and data ratio to avoid catastrophic forgetting, model merging to trade off domain vs. general capability without extra training — this is serious engineering with serious rigour. The VLM work (arXiv:2602.11733, 2026) is building the right way: three-stage training, synthetic data at 4M scale, multi-image item intelligence. This is not a company that is just prompting ChatGPT."

Tier 2 — The domain creates distinctive problems "E-commerce NLP is genuinely hard in ways that general-domain NLP is not. Product titles are short, multilingual, terminology-dense, and directly connected to economic outcomes. Getting machine translation wrong is not a quality-of-life issue — it's a lost sale. The RAG-for-MT work showed +15% chrF for underserved language pairs. The custom tokenizer shows how domain vocabulary design directly translates to inference cost savings. I do not think I would learn these specific problems anywhere else at the scale eBay operates."

Tier 3 — The team combines publication culture with real deployment "I care about both — I want to publish rigorous work AND see it shipped. The Amsterdam team has ACL, EMNLP, EACL Industry, NeurIPS, SIGIR — meaningful venues, not just workshop papers. But Magical Listing has 500M total listings and a 50%+ increase in listing rate. Those two things rarely coexist at this quality level."

Why This Team (Versley's team specifically)

"Yannick leads a team that is doing the full stack of LLM development — pretraining (LiLiuM), domain adaptation (e-Llama), tokenizer design (vocabulary customization), and now VLMs (arXiv:2602.11733, 2026). That breadth means I would be exposed to the whole pipeline, not just one component. And the team has a collaboration with UvA (Cees Snoek is a co-author on the VLM paper) which connects to the broader Amsterdam research ecosystem. I am also genuinely interested in learning from Yannick — someone who went from rigorous academic NLP to building Amazon Nova and then brought that experience to eBay. That kind of trajectory is rare."

Note: Do not say this in a sycophantic way. The goal is to show you have done your homework and are making a genuine assessment, not to flatter.

Why Now

"I have spent the past 15 months as an independent researcher building a body of work — five projects, four collaborations, papers at ACL, NeurIPS, EMNLP (under review). That mode was valuable: it forced independence, breadth, and speed. But I am now ready to be part of a team — to work at a scale I cannot reach alone, to have colleagues who can challenge my thinking, and to see my work deployed to real users. The timing is right for me to make that transition, and eBay's current trajectory (Q1 2026 revenue +19.5% YoY, agentic search showing 50% more engagement, Magical Listing at 500M listings) means this is a team that is in an exciting phase, not a consolidation phase."

Section 6 — Likely Team-Fit Questions with Strong Answers

Q1: "Tell me about yourself" / "Walk me through your background"

Use Section 1 arc verbatim (practiced version). Keep it under 2.5 minutes. End with "...and that's what brings me to eBay — I'd love to hear more about where the team is headed."

Q2: "What excites you most about this role?"

Strong answer:

"Three things. First, the technical scope — being part of a team that does LLM pretraining from scratch, not just fine-tuning. I've been working on routing and efficiency, but I've been working at academic scale. Getting close to a real 480-H100 training run is something I cannot do anywhere else at my current stage.

Second, the specific domain problem. E-commerce NLP has unique constraints: short product titles, high multilingual diversity, high economic stakes per translation error. Your vocabulary customization work — the monotone efficiency guarantee for domain tokenizer extension — is the kind of problem I didn't even know existed until I read the paper. That surprised me, which is a good sign.

Third, the publication culture combined with real deployment. I published because I wanted rigour and external accountability. The Amsterdam team does both — ACL and Magical Listing in the same year. That's rare."

Q3: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

Strong answer (honest and specific):

"I want to be a senior technical contributor who is also starting to lead — setting research direction for a sub-area, mentoring one or two more junior researchers, and having the judgment that comes from having been close to real scale: production training runs, serving at millions of QPS, seeing A/B tests run on real users. The role model I am drawn to is someone like Yannick's trajectory: deep technical plus people leadership, staying close enough to the work to co-author papers while also building a team.

Concretely: in 2–3 years I want to have shipped something measurable — a model or system that is live in a product, with a metric I can point to. In 5 years I want to be making real technical bets, not just executing on others' bets."

Q4: "How do you handle disagreement with a colleague or manager about a technical direction?"

STAR story — use Diffusion Weights:

Situation/Task: "In my Diffusion Weights project, a collaborator argued strongly that we should use a standard checkpoint-averaging approach as the baseline, which they argued was sufficient. I disagreed — I thought the more interesting and scientifically honest comparison was against full fine-tuning with the same compute budget, because checkpoint averaging is not a real alternative to fine-tuning.

Action: Rather than just asserting my position, I proposed we run a quick ablation — same model, same dataset (MMLU), three conditions: (1) our diffusion-weight method, (2) checkpoint averaging, (3) full fine-tuning at matched compute. I did the run over a weekend.

Result: The results showed that checkpoint averaging actually was competitive with full fine-tuning at a fraction of compute, but our diffusion-weight method was better than both. The data resolved the disagreement cleanly. We included all three baselines in the paper, which made the contribution more credible.

Principle: I default to 'let's run the experiment' when the cost of the experiment is low and the disagreement is about an empirical claim. If the cost is high, I try to find the minimum test that could falsify one position."

Q5: "Tell me about a time you received feedback that was hard to hear."

STAR story:

"After submitting a draft of the LLM Router paper for workshop review, a reviewer gave direct feedback that our routing evaluation was circular: we were selecting the routing threshold based on the same test set we were evaluating on. Hearing it was uncomfortable because we had been careful — but when I re-read the experimental section, I could see the issue. The train/val/test split was correct but the threshold selection procedure was not clearly separated.

I went back, restructured the evaluation to use a held-out val set for threshold selection and a clean test set for reporting, re-ran the numbers. The performance numbers were slightly lower — which was the honest result. We updated the paper. The lesson was that rigor in evaluation design is not something you can retrospectively paper over; you have to build it into the protocol from the start. I now treat evaluation design as requiring a review pass before any experiment runs."

Q6: "What do you find challenging about hybrid working, and how do you manage it?"

Answer (honest, brief):

"The main challenge in hybrid is that the informal knowledge-transfer moments — the hallway conversations where you pick up context about why something is designed the way it is — are harder to reproduce. I have managed it in my independent work by being more deliberate: async writing (short Slack/email summaries of decisions rather than relying on sync meetings), and over-communicating progress weekly even when there is not a forcing function.

In a team setting I would want to be in the office on the days when the team is making decisions or doing design reviews — not just showing up on arbitrary fixed days. Three days a week in central Amsterdam is very workable for me; I already live here."

Q7: "What do you do to stay current with the field?"

Answer (specific, not generic):

"A few things. I read arXiv daily but selectively — I follow specific author clusters (Tri Dao's efficiency work, Tim Dettmers' quantization work, the LLaMA/Qwen teams, eBay's own CoreAI output) rather than trying to read everything. I built ScholarHighlights, a Chrome extension that surfaces venue quality and author credibility on Google Scholar pages, partly because I wanted a faster way to triage what is worth reading.

For papers I want to understand deeply — the ones closest to my work — I try to reproduce the core result in code. Not always the full experiment, but the key mechanism. For Chinchilla, for example, I implemented the scaling law fit; for the e-Llama paper, I mapped out the ablation table by hand to make sure I understood why the 10% LR choice was made.

I also talk to people. My collaboration network (Tencent, McGill, Tsinghua) means I hear about work before it is public, which is increasingly important in a field that moves faster than the review cycle."

Q8: "What is your biggest professional regret?"

Answer (genuine, frames growth):

"Not engaging more with production systems earlier. I spent the first few years of my ML career in pure research environments — lab, fellowship, independent — and I learned to think rigorously, but I did not learn the feedback loop of deploying something and watching real users behave differently than your evaluation predicted.

The Human Intent World Model project at MeetaVista was the first time I saw real sales interactions change based on a model I built. The gap between lab accuracy and real-world utility was humbling and instructive. I wish I had encountered that gap earlier — it would have made me design experiments differently from the start. Part of why I want to join a team like eBay is to close that gap properly."

Q9: "How do you prioritise when you have more research ideas than time?"

Answer:

"I ask two questions for each idea: (1) What would I learn that I do not already know? — this separates ideas that are mostly execution from ideas that genuinely advance my understanding. (2) What is the smallest version of this experiment that could falsify the core hypothesis? — this forces me to find the minimum viable experiment, which usually reveals whether an idea is tractable in weeks or months.

In practice I maintain a ranked backlog of about 10–15 ideas in a note, with a short note on why each one stalled or advanced. Most ideas die in the first experiment — and that is fine. The LLM Router started as a smaller routing benchmark and evolved into a paper only after the first experiment showed the cost savings were unexpectedly large (>90% cost reduction with matched performance). If the first experiment had shown 15% savings, I would have deprioritised it."

Q10: "Tell me about a time you worked with a difficult stakeholder."

STAR story — MeetaVista VLM project:

"In the Human Intent World Model project with MeetaVista, the business stakeholder had a strong prior that the model should 'mimic how a salesperson thinks' — and kept asking us to add more rule-based prompting to force the model toward specific sales scripts. From a research perspective, this was at odds with our goal of building a model that generalises to novel customer interactions.

I handled it by first understanding their concern: they had real sales scripts that performed well in A/B tests, and they were worried about deployment risk if the model behaved unpredictably. Once I understood that, the conversation changed. I proposed a two-stage approach: a model backbone trained to infer customer intent from visual cues, with a thin instruction layer on top that could be adjusted without retraining. The stakeholder could configure the instruction layer to match their sales scripts, while we preserved the generalizable intent-inference backbone.

The result was a system they were willing to deploy. The lesson was that a technical disagreement often has a stakeholder concern underneath it that is legitimate — and finding that concern is faster than winning the argument."

Section 7 — Thoughtful Questions to Ask the Team-Fit Interviewers

Have 4–6 questions prepared. Use 2–3 per interview. Vary them across rounds — do not repeat the same questions to different interviewers. Mark which you have used.

Questions About the Team and Work

Q-A: "The VLM paper (arXiv:2602.11733, 2026) tested seven different text decoder variants — LiLiuM, e-Llama, Qwen3, Gemma3 — and found that domain-adapted text decoders gave 5–12% gains on e-commerce tasks. Is the team now converging on a standard backbone for VLM work, or is the decoder choice still treated as a hyperparameter depending on the task?" Why good: Shows you read the paper in detail; asks a genuine open research question.

Q-B: "The vocabulary customization paper showed a monotone efficiency guarantee for extending a tokenizer without degrading fertility. Has this been deployed in the production serving stack, and has the latency reduction been as large as the token compression suggests?" Why good: Bridges research to production; shows you care about the systems path, not just the paper.

Q-C: "For the e-Llama continued pretraining — you found that 10% of original LR worked best. How did you decide where to start that search? Was there a theoretical prior, or was it purely empirical?" Why good: Exactly the kind of design-choice probe Versley himself uses; shows you read the ablations.

Q-D: "How does the team decide what to publish externally vs. keep proprietary? I imagine some of the most valuable parts — the proprietary data, the specific e-commerce benchmarks, the production tokenizer — cannot be fully shared." Why good: Shows maturity about applied research in an industry context; opens conversation about IP culture.

Questions About Growth and Team Culture

Q-E: "What does the learning curve look like for a new hire joining this team? Is there a defined project to own in the first 6 months, or is it more exploratory?" Why good: Shows you are thinking about how to contribute quickly, not just about what you would receive.

Q-F: "How closely does the research team work with the product and engineering teams on something like Magical Listing? Is it a hand-off relationship or more continuous collaboration?" Why good: Shows interest in the research-to-production loop, which you genuinely care about.

Q-G: "What is the hardest unsolved problem on the team right now — the thing that keeps coming up in research meetings where you do not yet have a good answer?" Why good: This is a high-trust question that invites the interviewer to speak honestly; it signals confidence and genuine curiosity.

Questions to Ask Versley Specifically

Q-Versley-1: "You led pretraining at Amazon AGI on Nova and now at eBay on LiLiuM and e-Llama. What surprised you most about the differences between building foundation models at AWS scale vs. at eBay scale — not just compute, but the data and evaluation challenges?" Why good: Asks about his experience specifically; invites a genuine answer; shows you know his background.

Q-Versley-2: "The e-Llama work from your team did systematic ablations on LR, data ratio, and context size. Were there other hyperparameters you ablated but didn't report because the effect was small, or did those three turn out to genuinely dominate?" Why good: Shows you read the paper deeply and are genuinely curious about the research process, not just the published results.

Section 8 — Compensation: Hard Rule

Do not discuss specific numbers in technical or team-fit interviews.

If salary or compensation comes up in any technical or team-fit interview round, the correct response is:

"I'm happy to discuss that — I think I should have that conversation with the recruiter so we can talk through the full picture including base, bonus, and equity. I don't want to take time away from the technical conversation here."

That's it. Do not anchor. Do not say a number. Do not apologise for redirecting. The recruiter is the right channel for compensation negotiation, not a hiring manager or team-fit interviewer.

Background context (not to discuss in interviews): - Amsterdam Senior MLE range: ~€76.5k (25th pct) to €120k (75th pct); 90th pct ~€133k. eBay typically adds bonus + RSUs on top of base. - Tony's anchor: ~€80–100k base is a reasonable starting point for a mid/senior individual contributor with exceptional research output but limited production experience. - The 30% ruling (NL expat tax benefit) may reduce net tax burden significantly — confirm eligibility with recruiter/tax advisor separately. - Negotiate after offer, not before.
