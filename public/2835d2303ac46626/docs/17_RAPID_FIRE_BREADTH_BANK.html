RAPID-FIRE BREADTH BANK

85 short Q&A for the ~15-min breadth sweep in Stage 1 with Yannick Versley. Each answer is under 45 seconds to deliver. ★ = 20 highest-probability items based on Versley's pretraining/VLM/NLP profile and the eBay team's published work.

1. ML BASICS

★ Q1. What is the bias-variance tradeoff? Error = Bias² + Variance + irreducible noise. High bias = model too simple, underfits. High variance = model memorizes training set. The tradeoff means reducing one typically increases the other; regularization and more data help variance; richer models help bias.

Q2. What does L1 regularization do differently from L2? L1 (Lasso) drives weights to exactly zero; L2 (Ridge) shrinks them evenly. L1 induces sparsity — useful for feature selection. L2 penalizes large weights smoothly. In practice, L1 is piecewise-linear around zero so the subgradient pushes weights all the way off; L2's gradient is proportional to weight size.

★ Q3. When would you use dropout, and what does it actually do? Dropout randomly zeros units during training at rate p, forcing the network to learn redundant representations. At inference, weights are scaled by (1−p) — or equivalently, inverted dropout scales during training. It approximates an ensemble of exponentially many thinned networks. Most useful in overparameterized regimes; less used inside attention layers (where it can disrupt sharp attention patterns).

Q4. What is early stopping? Stop training when validation loss stops improving, using the best checkpoint. It implicitly bounds model complexity by limiting optimization steps — a form of regularization. Requires holding out a validation set and a patience hyperparameter.

★ Q5. Precision, recall, and F1 — define each and when to prefer which. Precision = TP/(TP+FP); recall = TP/(TP+FN); F1 = harmonic mean of both. Prefer precision when false positives are costly (spam filter); prefer recall when false negatives are costly (cancer screening). F1 balances both and is standard for imbalanced NLP tasks.

★ Q6. ROC-AUC vs PR-AUC — when does each mislead? ROC-AUC measures ranking quality over all thresholds; PR-AUC focuses on the positive class. ROC-AUC is optimistic under heavy class imbalance because it includes true negatives in the denominator (specificity). PR-AUC is the right metric when positives are rare — e.g., fraud detection, entity extraction in long documents. Versley's NLP work would care about PR-AUC.

Q7. Signs of overfitting? Low training loss, high validation loss, and a growing train-val gap. Also: validation metrics plateau or degrade while training metrics keep improving; model is sensitive to random seeds; test performance far below train. Remedies: more data, regularization, simpler architecture, dropout, early stopping.

Q8. What is k-fold cross-validation? Split data into k equally sized folds; train on k−1 folds, validate on the held-out fold; repeat k times and average metrics. Reduces variance of the performance estimate vs. a single train-val split. Expensive for large models — common for classical ML, less common for large LLMs (holdout + ablations used instead).

Q9. Generative vs discriminative models — what's the difference? Discriminative models learn P(y|x) directly; generative models learn P(x,y) or P(x|y). Examples: logistic regression and BERT are discriminative; naive Bayes, VAEs, and GPT (which models P(x)) are generative. Generative models can synthesize new samples and handle missing data; discriminative models often have higher accuracy on the classification task itself.

2. PROBABILITY & STATS

★ Q10. State Bayes' rule and give an NLP application. P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / P(E). In NLP: given observed words, the posterior over a label (spam/not-spam) is prior × likelihood of words given that label, normalized. Naive Bayes extends this by assuming feature independence. Bayesian updates are also how LLMs implicitly update "beliefs" about context.

Q11. MLE vs MAP — what is the difference? MLE maximizes P(data|θ); MAP maximizes P(θ|data) = P(data|θ)·P(θ). MAP adds a prior over parameters — equivalent to L2 regularization (Gaussian prior) or L1 (Laplace prior). As dataset size grows, MAP converges to MLE because the likelihood dominates the prior.

Q12. Central Limit Theorem — state it in one sentence. The sample mean of n i.i.d. draws from ANY distribution with finite variance converges in distribution to a Gaussian as n→∞, regardless of the original distribution. This justifies using z-tests and normal approximations in significance testing for NLP evaluation.

Q13. What is a p-value? The probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the one obtained, ASSUMING the null hypothesis is true. It is NOT the probability the null is true. A p-value < 0.05 is a common (but arbitrary) threshold for rejecting the null. In NLP evaluation, bootstrap resampling CIs are often preferred over p-values.

Q14. Define expectation and variance. E[X] = Σ x·P(X=x); Var(X) = E[(X−E[X])²] = E[X²] − (E[X])². Variance measures spread; standard deviation = √Var(X). For a sum of independent variables, variances ADD; standard deviations do not.

Q15. What is covariance and how does it relate to correlation? Cov(X,Y) = E[(X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y)] — how much X and Y move together. Correlation = Cov(X,Y)/(σ_X·σ_Y), normalized to [−1, 1]. Zero covariance does not imply independence (only for jointly Gaussian variables does it).

3. OPTIMIZATION

★ Q16. SGD vs Adam — core difference. SGD updates θ ← θ − η·g; Adam maintains per-parameter exponential moving averages of gradients (m) and squared gradients (v) and divides the update by √v + ε. Adam adapts the effective learning rate per parameter and converges faster, but can generalize worse than well-tuned SGD on some tasks. Adam is standard for LLM pretraining; SGD+momentum for vision.

Q17. What is momentum in optimization? Momentum accumulates a velocity vector in the gradient direction, damping oscillations across dimensions and accelerating progress in low-curvature directions. Update: v ← β·v + g; θ ← θ − η·v. β=0.9 is typical. Helps escape flat regions and sharp minima.

★ Q18. What is learning-rate warmup and why is it used? LR starts near zero and ramps up over the first N steps before following a decay schedule. Early in training, moment estimates in Adam are unreliable (bias not fully corrected) and weight initialization is random — large updates can destroy the loss basin before the optimizer has stabilized. Warmup prevents this. In pretraining (WSD schedule), warmup → stable phase → decay.

Q19. What does batch size affect? Larger batch = more stable gradient estimates but fewer updates per epoch; also flatter loss basin → sometimes worse generalization. The linear scaling rule says double batch size → double LR (heuristic). Larger batches are communication-efficient on multi-GPU but can require gradient accumulation to fit memory.

Q20. Why do we clip gradients? Gradient clipping caps the norm of the gradient at a threshold before applying the update, preventing exploding gradients from destabilizing training. Critical in RNNs and transformers processing long sequences. Global norm clipping (clip total norm across all parameters) is more common than per-parameter clipping.

4. DEEP LEARNING

★ Q21. How does backpropagation work? Backprop applies the chain rule to compute gradients of the loss w.r.t. every parameter by propagating the error signal from output to input. Forward pass computes activations; backward pass computes ∂L/∂W for each layer using cached activations. Implemented via autograd (reverse-mode AD) in PyTorch.

Q22. Vanishing and exploding gradients — what causes them and how do we fix them? Vanishing: repeated multiplication of values < 1 makes gradients exponentially small in deep/recurrent networks. Exploding: values > 1 make them exponentially large. Fixes: residual connections (skip gradients), normalization layers, proper weight initialization (Xavier/He), gradient clipping, and architectures that avoid deep sequential chains (e.g., transformers vs RNNs).

★ Q23. BatchNorm vs LayerNorm — when do you use which? BatchNorm normalizes across the batch dimension (mean/variance from batch statistics); LayerNorm normalizes across the feature dimension within each sample. BatchNorm works well for CNNs/images where batch statistics are stable; LayerNorm is preferred for transformers and NLP because sequence lengths vary and batch sizes can be small. LayerNorm has no dependency on other samples in the batch — safe for autoregressive generation.

Q24. What does dropout do at inference? Dropout is disabled at inference; all units are active. With standard dropout, weights are scaled by (1−p) at inference, OR equivalently, activations are divided by (1−p) during training (inverted dropout, used in PyTorch). Failing to disable dropout at test time is a common bug.

Q25. Why do residual connections help? They create a direct gradient highway from loss to early layers, mitigating vanishing gradients and allowing the network to learn identity functions easily. Also: they let each block learn a small residual correction Δ rather than a full transformation — much easier optimization. Critical for training deep transformers.

Q26. Xavier vs He initialization — when to use each? Xavier (Glorot): Var(W) = 2/(fan_in + fan_out) — designed for tanh/sigmoid activations. He: Var(W) = 2/fan_in — designed for ReLU (accounts for the half of inputs zeroed out). Using the wrong init can cause activations to vanish or explode before training starts. Modern transformers often use scaled init variants.

5. TRANSFORMERS

★ Q27. Explain self-attention in one sentence. Self-attention computes a weighted sum of value vectors, where weights are softmax-normalized dot products between each query and all keys, allowing every position to attend to every other position. This gives O(n²) complexity in sequence length but full pairwise dependency modeling.

★ Q28. What are Q, K, V in attention? Query (Q), Key (K), Value (V) are linear projections of the input: Q and K compute compatibility scores (dot product → softmax → weights); V carries the information that is aggregated. Conceptually: Q is "what am I looking for"; K is "what do I have"; V is "what I return if matched."

Q29. What is multi-head attention? Run h parallel attention heads with different Q/K/V projections, concatenate outputs, project back to model dimension. Each head can attend to different positional or semantic patterns. Multi-head cost ≈ single-head cost when d_head = d_model/h (same total computation).

★ Q30. Scaled dot-product attention — why scale by √d_k? Without scaling, dot products grow in magnitude with d_k, pushing softmax into saturation (near-zero gradients). Dividing by √d_k keeps variance of dot products ~1, making softmax gradients healthy. Derived from assuming Q, K have unit-variance entries: Var(Q·K^T) = d_k.

Q31. What is a causal mask in a decoder? A mask that sets attention weights to −∞ (pre-softmax) for all future positions, ensuring position i can only attend to positions ≤ i. Enforces autoregressive generation: each token prediction only sees past tokens. Implemented as an upper-triangular mask.

★ Q32. Positional encodings — why does a transformer need them? Self-attention is permutation-equivariant — without positional information, the model cannot distinguish word order. Sinusoidal encodings (Vaswani et al.) are added to embeddings; learned absolute encodings are an alternative. Modern LLMs use relative encodings like RoPE instead.

★ Q33. What is RoPE in one line? RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) encodes position by rotating the Q and K vectors by an angle proportional to their absolute position, so the dot product Q_i·K_j depends only on the RELATIVE position (i−j). No extra parameters; enables length generalization better than learned absolute encodings.

★ Q34. What is a KV cache and why does it matter? During autoregressive decoding, the K and V tensors for past tokens are cached so they don't need to be recomputed at each step — reducing per-token compute from O(n) to O(1). Without KV cache, generation cost scales quadratically with sequence length. KV cache is the dominant memory cost at inference time for long contexts.

★ Q35. GQA vs MQA vs MHA — what are they? MHA: each head has its own Q, K, V. MQA (Multi-Query): all heads share one K and one V (reduces KV cache size dramatically). GQA (Grouped-Query): G groups of heads share K/V — a middle ground. GQA (used in LLaMA-3, Gemma) reduces memory bandwidth by ~8× vs MHA while losing less quality than MQA. Critical for long-context inference throughput.

Q36. What is SwiGLU? SwiGLU = Swish(xW_1) ⊗ (xW_2), where ⊗ is elementwise multiply — a gated linear unit with a smooth nonlinearity. Replaces the standard FFN (ReLU/GELU) in modern LLMs (LLaMA, PaLM). Uses 3 weight matrices instead of 2 (one hidden dimension is 2/3 of the standard to keep parameter count equal). Empirically outperforms GELU FFNs.

Q37. What is RMSNorm? RMSNorm normalizes by the root-mean-square of activations (no mean subtraction, no bias): x / RMS(x) · γ. Simpler and faster than LayerNorm, no recentering step. Used in LLaMA family. Empirically similar performance to LayerNorm with slightly lower compute.

Q38. What is weight tying in LLMs? The input embedding matrix and the output (lm_head) projection matrix share the same weights. Motivation: both map between token IDs and the same semantic embedding space. Reduces parameter count by ~vocab_size × d_model (tens or hundreds of millions). Used in GPT-2, LLaMA, and many others.

6. LLM TRAINING

★ Q39. What is the difference between pretraining, SFT, and RLHF? Pretraining: next-token prediction on trillions of tokens to learn world knowledge and language modeling. SFT: supervised fine-tuning on curated (instruction, response) pairs to teach the model to follow instructions. RLHF: trains a reward model from human preferences, then uses RL (PPO) to optimize the LLM to maximize reward. Each stage targets a different capability: knowledge, instruction following, human alignment.

Q40. Is instruction tuning the same as SFT? Yes — instruction tuning IS SFT applied to instruction-following examples. The term "instruction tuning" emphasizes the format of data (prompt-response pairs with instructions); "SFT" is the training method. InstructGPT, Alpaca, and LLaMA-instruct all use SFT on instruction-formatted data. They are the same thing with different branding.

★ Q41. What do Chinchilla scaling laws say? For a fixed compute budget, optimally train a model with ~20 tokens per parameter. Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al. 2022) showed that prior models (GPT-3, Gopher) were undertrained relative to their size — you should scale data and model size jointly. A 70B model trained Chinchilla-optimally needs ~1.4T tokens.

Q42. What is perplexity? Perplexity = exp(cross-entropy loss) — the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood per token. Intuitively: the effective number of equally likely next-token choices the model sees. Lower is better. It is corpus-specific and not directly comparable across tokenizers.

Q43. What is cross-entropy loss for LLM training? L = −(1/N) Σ log P(token_i | token_{1..i−1}). Average negative log-probability of the correct next token under the model's distribution. Minimizing cross-entropy = maximizing likelihood = making the model assign high probability to training tokens.

Q44. What is gradient accumulation? Accumulate gradients over multiple mini-batches before applying one optimizer step, simulating a larger effective batch size without requiring more GPU memory. Used when the desired batch size doesn't fit in GPU memory. The accumulated gradient is the average (or sum, depending on convention) over the micro-batches.

★ Q45. What is activation checkpointing? During the forward pass, discard intermediate activations; recompute them during the backward pass rather than storing them. Trades compute (~33% extra) for a dramatic reduction in activation memory (proportional to model depth). Essential for training large models where activation memory is the bottleneck.

Q46. What is mixed precision training (bf16)? Parameters are stored in float32 but forward/backward passes run in bf16 (or fp16), reducing memory and increasing throughput on modern hardware. bf16 has the same exponent range as fp32 (crucial for training stability, avoids inf/nan) but less precision. fp16 has more precision but narrower exponent range → more overflow risk. Modern LLM pretraining uses bf16 almost exclusively.

★ Q47. FSDP/ZeRO vs DDP — what's the difference? DDP (Distributed Data Parallel): each GPU holds a full model replica; gradients are allreduced across GPUs. ZeRO/FSDP: shards optimizer states, gradients, and parameters across GPUs — each GPU holds only a shard. ZeRO-3/FSDP allows training models whose total state exceeds the memory of a single GPU. DDP is simpler and faster for models that fit; FSDP is necessary for 70B+ models.

Q48. What is a Mixture of Experts (MoE)? MoE replaces the dense FFN with N expert FFNs; a router selects top-k experts per token. Active parameters per token = k/N × total, so inference cost scales with active params not total. Training cost is higher (more parameters to update); load balancing across experts is a challenge. Used in Mixtral, DeepSeek-V2/V3, GPT-4 (rumored).

7. DECODING / INFERENCE

Q49. What does temperature do in LLM sampling? Temperature T scales logits before softmax: logits → logits/T. T < 1 sharpens the distribution (more deterministic, picks high-probability tokens). T > 1 flattens it (more diverse/random). T=0 is greedy; T=1 is standard sampling. Calibrated temperature is important for downstream tasks.

Q50. What is top-k sampling? Sample only from the k highest-probability tokens at each step, zeroing out the rest. Limits the "long tail" of low-probability nonsense tokens. Fixed k is a crude threshold — if k=50 but the distribution is highly peaked, you still sample from 49 poor options.

★ Q51. What is top-p (nucleus) sampling? Sample from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability ≥ p (nucleus). Adapts dynamically: when the model is confident, the nucleus is small (few tokens); when uncertain, more tokens enter. Typically p=0.9–0.95. Fixes the fixed-k problem. Introduced by Holtzman et al. (2019).

Q52. When does beam search fail? Beam search can produce degenerate, repetitive text and does not approximate MAP decoding well for long sequences. Because it maximizes joint probability, it prefers short, high-frequency phrases. Neural text degeneration (Holtzman et al.) showed beam output quality often degrades vs. sampling for open-ended generation. Best for constrained tasks (translation, summarization with length targets).

Q53. What is speculative decoding? A small "draft" model generates k tokens quickly; the large "verifier" model processes them in parallel (one forward pass) and accepts/rejects. If the draft is mostly correct, you get k tokens for ~1 forward pass of the big model. Achieves same output distribution as the target model (lossless) with 2–3× throughput gain. Requires a compatible draft model.

★ Q54. What is continuous batching? Instead of padding all sequences in a batch to the same length (static batching), continuously batch requests by filling in finished-sequence slots with new requests mid-batch. Dramatically improves GPU utilization for variable-length inference workloads — a key technique in production LLM serving (vLLM, TGI). Eliminates idle compute from padding.

8. ALIGNMENT

★ Q55. Explain RLHF at a high level. Collect human preference comparisons → train a reward model (RM) to score responses → use PPO to fine-tune the LLM to maximize RM score while staying close to the SFT baseline (KL penalty). The KL term prevents reward hacking by keeping the model close to the SFT distribution. InstructGPT (Ouyang et al. 2022) is the canonical paper.

Q56. What is DPO and how does it differ from PPO? DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) reparameterizes the RLHF objective so the optimal policy is directly expressed as a function of the reference model and the reward — no RL loop needed. Training directly on preference pairs with a classification loss. Simpler than PPO (no RM, no rollouts), competitive performance, lower variance. Can overfit to the preference dataset if it's small.

Q57. What is GRPO? Group Relative Policy Optimization — used in DeepSeek-R1: generates a GROUP of responses for each prompt, computes rewards, normalizes within the group (relative advantage), and updates with PPO-style clipping. Eliminates the need for a separate critic/value model (the group mean serves as a baseline). Efficient for reasoning RL fine-tuning.

Q58. What is RLVR? Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards — RL where the reward is computed from an external verifier (math checker, code executor) rather than a trained reward model. No reward hacking via a flawed RM; reward is ground-truth. Used in DeepSeek-R1, OpenAI o1-style training. Works when tasks have binary/graded verifiable answers.

Q59. What is reward hacking? The LLM learns to game the reward model — scoring highly on the RM without actually being better. Examples: repetitive sycophantic responses, specific formatting patterns the RM favors. Mitigated by: KL penalty, RM ensemble, iterative RM updates, or switching to verifiable rewards (RLVR).

Q60. What is Constitutional AI? Anthropic's method: the model is given a set of principles (a "constitution") and critiques/revises its own outputs to comply, generating preference data for RLHF without extensive human labeling. Red-teaming loop: model generates harmful outputs → model criticizes them → revised outputs become preference pairs. Reduces human annotation bottleneck for safety alignment.

Q61. PRM vs ORM — what's the difference? Process Reward Model (PRM): assigns a reward to each reasoning STEP. Outcome Reward Model (ORM): rewards only the final answer. PRM provides denser supervision for multi-step reasoning but is expensive to label. ORM is easier to obtain (just check final answer correctness). PRMs are used in verifier-based inference (selecting the best-of-N reasoning trace); ORMs for standard RLHF.

9. EFFICIENCY

★ Q62. What is LoRA and how does it work? LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the pretrained weights W and adds a trainable low-rank decomposition ΔW = A·B, where A ∈ R^{d×r}, B ∈ R^{r×k}, r ≪ min(d,k). Only A and B are trained (~0.1–1% of parameters), drastically reducing memory. At inference, ΔW can be merged into W for zero overhead. "Zero overhead" is only true when merged; multi-adapter serving keeps them separate.

Q63. What is QLoRA? LoRA fine-tuning on a 4-bit quantized base model, with double quantization and paged optimizers to fit a 65B model on a single 48GB GPU. The base model is frozen in NF4 (normal float 4-bit); LoRA adapters are in bf16. Gradients flow through the frozen quantized model to the adapters.

★ Q64. What is quantization and what are int4/int8? Quantization represents weights (and sometimes activations) in lower-bit formats. int8: 8-bit integer ≈ 50% memory reduction vs float16. int4: 4-bit ≈ 75% reduction — fits 70B models on ~40GB. Quality degradation is low for weights-only quantization (GPTQ, AWQ); activation quantization is harder. AWQ preserves "salient" weights; GPTQ uses second-order Hessian approximation.

Q65. What is knowledge distillation? Train a smaller "student" model to match the output distribution (soft targets / logits) of a larger "teacher" model, rather than just matching hard labels. Soft targets carry more information (probability of each class). Can also distill intermediate representations. Used to compress large LLMs into faster inference models.

Q66. Structured vs unstructured pruning — what's the difference? Unstructured: zero out individual weights (arbitrary positions) → sparse weight matrix, high compression ratio but requires sparse hardware to realize speedup. Structured: remove entire neurons, channels, or attention heads → dense subnetwork, immediately faster on standard hardware. Tony's first-author nnU-Net pruning work used purely unstructured magnitude/threshold pruning (post-training, no structured/channel conversion), removing >80% of weights while proxy Dice stayed >0.95. It reports no speedup number — converting the unstructured sparsity to structured pruning for a real hardware win is framed as future work (full verified detail in 20_OWN_PAPERS_GROUND_TRUTH.md).

Q67. What is KV-cache compression? Reduce the memory/bandwidth of the KV cache by evicting, quantizing, or low-rank-compressing the stored K/V tensors. Eviction (StreamingLLM, H2O): drop less-important past tokens. KV quantization: int4/int8 KV. Low-rank (MLA in DeepSeek): project K/V through a bottleneck. Critical for serving long-context models.

10. RAG / RETRIEVAL

★ Q68. How do embeddings enable retrieval? A dense encoder maps text to a fixed-size vector; similar texts are close in embedding space. At query time, the query is embedded and nearest-neighbor search (ANN, e.g., FAISS) retrieves candidate documents. Quality depends on the embedding model and the distribution match between query and document encoding.

Q69. BM25 vs dense retrieval — when does each win? BM25 is a sparse lexical method (TF-IDF-like with length normalization) — fast, no training, robust to domain shift, and wins on exact keyword and rare-term lookup. Dense retrieval (bi-encoder) captures semantic similarity but requires large in-domain training data. In practice, hybrid (BM25 + dense rerank) beats either alone. eBay product search benefits from BM25 for exact model/SKU matches.

Q70. Bi-encoder vs cross-encoder — tradeoffs? Bi-encoder: encode query and document INDEPENDENTLY, dot-product similarity at retrieval. Cross-encoder: concatenate query+document, output a single relevance score. Bi-encoder is fast at inference (documents pre-encoded); cross-encoder is much more accurate but slow (O(n) per query per doc). Standard pipeline: bi-encoder retrieves top-k, cross-encoder reranks.

Q71. What is reranking? Apply an expensive, accurate cross-encoder model to re-score the top-k candidates from a fast first-stage retriever. Two-stage: retrieve broadly (recall), rerank precisely (precision). Reranking is where most quality improvement comes from in production systems. Common in eBay product search, question answering, and LLM document selection for RAG.

Q72. What are common chunking strategies for RAG? Fixed-size (character/token count), sentence-boundary, semantic (cluster similar sentences), or document-structure-aware (sections/paragraphs). Chunk size trades recall (smaller = more targeted) vs. context (larger = more context per chunk). Overlap between chunks prevents information loss at boundaries. The right strategy depends on document type and query distribution.

Q73. What is hallucination in RAG and how do you measure it? Hallucination: the model generates factually incorrect information not grounded in retrieved context. RAGAS measures faithfulness (is the answer entailed by the context?) and answer relevance. Faithfulness is the key RAG-specific metric: it separates "LLM made up" from "retriever failed." Faithfulness < 1 even when retrieval is perfect indicates model-side hallucination.

11. NLP

Q74. What is TF-IDF? Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency — a sparse word-weighting scheme where TF rewards frequent terms in a document and IDF down-weights terms common across all documents. High TF-IDF = term is locally frequent but globally rare, hence discriminative. Foundation of BM25.

Q75. What is BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding)? BPE iteratively merges the most frequent adjacent byte-pair in the corpus into a new token, building a vocabulary of subword units. Balances coverage (rare words are decomposed) vs. vocabulary efficiency (common words stay whole). Used in GPT-2/3/4, LLaMA. Variants: Unigram LM (SentencePiece), WordPiece (BERT).

Q76. Word2Vec — what does it learn and how? Word2Vec trains a shallow 2-layer network to predict context from word (Skip-gram) or word from context (CBOW), resulting in dense word embeddings where similar words cluster. The embedding is the byproduct of a pseudo-classification task. Captures analogy structure (king − man + woman ≈ queen). Superseded by contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT) but still relevant for lightweight applications.

Q77. BLEU score — what are its known limitations? BLEU measures n-gram overlap between hypothesis and reference translations. Limitations: (1) no semantic understanding (wrong synonym = 0 overlap); (2) insensitive to fluency unless n>1; (3) reference-dependent (poor for paraphrase-rich targets); (4) not well-correlated with human judgment on sentence level; (5) decomposition (precision only, brevity penalty is crude). ChrF and COMET are preferred today.

Q78. What is chrF? Character-level F-score: harmonic mean of character n-gram precision and recall between hypothesis and reference. Default β=1 (equal precision/recall weight; chrF2 uses β=2, recall-heavy, which is the WMT standard variant). More robust than BLEU for morphologically rich languages (German, Polish, Turkish) where surface forms vary.

Q79. What is COMET? COMET is a learned MT evaluation metric trained on human direct assessment (DA) and post-editing effort scores — NOT n-gram overlap. Takes source, hypothesis, and (optionally) reference as input. Much higher correlation with human judgment than BLEU/chrF. COMET-Kiwi is reference-free (quality estimation). Standard at WMT since ~2021.

Q80. Coreference resolution in one line. Identify all noun phrases (mentions) in a document that refer to the same real-world entity and cluster them — "Jane said she was tired" → {Jane, she} are coreferent. Versley has deep expertise here (BART coref toolkit, 2010s Tübingen/Heidelberg work) — be ready to give this definition cleanly.

12. GENERATIVE MODELS

★ Q81. DDPM in one line. DDPM (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models) defines a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to data over T steps, then trains a neural network to REVERSE this process step by step, learning a denoising score function. At generation: sample pure noise, iteratively denoise. DDIM is a faster deterministic variant.

Q82. VAE in one line. A VAE trains an encoder to map inputs to a latent distribution q(z|x) and a decoder to reconstruct from z, maximizing the ELBO = reconstruction term − KL(q||prior). The reparameterization trick (z = μ + ε·σ, ε~N(0,1)) enables backprop through sampling. Posterior collapse and blurry samples are known weaknesses.

Q83. GAN in one line. A GAN trains a generator G to produce samples indistinguishable from real data and a discriminator D to tell them apart, in a minimax game: min_G max_D E[log D(x)] + E[log(1−D(G(z)))]. Mode collapse (G generates few modes) and training instability are key failure modes. WGAN uses Wasserstein distance for more stable training.

Q84. Flow matching in one line. Flow matching trains a neural network to predict the velocity field of a straight-line (optimal-transport) path between noise and data, enabling faster and more stable training than diffusion with fewer NFEs. Used in Stable Diffusion 3 and FLUX; replaces the complex noise schedule of DDPM with a simple linear interpolation.

★ Q85. What is CLIP and how is it trained? CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-Training) jointly trains an image encoder and text encoder with a contrastive loss: maximize cosine similarity of matching (image, caption) pairs and minimize it for mismatched pairs within the batch. Creates a shared embedding space for zero-shot classification and cross-modal retrieval. Used in DALL-E 2, Stable Diffusion, and eBay's VLM pipeline.

Q86. What is classifier-free guidance (CFG)? Train a conditional diffusion model jointly on conditioned and unconditioned (null token) inputs; at sampling, interpolate away from the unconditional prediction toward the conditional: ε̂ = ε_uncond + w·(ε_cond − ε_uncond). Higher guidance weight w = more adherence to condition, less diversity. CFG is standard for all production image/video generation models.

13. VLM / MULTIMODAL

★ Q87. CLIP loss vs SigLIP loss — what's the difference? CLIP uses softmax-normalized contrastive loss (InfoNCE) across the full batch — O(batch²) comparisons, requires large batches for stability. SigLIP uses a sigmoid binary classification loss on each (image, text) pair independently — scales to larger batches and smaller batch sizes, more stable. SigLIP (Zhai et al. 2023) is used in PaLI, Gemma-based VLMs, and eBay's VLM pipeline (finetuned Gemma3-4B).

Q88. Cross-attention projection vs direct projection for VLM fusion. Direct projection (LLaVA-style): a simple linear layer (MLP projector) maps ViT patch tokens into the LLM's embedding space — fast, minimal parameters. Cross-attention fusion (Flamingo-style): interleaved cross-attention layers in the LLM attend to visual tokens — more expressive but heavier. LLaVA-style projection is dominant in recent small VLMs; eBay's VLM paper uses Gemma3 (which has cross-attention to image tokens).

Q89. What are ViT patches? Vision Transformer (ViT) splits an image into fixed-size patches (e.g., 14×14 or 16×16 pixels), flattens and linearly embeds each patch as a token, then processes all patch tokens with a standard transformer. Resolution and patch size trade compute vs. fine-grained spatial detail. Higher resolution → more tokens → quadratic attention cost — addressed by efficient attention or downsampling.

14. EVALUATION

★ Q90. What is benchmark contamination? When test-set data appears in the model's training corpus — either verbatim or paraphrased — the benchmark no longer measures generalization. A widespread problem as web-scraped pretraining corpora grow. Detection: n-gram overlap checks, perplexity analysis, canary injection. Contaminated benchmarks can make models look far stronger than they are.

Q91. What are common LLM-as-judge biases? Position bias (prefers first/last option), verbosity bias (prefers longer outputs), self-preference (model rates own outputs higher), and sycophancy bias (rates confident-sounding answers higher regardless of correctness). Mitigation: swap order and average scores, calibrate with human labels, use multiple judge models, or use reference-based metrics instead.

Q92. NDCG and MRR — what are they? NDCG (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain): measures ranking quality, with higher-ranked relevant results getting more credit (discounted by log position), normalized by ideal ranking. MRR (Mean Reciprocal Rank): average of 1/rank of the first relevant result across queries. Both used in search/recommendation evaluation. NDCG supports graded relevance; MRR is for binary first-hit tasks.

Q93. What is train-serve skew? The distribution of inputs at inference time differs from the training distribution — causing performance degradation in production. Common causes: data pipeline differences (feature computation inconsistency), temporal shift (model trained on historical data, served on current), or domain drift. A major practical ML reliability issue; mitigated by online monitoring and retraining pipelines.
