6. The 10 Things Tony Must Be Able to Say Flawlessly

These are the 10 statements that, if delivered with precision and confidence, will cover 80% of the signal Versley is looking for. Memorize the content (not the exact words) and be able to produce each one from a cold start.

1. The 2-minute thesis narrative.

"AI has moved from a scaling race to a systems race. What matters is how you train efficiently, route intelligently, compress appropriately, and deploy under real constraints. Everything I have built in the last 18 months — the router, the diffusion weight predictor, the VLM for customer intent, the agentic benchmarks — is an instantiation of that thesis. It is also exactly what eBay is proving with their VLM paper: a finetuned 4B model outperforming a zero-shot 27B model on F1 while running about 3.8 times faster."

2. LLM pretraining data pipeline (what it takes to train LiLiuM-style).

"You need a data pipeline that does: deduplication (MinHash LSH or exact hash at document and paragraph level), quality filtering (heuristic — length, punctuation density, N-gram repetition ratio — plus model-based scoring using a perplexity filter trained on clean text), language identification, PII removal (especially for e-commerce where seller names and addresses appear), and domain mixing. For LiLiuM on 3T tokens multilingual with heavy e-commerce content, the custom tokenizer vocabulary is a critical systems decision — eBay's team showed it gives 34% generation speedup versus LLaMA-2, entirely from fewer tokens per word for e-commerce terms and non-Latin scripts."

3. Why tokenization matters for LiLiuM and what the trade-off is.

"Tokenizer vocabulary is a pretraining decision, not a deployment afterthought. A general tokenizer trained on web text will fragment e-commerce product titles and non-Latin text into many subword tokens, which means longer sequences at constant context length, more attention compute, and slower generation. LiLiuM's custom vocabulary reduces average tokens per character for the target domains, shortening sequences and directly reducing autoregressive decoding steps. The trade-off is that a specialized vocabulary makes the model less transferable to general web text — which is fine if you commit to a domain-specific deployment pipeline."

4. How LLM routing works and why it cuts cost without hurting quality.

"The core insight is that the difficulty distribution of real-world queries is heavy-tailed: most queries in a production system are routine and could be answered correctly by a much smaller model. The router's job is to predict, for each query, the minimum model that will produce an acceptable response. In our LLM Router, we frame this as a classification problem: given query features (token length, vocabulary complexity, semantic embeddings from a small encoder), predict whether a small model will succeed. We train the router on trajectories collected from a strong oracle model, using the oracle's success/failure signal as labels. At inference time, the router adds negligible latency (it is a tiny classifier) and routes 80–90% of queries to the small model, sending only the hard tail to the expensive model. On our benchmark this delivers greater than 90% cost reduction versus routing everything to the SOTA model, with matched performance."

5. How e-Llama domain-adapts an existing foundation model without catastrophic forgetting.

"The e-Llama paper (arXiv:2501.09706, eBay) uses continued pretraining of Llama 3.1 on 1T tokens of e-commerce text, for both 8B and 70B models. The key hyperparameter finding is the 10% learning rate rule: for the 8B, the max LR was set to 3.0e-5, which is 10% of Meta's original 3.0e-4 pretraining rate; for the 70B, 1.5e-5 from 1.5e-4. This is the hinge point — too high and you overwrite general-domain representations (catastrophic forgetting), too low and you make no progress on e-commerce. The data mixture also mattered: 50% e-commerce, 50% general, rather than the 5–15% general that prior work used. The result: roughly 25% relative improvement on English e-commerce benchmarks, 30% on non-English, with some general-domain degradation — more pronounced on the 8B — though NLU stays roughly comparable. This is a principled recipe Tony can engage with deeply as an informed reader of eBay's published work."

6. How Diffusion Weights replaces stepwise gradient descent.

"Standard fine-tuning is Markovian: you update weights using the gradient of the current loss. Each step is local and small. Diffusion Weights reframes this: treat the sequence of weight checkpoints during fine-tuning as a trajectory in weight space, and train a denoising diffusion model to predict future weight states from earlier states — in a single forward pass, multi-horizon. At fine-tune time, instead of running 10,000 gradient steps, you run the diffusion model to predict a future weight state directly — decoded via the reverse diffusion chain (Algorithm 1) — with robustness coming from multi-horizon weight-space merging rather than any corrective gradient steps. The net effect is roughly 3.2x wall-clock speedup — the conservative headline figure; the per-task Table 1 numbers actually run 3.8–4.3x — measured across a suite that includes GLUE, LAMBADA, MMLU, OpenBookQA, WikiText and WMT16, with downstream accuracy comparable to full fine-tuning. The WMT16 result is particularly relevant to eBay's machine translation use case."

7. What Versley's VLM paper achieved and why the number matters.

"The paper (arXiv:2602.11733, 2026) fine-tuned a Gemma3-4B on a multi-image item intelligence task in e-commerce. The fine-tuned 4B achieves F1 of 50.5 versus 44.8 for zero-shot Gemma3-27B — outperforming a model 7x its size — while delivering about 3.8x inference speedup from the smaller model and shorter prompts. The training data was synthetic: InternVL-2.5-26B generated captions, verified by Mistral-Small-3-24B. This is the same paradigm I used in the Human Intent World Model: generate high-quality synthetic reasoning traces from a large model, use them to fine-tune a small deployed model, and get task-specific performance that outperforms raw scale."

8. Coreference resolution and why it matters for an e-commerce LLM (Versley's academic home).

"Coreference resolution is the task of identifying all expressions in a text that refer to the same real-world entity — both identity anaphora (direct pronoun or noun-phrase reference) and bridging anaphora (where the link requires inference from world knowledge, which is the hard case Versley's thesis addressed). In an e-commerce context, coreference matters for product understanding: a listing might say 'the device' in one sentence and 'it' in the next, and the model needs to track that both refer to the specific product. It also matters for dialogue systems (ShopBot, agentic shopping) where a buyer says 'does it come in blue?' and the system must resolve 'it' to the product discussed 3 turns earlier. Modern LLMs handle entity tracking through attention and implicit memory, but they degrade on long documents and multi-product listings — which is a real open problem for eBay's agentic shopping work."

9. Why Tony left the MSCA fellowship — in 30 seconds.

"The fellowship was a rare opportunity and I am proud of the wireless-sensing and world-model work there. But I found myself more energized by the deployment side of AI — how do you make frontier models work under real cost and latency constraints — than by the pace of academic grant cycles. Leaving was a calculated bet. In the 12 months since, I have published or submitted six papers and built working systems with Tencent, Gradient Networks, and MeetaVista. I do not regret it."

7. Top Gotchas

Gotcha 1: Inventing a number from a Versley paper. He wrote those papers. He knows every number. If you cannot recall the exact figure, say so and give the directional claim instead. Example: "I don't have the exact LR in front of me but the key result was setting it at roughly 10% of the original pretraining rate to avoid catastrophic forgetting."

Gotcha 2: Saying "LLM routing is just ensembling." It is not. Ensembling runs all models and combines outputs. Routing runs exactly one model per query, chosen by a router. Be precise on this — Versley will notice the conflation.

Gotcha 3: Skipping the "why this design choice" layer. Versley will not stop at "what did you build." He will ask "why not X instead?" For every project you present, have a prepared answer to the most obvious alternative. For LLM Router: "why not a mixture-of-experts architecture?" Answer: MoE requires all experts to share the training run and cannot leverage already-trained specialist models; routing is a post-hoc systems decision that works with any existing models.

Gotcha 4: Being vague about classical NLP. Versley has 15 years of genuine computational linguistics expertise. If he asks about discourse relations, coreference types, or parsing, give precise answers with the right terminology (identity anaphora vs. bridging, discourse connectives, RST vs. PDTB, shift-reduce vs. chart parsing). Vague answers will register as a gap.

Gotcha 5: Presenting Diffusion Weights without grounding the mechanism. "We use diffusion to predict future weights" is not enough. You must say: denoising diffusion model, trained on weight checkpoint sequences, multi-horizon prediction of future weight states decoded via the reverse diffusion chain, with weight-space merging across horizons for robustness rather than corrective gradient steps. The mechanism is what makes the speedup plausible.

Gotcha 6: Ignoring eBay's MT use case when discussing machine translation. Your WMT16 result in Diffusion Weights is directly relevant to eBay's cross-border machine translation business. When you cite WMT16, add: "which is also directly relevant to eBay's cross-border MT pipeline." Connecting dots is your job, not his.

Gotcha 7: Running out of questions for Versley. He will give you 10 minutes for questions. Using only 3 minutes signals you have not done your homework on his work. Have five questions prepared; use all available time. The three listed in item 10 above are verified against his published papers — use them.

Gotcha 8: Treating this as a pure ML quiz. Versley's job is to assess whether you would be a productive collaborator on his team's real research problems — LLM pretraining, domain adaptation, VLMs for e-commerce. Show intellectual curiosity about his work, not just competence on textbook questions. The best Stage 1 outcomes are when the candidate makes the interviewer want to work with them.

Gotcha 9: Being unprepared for "what do you think the main challenge is in LLM pretraining at scale?" Prepare a specific answer: data quality and deduplication at 3T-token scale; training instability (loss spikes, gradient norm explosions, especially mid-training on modern LR schedules); tokenizer vocabulary as a systems bottleneck; evaluation at scale (you cannot afford a full eval run every checkpoint). Cite at least one concrete mechanism per point.

Gotcha 10: Not having a prepared eBay-specific observation from browsing the site. At some point the conversation will touch on "why eBay." Having a concrete, specific observation from actually using the product ("I was searching for a used lens last night and noticed the listing title quality varies enormously — the filtering/ranking problem is real") is worth more than any generic enthusiasm statement.
