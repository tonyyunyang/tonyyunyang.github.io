Design Skeletons Bank

A bank of design prompts and ready-to-whiteboard skeletons for Interview 2 (ML System Design). Read this to rehearse framing any prompt in 30 seconds and surviving one round of probing. For the 14-step method itself use ML System Design Framework; for the deep search/recsys design use Flagship: Search Ranking at eBay Scale; for "now 10× it" use the Scaling Playbook. This doc does not repeat those.

You drive this round. The graders are watching for a production engineer, not a researcher: lead with a business metric, propose a spine, then go deep where they probe. The skeletons below give you the spine + the two or three tricky bits that earn the point.

1. Ranked probable prompts (with a 30-second opener)

Ranked by likelihood for this role (Amsterdam Foundation Models / Applied Research). LLM / RAG / routing / evals out-rank pure recsys because this team lives there — but search/recsys is eBay's core and a flagged weak spot, so be airtight on it. Tags: [EDGE] = lean in, real strength · [CORE] = eBay staple, neutral · [RISK] = must cover credibly, less natural.

# Prompt Tag How I'd frame it (opener)
1 LLM serving + routing gateway for all eBay GenAI (Magical Listing, MT, agentic, relevance scoring) under a hard cost ceiling [EDGE] "One gateway, many features with different latency/cost SLAs. A router — complexity → model tier — in front of a shared vLLM fleet with semantic + prefix caching, and I instrument the escape/escalation rate from day one. My router work shows >90% cost reduction at iso-quality, so the real engineering is measuring and controlling the ~5–10% that must escalate." (§2a)
2 RAG product-Q&A / shopping assistant grounded in catalog + reviews + policies [EDGE] "Retrieval-first, generation-second, refuse-when-unsure. I separate retrieval quality from generation faithfulness and measure them independently — RAGAS-style faithfulness plus an LLM judge I've calibrated against humans. The winning behavior is refusal over fabrication." (§2b)
3 Product search ranking / relevance at eBay scale [RISK] "Retrieve-then-rank over 2B listings. I spend my time on the two parts that decide quality: how I train the two-tower retriever (sampling bias is the whole game) and how I make the ranker multi-task and calibrated, then prove a win offline → interleaving → A/B." (Full: Flagship doc.)
4 LLM-as-judge / evaluation harness at scale (ranking labels, listing quality, MT) [EDGE] "The judge is a model, so I treat it as a system to validate: calibrate against a human gold set (spot-check 2–5%), correct position/verbosity/self-enhancement bias, and gate any judge below ~80% human agreement out of the training-signal path. Dynamic held-out sets to fight contamination."
5 Continued-pretraining / domain adaptation (e-Llama-style: make a base LLM e-commerce-native) [CORE] "Data curation is 80% of the work — dedup, quality filter, PII scrub — then a 50/50 domain/general mix at 10% of original LR to avoid catastrophic forgetting. I build the eval harness before burning GPU-hours." (§2h)
6 Magical Listing — photo(s) → full structured listing (VLM + KG + agentic) [CORE] "Separate perception (VLM: what is this?) from structured validation (KG: what are legal attribute values for this category?), glued by an agent orchestrator with per-step fallback so the seller always gets a partial-useful result." (§2g)
7 Machine translation for cross-border listings at billions/year [CORE] "Two paths: zero-shot in-house model for high-resource pairs, RAG / translation-memory for low-resource pairs. A real-time path for buyer-visible text and a throughput-optimized batch path for indexing." (§2f)
8 Query understanding / semantic search (lang-ID → spell → intent → NER → embedding) [CORE] "A cheap shared layer that runs before retrieval and feeds every downstream consumer. Because retrieval, ranking, ads and MT all read its output, latency (<20 ms) and cross-consumer consistency are the hard constraints — not model accuracy." (§2d)
9 Personalized recommendation / homepage feed [RISK] "Same retrieve-then-rank spine as search, but the query is the user. Candidate gen from a sequential user tower + item-to-item co-engagement; multi-task ranker (click/cart/purchase) with a diversity/exploration budget so the feedback loop doesn't collapse." (Recs forks live in the Flagship doc.)
10 Real-time fraud / anomaly / trust & safety for transactions or listings [RISK] "Streaming, sub-100 ms, extreme imbalance, adversarial and drifting. Cost-sensitive fast model on point-in-time streaming features + a rules/allow-deny layer + human-in-the-loop for the gray zone. Metric is precision@review-capacity, not accuracy." (§2c)
11 Sponsored / ads ranking with auction + budget pacing [CORE] "Two coupled systems: a calibrated pCTR·pCVR model (calibration matters because the score sets a price, not just a sort order) and an auction + pacing layer. eCPM ranking, budget pacing as control theory, and a relevance floor via LLM-judge." (§2e)
12 Generative recommender / semantic-ID system (OneRec/TIGER — unify retrieve + rank) [EDGE, frontier] "Where recsys meets foundation models — my lane. Tokenize items into semantic IDs (RQ-VAE over content embeddings), then an encoder-decoder generates the next-item ID, collapsing retrieval and ranking into one model. Honest tradeoffs: it's a serving-latency and cold-start bet, not a free lunch." (Frontier detail in the Flagship doc.)
13 Delivery-time / ETA prediction (or demand forecasting) — the likely outside-domain prompt [RISK] "This is regression / forecasting, not retrieve-then-rank — I'd say that out loud and switch shapes. Predict a P90 delivery promise with quantile/pinball loss (late ≫ early), backtest with rolling-origin CV, and calibrate by interval coverage, not ECE. North-star is on-time-rate vs. the promised date." (§2j)

Honorable mentions (frame in one line): agentic shopping assistant with tool use + guardrails; a 3T-token data-curation/dedup pipeline; visual "Shop the Look" similarity search; a GPU-fleet cost-optimization design; an online/near-real-time re-ranking pipeline (ingestion → retrain trigger → shadow).

Trap: never open with architecture. Open with the business metric and the guardrail bundle, then the spine. The #1 silent way to fail this round is "thinking like a researcher, not a production engineer."

2. Fuller skeletons (frame → components → tricky bits → metrics)

Each skeleton is enough to draw the boxes and survive one probe per tricky bit. Shared method (latency budget, two-stage retrieval, hybrid BM25+dense, the honest-ops pivot) lives in the ML System Design Framework, Flagship, Scaling Playbook, and MLOps Honest-Gap Script — I reference, not repeat.

2a. LLM serving + routing gateway under a cost ceiling [EDGE — my strongest design]

Frame. One shared GenAI platform behind many features (Magical Listing, MT, relevance scoring, agentic flows) that have different latency and cost SLAs. The design goal is not "serve an LLM" — it's maximize quality per dollar under a hard cost ceiling, which means routing cheap-model-first and controlling the escalation tail.

Key components.

  • Router — classify each request by complexity/skill → model tier (small in-house → mid → frontier). Upfront routing for latency; optional cascade fallback for safety. This is exactly my MERA/LLM-Router work — details of architecture + training signal in Own-Papers Ground Truth.
  • Shared vLLM fleet — continuous batching + PagedAttention for throughput; quantized weights (FP8 default on Hopper); prefill/decode disaggregation if TTFT and inter-token latency need independent tuning.
  • Two caches. Semantic cache (embed the request, return a prior answer on high similarity — near-zero quality hit for repeated intents) and prefix/KV cache (reuse shared system-prompt prefixes across calls).
  • Model pool = eBay's zoo: LiLiuM (1B/7B/13B, ~34% faster generation from the custom tokenizer) for the cheap tiers, e-Llama (8B/70B) for harder e-commerce tasks, a frontier API only for the genuine tail. (Attribute LiLiuM/e-Llama as eBay's published work, not mine — see eBay AI Dossier.)
  • Escape/escalation-rate instrumentation from day one — the single metric that tells you whether the router is safe. Log every escalation and every low-confidence route.

Tricky bits.

  1. Controlling the tail, not the average. >90% of mixed-difficulty queries go to a smaller model at iso-quality; the whole risk lives in the ~5–10% that must escalate. Instrument the escape rate, alarm on it, and keep the cascade as a fallback so a mis-route degrades cost, not quality.
  2. The router is a model that drifts. Monitor the decision distribution (share of traffic per tier) as a first-class drift signal — a silent shift means either input drift or a router regression. Retrain cadence + shadow-eval before promoting a new router.
  3. Calibration of the routing score. A confidence threshold that sets the tier must mean what it says; recalibrate on a schedule.

Metrics. Cost per query at iso-quality (north-star); escape/escalation rate; per-tier quality (LLM-judge + human spot-check); TTFT / TPOT / p99; KV-cache utilization and goodput (not raw throughput); cache hit-rate. Quantify the win with the verified numbers: >90% cost reduction at iso-quality (LLM Router), 87.3% router-decision accuracy at 51.8% serving cost (MERA — router-decision accuracy, never "task accuracy"), 53% on TwinRouterBench (trained router vs. unrouted Opus, matched resolve, dynamic track).

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: whether MERA is strictly upfront-routing or a hybrid with a cascade fallback, and the exact model pool used — keep these precise, they've caused a fix pass before.

2b. RAG product-Q&A / shopping assistant [EDGE]

Frame. Answer buyer/seller questions grounded in catalog + item specifics + reviews + policies. Two independently measured qualities: retrieval (did we fetch the right context?) and faithfulness (did we answer only from it, and refuse when we can't?). A plausible wrong answer about a real transaction is worse than no answer.

Key components.

  • Query → query understanding (reuse §2d) → hybrid retrieval: dense over product/review chunks + BM25 for exact SKUs/specs + KG lookup for structured attributes → rerank top-k.
  • Generator = cost-routed in-house LLM (§2a) with a strict "answer from context or say I don't know" system prompt.
  • Structured, attributed output — answer with citations back to the source listing/review, so it's auditable.
  • Eval harness = a rolling human-checked gold set + a calibrated LLM judge (this is part of the system, not an afterthought).

Tricky bits.

  1. Faithfulness / hallucination. Evaluate with RAGAS-style reference-free faithfulness + a calibrated judge; enforce refusal over fabrication. Separating this from retrieval recall is what lets you debug "wrong answer" = "wrong context" vs. "unfaithful generation".
  2. Freshness. Prices and stock change; retrieval must hit live structured fields at query time, not a stale vector snapshot — otherwise you confidently quote last week's price.
  3. Grounding vs. helpfulness tension. Too strict → refuses useful questions; too loose → fabricates. Tune the refusal threshold as a product decision and monitor the refusal rate.
  4. Structured-vs-unstructured retrieval arbitration. Classify the query type first: a structured-fact question ("what's the screen size / is it waterproof?") routes to the product knowledge graph, not the passage retriever, which only fuzzily matches prose; open-ended questions go to passages. When KG and passage retrieval conflict on a fact, resolve with the KG as authority (structured attributes are curated ground truth; review/description text is noisy).

Metrics. Retrieval: recall@k / MRR over catalog+reviews+KG. Generation: faithfulness score, refusal-correctness (refuses when it should, answers when it can), citation accuracy. System: judge–human agreement (gate <~80%), end-to-end answer helpfulness, latency, cost per answer (routed).

2c. Fraud / anomaly / trust & safety [RISK — own the ops gap honestly]

Frame. A streaming binary/three-way decision under a sub-100 ms budget, with extreme class imbalance (fraud <<1%), adversarial and non-stationary. Optimize precision@review-capacity and recall on high-loss events — never raw accuracy (a 99.9%-accurate "always allow" model is useless). Cost-sensitive: a missed fraud costs ~100× a false alarm.

Key components.

  • Event stream (Kafka) → point-in-time-correct streaming features: velocity ("# transactions this account, last 5 min"), graph features (shared device/IP/payment instrument across accounts).
  • Fast model — GBDT / isolation-forest baseline; LSTM or transformer autoencoder for sequence anomaly.
  • Rules / allow-deny layer for known patterns — cheap, auditable, instant to ship, and it catches the obvious while the model handles the gray zone.
  • Three-way decision: allow / block / route to human review for the uncertain band.
  • Delayed labels — ground truth (chargebacks) arrives days-to-weeks later.

Tricky bits.

  1. Label delay. You learn truth weeks later → use proxy labels + delayed-feedback correction, and monitor input/prediction drift now rather than waiting for labels (the proxy ladder from the MLOps Honest-Gap Script).
  2. Adversarial drift. Attackers adapt within days → drift monitoring (PSI/KS on inputs, decision-mix on outputs) + fast retrain is core infrastructure, not a nice-to-have.
  3. Graph structure. Fraud rings share entities → account-linkage / GNN features beat per-transaction features.

Metrics. Precision@review-capacity, recall on high-$ loss, dollar-weighted loss caught, review-queue volume (ops cost), decision latency p99, drift alarms. My anomaly/compression instincts transfer here — but I'd own the "I haven't personally run live payment on-call" gap using the MLOps Honest-Gap Script, not bluff it.

2d. Query understanding / semantic search [CORE]

Frame. A cheap shared pre-retrieval layer whose outputs every downstream stage consumes (retrieval, ranking, ads, MT). Because it's shared, the hard constraints are latency (<20 ms) and cross-consumer consistency — a change here silently moves ranking and ads revenue — not raw model accuracy.

Key components (a pipeline of small models):

  • Language ID (fastText) → spell/normalization (edit-distance + LM prior) → intent & category classification (soft taxonomy distribution) → NER / attribute tagging (brand, model, size, color, condition → structured filter constraints) → query embedding (eBERT/LiLiuM encoder) → optional query rewriting/expansion (increasingly an LLM, run async/cached, never on the hot path).

Tricky bits.

  1. It's shared infra — version it and monitor per-consumer, because one change ripples into every downstream system at once.
  2. Head vs. tail queries — traffic is Zipfian, so cache/precompute the head and spend model budget on the tail.
  3. Multilingual — 7 markets → per-locale tagging/normalization; this is where eBay's custom tokenizer + MT plug in.

Metrics. Per-stage: spell-correction accuracy, intent/category F1, NER F1. System: p50/p99 latency, cache hit-rate, and — the one that matters — downstream lift (does better QU raise retrieval recall / GMV per session?). Consistency: version-diff monitoring per consumer.

2e. Ads / sponsored ranking [CORE]

Frame. Two coupled systems — a calibrated response model and an auction + pacing layer. Calibration is load-bearing here (unlike organic sort) because the predicted probability sets a price and a spend, not just an order.

Key components.

  • Candidate ads → multi-task pCTR·pCVR model (MMoE/PLE, shared with organic ranking method) → eCPM = bid × pCTR (or pCVR) ranking → auction (generalized second-price / VCG-style) → budget pacing (throttle each advertiser so daily spend is smooth, not front-loaded) → relevance floor (don't show irrelevant high-bid ads) → blended with organic under a whole-page objective.

Tricky bits.

  1. Calibration = revenue. A miscalibrated pCTR mis-prices the entire auction, so ECE is a revenue metric here, monitored per segment and recalibrated on a schedule (Platt/isotonic).
  2. Pacing is control theory, not ML. PID-style controllers smooth spend across the day — name it explicitly; it's a common gap.
  3. Multi-sided objective. Buyer relevance vs. advertiser ROI vs. eBay revenue, tuned jointly; a relevance floor via LLM-judge keeps the buyer experience honest. eBay Ads publishes here (keyphrase-relevance LLM-judge, LLMDistill4Ads) — ground it there.

Metrics. Revenue/RPM, eCPM, calibration (ECE), advertiser ROAS + budget-delivery smoothness, relevance floor pass-rate, and buyer guardrails (organic CTR/GMV not cannibalized).

2f. Machine translation for listings [CORE]

Frame. Translate billions of listing texts/year across 7 markets. Split by resource level and by path: high-resource pairs get zero-shot in-house MT; low-resource pairs get RAG / translation-memory grounding. And real-time (buyer-visible text, tight latency) vs. batch (bulk indexing, throughput-optimized) are two different serving profiles.

Key components.

  • High-resource pairs: zero-shot in-house model (LiLiuM/e-Llama) — fast, good enough, cheap.
  • Low-resource pairs: RAG / translation-memory — BM25 over the bilingual catalog retrieves prior human/verified translations of similar listings, injected as context to steer terminology.
  • Two serving paths: real-time (buyer clicks a foreign listing → <~2 s) vs. batch (index-time bulk translation → optimize throughput/cost).
  • Domain glossary / terminology enforcement — brand names, sizes, category terms must not be "translated" wrongly.

Tricky bits.

  1. Terminology fidelity — e-commerce entities (brand, model, size) must survive translation; translation-memory + glossary constraints handle this better than raw MT.
  2. Real-time vs. batch tradeoff — same model, different optimization (latency vs. throughput); route by path.
  3. Quality estimation without references — you rarely have gold translations at scale, so use a reference-free QE model / LLM-judge to flag low-confidence outputs for review.

Metrics. COMET/BLEU where references exist; reference-free QE score; terminology-accuracy on entities; real-time latency p99 vs. batch throughput/cost; buyer engagement on translated listings. eBay publishes on RAG-MT — cite it (eBay AI Dossier).

2g. Magical Listing (photo → listing) [CORE]

Frame. Seller uploads photo(s) → system produces a full structured listing (title, category, attributes, description, price hint). The design principle: separate perception from validation, and orchestrate with per-step fallback so the seller always gets a partial-useful draft even when a step fails.

Key components.

  • VLM perception — "what is this?" → category guess, visible attributes, a draft title/description. (eBay's VLM work: a fine-tuned Gemma3-4B reaches F1 50.5, beating zero-shot Gemma3-27B at 44.8, ~3.8× faster — cite as eBay's published work.)
  • Product knowledge-graph structured validation — "what are the legal attribute values for this category?" The KG constrains and corrects the VLM's free-text into a valid, filterable listing (enum snapping, required-attribute completion).
  • Agentic orchestrator — sequences perception → KG validation → title/description generation → price suggestion, with per-step fallback (if the VLM is unsure of category, fall back to asking the seller one question rather than failing the whole flow).

Tricky bits.

  1. Perception vs. validation separation — the VLM hallucinates attributes; the KG is the source of truth for what's legal in a category. Don't let the VLM write directly to the listing.
  2. Per-step fallback / graceful degradation — a partial listing the seller edits beats a blocked flow; every agent step needs a fallback.
  3. Structured-output reliability — force the model to emit schema-valid attributes (constrained decoding / validation-and-repair loop against the KG).

Metrics. Attribute-extraction F1 (vs. KG-valid ground truth), category accuracy, listing completion rate, seller edit-rate (how much they had to fix — lower is better), time-to-list, and downstream: do generated listings convert as well as hand-written ones?

2h. Continued-pretraining / domain adaptation (e-Llama-style) [CORE — Versley/eBay's turf]

Frame. Take a strong base LLM (Llama-3.1 8B/70B) and make it e-commerce-native via continued pretraining on domain tokens without catastrophic forgetting of general ability. The unglamorous truth: data curation is 80% of the work, and the eval harness must exist before you spend a single GPU-hour.

Key components.

  • Data pipeline (the 80%): collect e-commerce corpus → dedup (MinHash/LSH; at 1T+ tokens, 1% dup = 10B wasted tokens) → quality filter → PII scrub → contamination audit against the eval sets.
  • Training recipe: 50/50 domain/general mix (the general half is the anti-forgetting anchor) at LR = 10% of the original — both ablated by eBay. e-Llama = continued pretraining of Llama-3.1 8B & 70B on 1T e-commerce tokens (attribute as eBay's published work).
  • Eval harness built FIRST: internal e-commerce NLU tasks + general-capability regression checks + contamination detection. You cannot tell if adaptation worked without it.

Tricky bits.

  1. Catastrophic forgetting — the whole reason for the 50/50 mix and the low LR; measure it with a general-capability regression suite, not just domain gains.
  2. Data curation dominates outcome — dedup + quality filtering move the needle more than architecture; PII/contamination are correctness and compliance issues.
  3. Eval-first discipline — build the measuring stick before training, or you're flying blind on a very expensive run (this is also a strong PM story — see the project walkthrough).

Metrics. Domain NLU lift (e-commerce task suite) vs. general-capability regression (must stay flat), training loss/perplexity on held-out domain + general, MFU during the run (40–47% good on H100), contamination-audit clean. Tie the payoff to serving: a well-adapted 8B beats a zero-shot larger model at a fraction of the cost — the small-beats-big economics that run through the whole eBay AI Dossier.

2i. Online / near-real-time re-ranking [CORE]

Frame. The base ranker (trained offline, see Flagship) is stale the moment it ships: fresh inventory, flash demand, price changes, and same-session intent aren't in it. This skeleton is the freshness layer on top of the flagship spine — it keeps the retrieval + two-tower + multi-task ranker from the Flagship doc and adds the machinery to react in minutes-to-seconds, not the daily/weekly training cadence.

Key components.

  • Streaming ingestion — Kafka/Flink over the click/cart/purchase/impression stream, producing point-in-time-correct engagement counters (leak-free, same discipline as the fraud features in §2c).
  • Online / near-line feature updates — a feature store with two write paths: near-line (materialized every few minutes: item CTR, trending velocity, in-stock/price) and online (per-request session context: what this user just clicked). Reuse the query-understanding output (§2d) rather than recomputing.
  • Re-ranking layer — a light model on top of base ranker scores that folds in the fresh signals (real-time popularity, session sequence, business boosts/de-boosts). Cheap enough to run on the top-k the base ranker already returned.
  • Retrain-trigger policy — don't retrain on a fixed clock; trigger on drift + staleness signals: PSI/KS on feature inputs, decision-mix shift on outputs, and a freshness SLA (e.g. new-listing exposure lag). A trigger fires a shadow run, not a direct push.
  • Shadow → canary → ramp — new re-ranker runs in shadow (scores logged, not served) to compare against live, then canary on a small traffic slice with automated guardrail checks, then gradual ramp. Auto-rollback on guardrail breach.

Tricky bits.

  1. Point-in-time correctness / no leakage. Online features are the easiest place to leak future info into training labels; the streaming pipeline must stamp features as-of request time, or offline eval lies.
  2. Feedback-loop collapse. Real-time popularity signals are self-reinforcing (rich-get-richer) → keep an exploration budget and de-bias so the loop doesn't starve fresh inventory.
  3. Latency budget. The re-rank step spends from the same end-to-end budget as retrieval + base ranking, so it gets a tight slice (single-digit ms on top-k only); a slow feature lookup silently blows the p99. Degrade gracefully to base-ranker order if the online store times out.

Metrics. Freshness/coverage (new-listing exposure lag, % catalog served), online-vs-offline score agreement (leakage canary), engagement lift on the canary (CTR/CVR/GMV per session) vs. guardrails not cannibalized, feature-pipeline freshness SLA + p99, retrain-trigger precision (did fires correspond to real drift?), re-rank step latency p99.

2j. Delivery-time / ETA prediction (+ demand forecasting) [RISK — the outside-domain curveball]

Frame. A continuous-target regression / forecasting problem — not retrieve-then-rank, and that's exactly the point. The recruiter flagged the design round may sit outside your core domain, and "estimate the delivery date for this listing" (a real buyer-facing eBay surface) or "forecast demand for inventory/pricing" is the canonical outside-domain e-commerce prompt. The candidate-gen → rank spine does not apply here; say that out loud and switch shapes. North-star is on-time rate against the promised date (plus MAPE / weighted-quantile loss on the estimate) — never NDCG or GMV/session.

Key components.

  • Target & horizon — predict the distribution of delivery time (origin → destination), or forecast demand per item/category over a horizon. Frame the ETA as a quantile you promise, not a point estimate.
  • Features (point-in-time correct) — carrier + service level, origin/destination zones, historical lane transit times, per-seller handling time, day-of-week / holiday / peak-season calendars, weather/disruption signals. Reuse the feature-store + as-of-request-time discipline from §2c/§2i — leakage is just as fatal here as in ranking.
  • Model — gradient-boosted trees are the strong tabular baseline; add a quantile-regression head for the promise. For demand, a global forecasting model (one model over all series with covariates) beats per-series ARIMA at catalog scale.

Tricky bits.

  1. Quantile / pinball loss + asymmetric cost. A delivery promise is a P90, not a mean — being late costs far more than being early (a broken promise hurts trust and triggers claims), so you optimize the pinball loss at τ≈0.9, which penalizes under-prediction asymmetrically. Naming this is the single highest-signal move in the room.
  2. Backtesting, not a random split. Evaluate with rolling-origin / time-series cross-validation (train on the past, test on the future, roll the origin forward) — a random train/test split leaks the future into the past and lies. This is the forecasting analogue of point-in-time correctness.
  3. Interval calibration, not classifier ECE. The right calibration metric is prediction-interval coverage — does your P10–P90 band actually contain the outcome ~80% of the time? — distinct from the classification ECE used everywhere else in this bank.
  4. Hierarchical reconciliation (demand variant). Forecasts at item / category / region levels must be mutually consistent (they should sum); reconcile them (e.g. MinT) so the item forecasts agree with the category total the business actually plans against.

Metrics. On-time rate vs. promised date (north-star), pinball loss at the promised quantile, MAPE / WAPE on the point estimate, prediction-interval coverage (calibration), and the business tie-in: promise-breakage rate, conversion lift from tighter/earlier promises. For demand: revenue-weighted forecast accuracy, stock-out / over-stock cost.

The unfamiliar-domain move (say this when the prompt is truly outside your lane). "I'll state the invariant spine — objective + guardrails, features with point-in-time correctness, a simple strong baseline, an offline metric that matches the business cost, then shadow → canary → ramp — and I'll flag the domain-specific piece (here, the carrier/logistics physics) as the part I'd validate with a domain expert before trusting it." That's the design-round analogue of the MLOps Honest-Gap Script: a credible non-bluffing spine plus an explicit "here's what I'd confirm" reads as senior judgment, not a gap.

3. How to use this bank

  • Before the round: pick the 4–5 prompts you're weakest on from §1 and rehearse the opener until it's automatic. Skim the matching §2 skeleton so you can expand any node on probing.
  • In the room: open with the metric bundle, draw the spine, then say "let me go deep on X" and pick the tricky bit you want to defend. You control the depth.
  • When they say "now 10× it": switch to the Scaling Playbook 6-axis walk (data → model → training → serving → cost → org), ending each on "what breaks first + the cheapest lever."
  • When ops comes up: use the MLOps Honest-Gap Script verbatim — own the live-canary/drift gap in one clause, pivot to efficiency. Don't overstate.

Trap: don't quote a wrong own-paper number. >90% cost reduction at iso-quality; 87.3% router-decision accuracy (never "task accuracy") at 51.8% serving cost; 53% TwinRouterBench (specific matched-resolve dynamic-track figure); Diffusion Weights ~3.2×. When in doubt, defer to Own-Papers Ground Truth.
