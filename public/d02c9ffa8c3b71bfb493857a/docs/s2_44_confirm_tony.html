Fill These Before the Room

This is the one page that no prep writer could finish for you — every blank here is a named person, a real number, a lived observation that only you can supply, and each one is a place a tough interviewer will dig until it either holds or collapses. The shapes are already written across the rest of the pack (s2_30, s2_31, s2_32, s2_15, s2_40, s2_41); what's missing is the true specific that turns "I answered an anticipated objection" into "I changed this person's mind." Fill every blank in your own words from a real memory — never invent — then rehearse each one out loud once so it comes out clean under pressure.

How to use this page

  • Work top to bottom. Each row is: what to supply, why it scores (the eBay value it earns), and a safe default — how to phrase it if the real specific won't come. The safe default is always anchored on something already true in your record, never a fabrication.
  • If you genuinely have nothing, say the safe default and stop. An honest "I'd have to check the exact specifics" beats a confident invention that dies on the first follow-up — eBay scores Act With Integrity explicitly.
  • Never improvise a name, number, or lived experience to fill a blank. A blank spoken honestly is a small cost; a fabrication caught on a probe is the whole loop.
  • Fill it, then say it aloud once. Reading it isn't rehearsing it.

STAR skeletons — fill the ___, keep each box to one spoken line.

B1 — Difficult stakeholder. (Feeds: "difficult customer/vendor," "a disagreement," "stood firm under pressure," "who were your stakeholders" — each has its own anchor; see note below.)

  • S: On ___ (which collaboration) I worked with ___ (name the person — a co-author / advisor / industry partner) who didn't report to me.
  • T: The friction: they pushed for ___ (what they literally wanted — a broader claim / a faster headline number / a different scope) while I wanted ___.
  • A: I ___ (what you actually did — depersonalized it, showed the data behind the limit, proposed a narrower defensible scope, ran a control).
  • R: It landed as ___ (did they concede / narrow the claim / change a score — the human outcome).
  • If you have nothing better, use: Reverse Imaging RI-T2S — "the method underperformed a baseline on short-echo data, and in a cross-discipline team the physics side and the ML side could each assume it was the other's bug; my job was to name exactly where it broke and align on the fix without authority." Name the collaborator you aligned with.

B2 — Influence with data. (P4 in s2_31.)

  • S: When I claimed >80% of a trained nnU-Net could be removed, the natural objection was "your proxy Dice just measures the model agreeing with itself — that's circular."
  • T: Make the compression claim credible to a skeptic.
  • A: I led with the ACDC ground-truth curve alongside the proxy curve — showed they track, so the proxy is calibrated against truth where we can check it — plus the inverted-U redundancy map for mechanism.
  • R: ___ — name the real person who voiced the doubt and then changed their mind (advisor Qian Tao / a specific co-author / a reviewer), what they believed before, and what they did differently after (raised a score, dropped the objection, greenlit the framing).
  • If you have nothing better, use: tell it with the curve as the persuader and attribute the shift to "the review" honestly — do not invent a name.

B3 — Advise a person. (P3 in s2_31.)

  • S: On TwinRouterBench the slice the team relied on me for was the evaluation methodology.
  • T: Get the team to build the measuring stick before betting on any router.
  • A: I advised — and built — the greedy sequential-locking labeling protocol and failure-aware cost accounting; the through-line was "a single router's number is unfalsifiable, measure comparatively first."
  • R: ___ — name who received and adopted the advice (a co-author / one of the equal-contribution leads Pei Yang / Wanyi Chen) and what they changed.
  • If you have nothing better, use: own the methodology slice and hand the floor back ("curious how you'd label step difficulty at eBay scale") — don't turn it into a lecture on routing.

B4 — Feedback you gave / mentoring.

  • S/T/A/R: As TA/MSc mentor across ~5 courses I ___ (one real mentee, what you told them — including when an approach wasn't working, and the outcome).
  • If you have nothing better, use: your genuine reflex — "make the problem crisp, then hand ownership back" — spoken about mentoring in general, without fabricating a specific confrontation.

B5 — Timeline that slipped.

  • S/T/A/R: On ___ a ___ (task / metric fix / control) cost more time than estimated; I ___ (re-cut scope, flagged it early, made the call) and ___ (the outcome). Give a real date/decision.
  • If you have nothing better, use: the nnU-Net MIDL deadline — front-loaded the riskiest runs, kept a publishable baseline regardless. Confirm your actual milestone cadence and who you reported milestones to (e.g. advisor Qian Tao) before telling the cadence as fact.

B6 — Failure story (averaging trap).

  • S/T/A/R: MERA's model-evolution half showed no cumulative gain — ~47% flat; we published the null instead of dropping it. The systemic change: I now score per-slice with a per-slice guardrail by default.
  • ___ — the concrete origin episode where an aggregate hid a failing slice and you switched to per-slice scoring, if you can name a real instance.
  • If you have nothing better, use: the verified MERA null as the whole story; on "which category, what numbers?" give the direction and offer to pull the exact per-slice figure — never invent the category.

B7 — Influence without authority.

  • S/T/A/R: On the eye-tracking paper I coordinated a co-first author (Bishwas Regmi) and a senior external collaborator (Andreas Bulling) — people I couldn't direct — so the job was alignment, not instruction.
  • ___ — one concrete steering moment: a design call you disagreed on and how you moved it.
  • If you have nothing better, use: the benchmark-first framing — you won the team to it by showing a single-router number is unfalsifiable, alignment by evidence not title.

2. Your real hands-on stack — what you personally ran vs. know by design

This is the deep-probe follow-up to every MLOps question, and it's an integrity trap in both directions: over-claim and it unravels on "walk me through your config"; under-claim and you sound like you've never touched serving. Lock the two sentences once (they mirror s2_15 and are reused in s2_13 / s2_14) and say each the same way everywhere.

What to supply Why it scores Safe default / how to phrase
S1 · Serving stack — which of vLLM / SGLang / Triton / TensorRT-LLM you've personally run vs. know by design Act With Integrity; precision here reads as senior Confirm the one you've truly launched, then: "I've run ___ at research/shadow scale; ___ I know by design — the tradeoffs and when I'd reach for each — but haven't operated in production." (Default assumes vLLM is the hands-on one — correct if not.)
S2 · Distributed-training stack — DDP vs FSDP vs DeepSpeed ZeRO, personally launched Act With Integrity; over-claiming multi-node FSDP gets caught on the follow-up "I've personally launched multi-GPU training with ___ (e.g. PyTorch DDP); I understand FSDP / ZeRO sharding for when the model won't fit on one device, though my hands-on runs were ___-scale." State single-node multi-GPU if that's the truth.
S3 · Live drift / canary / on-call — how much you've personally operated in production Act With Integrity; this is your known gap Don't claim it. Use the Honest MLOps-Gap Script (s2_15) verbatim: own the gap in one clause ("what I haven't personally run is a large-scale on-call rotation with live canary and real-time drift — my deployments have been research/shadow scale"), then pivot to efficiency. Know the machinery cold; never narrate it as lived experience.

3. Exact project facts — thresholds, architecture, deployment status

An informed interviewer digs here. State the real numbers; if you can't, describe the mechanism and offer to pull the figure — do not invent round numbers. Verified figures live in s2_41; match them verbatim.

What to supply Why it scores Safe default / how to phrase
F1 · The exact routing thresholds you used — quality-margin %, cost target, routing-overhead budget in ms Deliver With Impact; precise thresholds read as an operator, not a tourist State the real ones. If unsure, describe how you set them ("a per-slice quality floor with a routing-overhead budget") without quoting a number you can't defend.
F2 · MERA's architecture shape + the exact model pool Technical depth; this has caused a fix pass before Ground truth: MERA is "upfront route → verify → fallback," not a pure cascade — an input-only router (sees the serialized prompt + local context, not hidden agent state), a BERT-tiny classifier trained with weak-supervision cheap/strong labels, a SkillBook, and a Qwen2.5-Coder-1.5B LoRA student via GRPO. Confirm the exact model pool you used; don't guess model names in the room.
F3 · Deployment status — prod vs offline/shadow, and any traffic % Act With Integrity; never imply production you didn't run "This was an offline benchmark study" is a completely fine, honest answer — say the true one. The 53% is a dynamic-track training-utility result on a 100-case held-out split, not a live-production number. Do not imply live traffic or a traffic split you didn't run.
F4 · How you actually calibrated / thresholded — temperature scaling? per-slice threshold sweep on a dev set? verifier-confirmed labels? Technical depth; an informed interviewer digs here State the real method. If unsure, describe the family ("I'd sweep a per-slice threshold on a dev split and confirm against the verifier") rather than asserting one you didn't use.
F5 · Authorship scope — the slice you claim as "I," plus lead-author names/roles and the manual-audit scope (sample size, what you checked) Act With Integrity; you're co-author (3rd), not lead, on TwinRouterBench and MERA Say "I" only for the slices you drove (labeling protocol / cost-accounting / evaluation you can defend line-by-line); credit leads for program direction. Confirm the real names and the ~audit sample size before stating them — don't invent an audit metric in the room.
F6 · The "$200K/month, 70% routine traffic" figure — and any eBay-scale dollar translation Act With Integrity; it's an external citation, not your result If you use it: "public reports put enterprise NLP serving north of…" — never imply it's your measurement. Keep any eBay dollar/GPU translation directional ("at real serving volume, halving cost on routable traffic is a seven-figure line item"), never a specific eBay infra number you can't defend.
