Overview & Study Plan

The front door to your Stage-2 prep. Read this first, then re-skim it the morning of each interview to reload what each round is actually scoring.

You passed Stage 1. Stage 2 is three 60-minute interviews, all in one loop:

  • (1) Live Coding & Engineering — plain Python, no AI in the room.
  • (2) ML System Design — you drive, argued from your own head.
  • (3) Project Management & Retrospective — own a project start to finish.

No AI in the room means your edge is real depth, not tool-assisted breadth. This doc tells you exactly what each round measures, the few ways strong candidates still lose it, your opening lines, the one rule you never break, and a 5-day plan to walk in loaded.

The 3-round loop at a glance

Round Format reality What it's really testing
1. Live Coding & Engineering ~5 min intro → ~35–45 min live Python coding (1 problem, sometimes 2) → ~15–25 min rapid engineering Q&A → your questions. Shared editor, AI off, possibly no run button. Can you write clean, correct Python while narrating, and can you talk production ML (docker, testing, training + inference optimization, deploy, monitoring) like someone who's shipped?
2. ML System Design 60 min, open-ended, you drive. Interviewer probes wherever you go shallow. Marketplace + ML flavored (search re-ranking, recommendations, fraud), not generic distributed systems. Do you think like a production engineer — business metric → data → model → serving → monitoring → trade-offs — or like a researcher who jumps to architecture?
3. PM & Retrospective Two halves: present one project start→finish (~12–13 min walkthrough) then STAR behavioral against eBay's 5 values, with deep probing on your decisions. Can you own a project end to end, quantify impact, say "I" not "we", and tell a real failure story without overclaiming?

Don't blow the hour on the code. Round 1 was deliberately split into "coding" + "engineering Q&A" because both are scored. If you burn 55 minutes on the problem, you forfeit a block you can win.

What each round scores — the criteria in plain behaviors

The recruiter's five named criteria (Communication, Problem Solving, Coding/edge-cases, Complexity, Debugging) are the standard coding rubric. They primarily grade Round 1's coding half — but Communication and Problem Solving follow you into all three rounds. Here's what a high score looks and sounds like:

Criterion "Green" behavior the interviewer is watching for
Communication Restate the problem in your words and confirm before coding. Think out loud continuously — state the approach before you type, narrate trade-offs while you type. No silent stretches; if you need to think, say "let me think for 20 seconds."
Problem Solving A consistent template: restate → constraints → propose approach → code → validate. Baseline first, then optimize ("here's the O(n²), it's slow because…, now the hashmap version"). Justify each data-structure choice aloud.
Coding (edge cases & bugs) Runnable, readable Python — clear names, helper functions, "code that could go to prod." Enumerate edge cases before and after coding: empty, single element, large N, negatives, ties, duplicates, None. Walk one concrete example through by hand.
Complexity State time and space Big-O unprompted. Compare to the brute force. Name the trade-off you chose ("O(n) space to buy O(1) lookup"). Have the next optimization ready if asked "can you do better?"
Debugging When a test fails, don't panic-rewrite. Read the failure → form a hypothesis out loud → isolate (print/assert, check boundaries) → fix. A bug you catch and reason through often scores higher than silent perfect code.

The sixth, unnamed dimension (Round 1's Q&A half): production fluency. Know the concept, give the real trade-off, and where you lack hands-on scale, say so and pivot to efficiency. That pivot is scripted — see below.

How strong ML candidates still fail each round

For each round: the top ~3 ways people lose it, and your counter.

Round 1 — Live Coding & Engineering

  • Silent coding. A correct-but-silent solution still scores mid, because Communication is named. → Narrate before you type; think out loud.
  • Jumping to code without clarifying. Signals you don't gather requirements. → Restate + ask about input ranges, types, invalid input, scale — then code.
  • Bluffing the engineering half. Invented ops experience unravels under one follow-up. → Concept + real trade-off, then the honest pivot to efficiency (the gap script).

Round 2 — ML System Design

  • Sounding like a researcher, not a production engineer — the #1 silent failure. Jumping to model architecture, skipping the data pipeline, forgetting monitoring/drift is the fastest signal you've never run something in production. This is exactly your known-gap surface, so neutralize it on purpose: open with the business metric, clarify scale/latency/freshness, spend real time on data → features → splits, and always close with drift + shadow/canary + retraining. Lead with GMV/CTR/latency SLA, never with a model.
  • Skipping the data pipeline. 80–90% of ML failures are data issues; glossing over sources, features, leakage, and time-based splits reads as inexperience. → Budget real minutes for data.
  • No monitoring / rollout plan. Marks you as not production-ready. → End every design with PSI/KS drift, shadow→canary→A/B, rollback, retraining cadence.

Round 3 — PM & Retrospective

  • Rambling setup, thin on your actions. The interviewer can't tell what you did. → 20% setup / 80% action + outcome; say "I".
  • No quantified impact. Fails "Deliver With Impact." → Lead with the numbers (see Own-Papers Ground Truth for the exact figures — quote them, never round or reinvent them).
  • Overclaiming production ops / no failure story. Punished under probing and "Act With Integrity"; and the round literally asks for a retrospective. → Own the ops gap honestly, and bring one real bet-that-didn't-pan-out + the systemic change it produced.

"Tell me about yourself" — your 60–90s opener

The single most predictable opening line of Rounds 1 and 3. This is distinct from the ~12-min project walkthrough — it's a tight headline reel, not the deep dive. Structure: one identity line → the systems-race thesis in one sentence → three proof-points → one line of why-eBay. Rehearse it to a stopwatch and land it at 60–90s; if it runs to two minutes you've turned an opener into a monologue.

Say it like this:

I'm an ML engineer and researcher focused on model efficiency — training, routing, and compression. My whole thesis is that the next decade is a systems race, not a scaling race: the winner isn't the biggest model, it's whoever trains, routes, compresses, and serves models most efficiently under real constraints. Three things I've built that prove it: a routing system that cut serving cost >90% at iso-quality; unstructured pruning that removed >80% of a segmentation model's weights while proxy Dice stayed >0.95 across four datasets; and a diffusion-based fine-tuning method that hit ~3.2× wall-clock speedup. I want to do that at production scale, and eBay is one of the few marketplaces where efficiency at scale is the business — your own stack already goes that way, so it's a direct fit.

Why a company now — why leave the independent path

Expect "why leave independent research?" or "why eBay now?" in Round 3. Have this rehearsed — it's also the honest frame for the MLOps gap.

Say it like this:

Independent research let me prove the thesis end to end — but it capped me at research and shadow-eval scale. I could show routing, pruning, and generative-training acceleration work; I couldn't run them against live traffic, real SLAs, and a retraining loop. The deliberate next step is a place where the systems-race thesis is the business model, run at production scale — and that's exactly eBay. I'll be honest that production MLOps is my thinner surface; that gap is why this role is the next move, not a reason against it.

Your core message — open with this

Two sentences. Say them early in Round 2 or 3, and let them frame everything after.

Say it like this:

The industry spent five years on a scaling race — bigger models, more tokens, more GPUs. I think the next decade is a systems race: the winner won't have the biggest model, it'll be whoever can train, route, compress, deploy, and apply models most efficiently under real constraints — which is exactly the work I've done, and exactly what a marketplace at eBay's scale needs.

Everything you'll present — routing, compression, generative-training acceleration — is one argument: small, specialized, and well-served beats big and general on cost at matched quality. eBay's own stack already agrees with you (a finetuned Gemma3-4B beating a zero-shot 27B at ~3.8× faster inference; LiLiuM's tokenizer at ~34% faster generation). You're not pitching against their roadmap — you're already on it. (Details in eBay AI Dossier.)

The one rule (do not break it)

Never invent a specific. No number, name, date, result, or quote you can't stand behind. When you don't know something about your own work — an exact threshold, a stakeholder's reaction, whether a system was live or shadow — caveat first: say what you do know, then name the boundary. "Caveat-first" beats bluffing in every round, and it's literally rewarded under "Act With Integrity."

The single place this bites hardest is the MLOps question — "how much production ops have you actually done?" You have one rehearsed answer for it, used verbatim everywhere it comes up. Read and memorize The Honest MLOps-Gap Script before the loop; don't improvise this one.

Your canonical figures (MERA, LLM Router, TwinRouterBench, Diffusion Weights, pruning) live in Own-Papers Ground Truth — that doc is the source of truth. If your memory and that doc ever disagree, the doc wins.

5-day drill plan

Compressed, front-loaded on your weaker surfaces (the engineering Q&A and the "production engineer" framing), and heavy on practice out loud — reading is not rehearsing.

Day Read Practice (out loud / unaided)
1 — Message & facts Overview & Study Plan (this), eBay AI Dossier, Own-Papers Ground Truth Say the core message and the honest MLOps-gap script 3× each until they're muscle memory. Recite your 5 canonical numbers cold.
2 — Coding round Live-Coding Protocol, ML Primitives From Scratch, Coding Practice Regimen 3–4 unaided problems, narrating the whole time, no run button — type, then hand-trace. Write softmax+cross-entropy and scaled dot-product attention from memory.
3 — Engineering Q&A Engineering Q&A: Training & Infra, Engineering Q&A: Serving, Deployment & Monitoring, The Honest MLOps-Gap Script Rapid-fire self-quiz: docker multi-stage, train/serve skew, quantization tiers, KV-cache/PagedAttention, PSI thresholds, shadow→canary→A/B. Rehearse the gap script until it's one clean breath.
4 — System design ML System Design Framework, Flagship: Search Ranking & Recsys, System Design Skeletons Bank, The Scaling Playbook One full mock design out loud, whiteboard, ~45 min: "design near-real-time re-ranking for eBay search." Force yourself to open with the business metric and close with monitoring. Then walk the 6-axis "now 10× it."
5 — Project & close Project Walkthrough: Cost-Efficient LLM Routing, Behavioral & STAR Library, Questions to Ask & Closing, Final-Hours Cheatsheet Rehearse the 8-beat walkthrough timed to ~12 min, "I" not "we." Drill 2–3 STAR stories mapped to eBay values. Fill in every [CONFIRM TONY] gap in the walkthrough with your real facts.

Morning of: re-skim this doc's scoring tables + the Final-Hours Cheatsheet. Then trust the reps.

You're interviewing from strength. You have owned the efficiency stack end to end — routing, pruning, training acceleration — and that's precisely where the industry, and eBay, is heading. Round 1 is winnable with clean narration. Round 2 is yours the moment you frame like a production engineer. Round 3 is a story you've already lived. Prepare the gaps honestly, rehearse out loud, and let the systems-race thesis carry the room.
