Design Framework & How to Drive

The method doc for Interview 2 (ML System Design). Read this first, before the worked designs — it's the spine every design applies. Internalize the skeleton and the three "how to drive" moves until they're automatic, then drill the flagship and skeletons on top of it.

Interview 2 is open-ended and you drive. There's no single right answer — the graders watch how you think: do you clarify before designing, anchor on a business metric, reason about trade-offs at every layer, and sound like someone who has shipped and operated systems? This doc gives you (1) the reusable skeleton, (2) how to run the session, (3) the latency-budget method, (4) the red flags that fail the round, and (5) where to go for the worked material.

The one mantra

Think like a production engineer, not a researcher.

This is the #1 silent failure in this round. A researcher answer optimizes a metric, reaches for the fanciest model, and stops at "it works offline." A production-engineer answer starts from the business outcome, treats data/labels/serving/monitoring as first-class, quantifies latency and cost, and names what breaks and how you'd know. Same knowledge, different framing — and the framing is what's scored.

Every time you're about to say "I'd use model X," pause and ask yourself the production version: what does this cost to serve, how do I get labels, how do I know it's working in prod, what happens when it drifts? Say that part out loud.

The reusable skeleton (11 layers)

This applies to nearly every ML system. In the room you adapt and trim — never robotically march through all eleven. State the spine in ~60 seconds, then let the interviewer's interest steer where you go deep.

# Layer The core move
0 Clarify Scope, scale, latency SLA, cost budget, existing data — before any architecture
1 Metrics Business → ML → offline proxy. Name the north-star and its guardrails together
2 Data & labels Where labels come from, and how they're biased (position/selection/delay)
3 Features Categories (query/item/user/cross), learned vs. exact-match, freshness tiers
4 Candidate generation Two-stage: cheap retrieval over the huge space → recall@K
5 Ranking Precision over the shortlist; multi-task + calibrated when scores feed downstream
6 Training Loss, negatives/sampling bias, infra, the key hyperparameter decisions
7 Offline eval Held-out that mirrors prod (temporal split), full-pipeline, strong baseline
8 Serving Latency budget per service, cost levers, the two-stage cascade
9 Experimentation Offline → interleaving → A/B. Never ship on offline alone
10 Monitoring & drift Input drift, decision/output drift, business KPI; PSI/KS; scheduled retrains
— Trade-offs At every layer — this is the connective tissue, not a final section

0. Clarify (never skip — jumping to architecture is an instant ding)

Open with these. Ask the ones that actually change the design, then state the assumptions you'll proceed on so you keep momentum.

  1. What business objective does this serve? (GMV, engagement, cost, trust/safety)
  2. Who's the user and what's the interaction pattern? (buyer/seller/internal; one-shot vs. session)
  3. What's the scale? (QPS, catalog size, user base, data freshness)
  4. What's the latency SLA? (p50/p99; sync vs. async; real-time vs. batch)
  5. What's the cost budget? (GPU/hour, per-query inference cost, API ceiling)
  6. What data already exists? (logs, labels, catalog/KG, third-party signals)
  7. What does success look like in 6 months, quantified?
  8. Any regulatory/fairness constraints? (PII, GDPR/DSA in the EU, seller fairness)

eBay defaults to assume out loud (unless told otherwise): ~2B active listings, ~135M MAU, hundreds of QPS on core paths, EN/DE/FR/ES/IT primary markets, and real cost sensitivity (eBay ships efficiency work in production — e.g. LiLiuM's custom tokenizer at ~34% faster generation is operational, not academic). Stating these signals you know the terrain.

1. Metrics — business → ML → offline proxy

Define metrics before designing anything, in three descending layers, and always name a guardrail:

  • Business (north-star): GMV per search session, conversion, cost per query, listing-creation rate. Lead here.
  • Online ML (A/B signal): CTR, add-to-cart, task-completion — with guardrails (bounce/pogo-sticking, seller-concentration Gini, latency p99, cost per query).
  • Offline proxy (training/eval signal): NDCG@K / MRR for ranking, AUC/F1 (PR-AUC when positives are sparse), Recall@K for retrieval, chrF/COMET for MT, ECE for calibration.

Say it like this: "I'd optimize GMV per search session, not raw CTR — CTR rewards clickbait. My guardrails are purchase rate, no increase in seller-impression concentration, and p99 latency. Offline I'll track NDCG@10 on graded relevance as a proxy, but I treat proxy-vs-north-star divergence as the classic failure and re-validate the proxy against the online metric periodically."

Trap: optimizing a single metric with no guardrail. Always name at least one side-effect you'd protect against.

2. Data & labels — where everyone hand-waves

Behavioral logs are the only signal at scale, and they're biased. Name the bias and a fix:

Bias Mechanism Fix
Position Top slots clicked regardless of relevance IPS-weight by 1/propensity; estimate propensity via randomization on a small traffic slice
Selection Only shown items can be clicked; unshown are unlabeled, not negative Counterfactual/off-policy eval; exploration budget so some propensities are clean
Feedback delay Purchase lags click by days Multi-task with a click proxy + purchase target; delayed-feedback correction

The honest move: "Clicks train the model; graded editorial relevance judges it. I never let the biased signal be the final arbiter." Two label regimes, reconciled — that one sentence lands.

3. Features

Query, item, user, and cross features. Two principles worth stating: prefer learned embeddings for semantic similarity, but keep exact-match signals (BM25 term overlap, category/brand exact match) as separate features — neural doesn't beat keyword matching for product IDs and brand names. And tier features by freshness (static attributes: hours OK; item CTR/CVR: minutes; session context: seconds) rather than treating them uniformly.

4–5. Two-stage candidate-gen + ranking (mandatory at scale)

For any huge item space (eBay: ~2B listings) you cannot run an expensive model over the full catalog — so retrieve-then-rank:

  • Candidate generation (recall@K): multi-channel — dense two-tower ANN + BM25 (exact codes/brands) + item-to-item CF — fused (RRF as v1; learned per-query weights as v2). Narrow N=2B → K≈100–1000.
  • Ranking (precision): expensive model on the shortlist only. Multi-task heads (click/cart/purchase) blended to a business-aligned score; calibrate the moment the score feeds a threshold, auction, or blend.

Say it like this: "A cross-encoder over 2B items at 50ms each is physically impossible, so it's two-stage: a cheap retriever gets recall to ~1000 candidates, and I spend the expensive cross-attention only on a manageable shortlist. This isn't eBay-specific — it's standard LTR at Google, Amazon, Airbnb."

The training details that decide retrieval quality (in-batch negatives, logQ correction, hard-negative mining) and the multi-task/calibration depth live in Flagship: Search Ranking & Recs — don't re-derive them here, point to them.

6. Training

Name the loss (pointwise BCE / pairwise BPR / listwise LambdaRank for ranking; contrastive with hard negatives for two-tower), the infra shape (FSDP/ZeRO for large models; distributed GBDT for feature-heavy rankers), and the load-bearing hyperparameter decisions. For LLM/domain-adaptation designs, the eBay-published levers are worth citing: continued pretraining at LR = 10% of original to avoid catastrophic forgetting, and a 50/50 domain/general data mix (e-Llama). Attribute those as eBay's published findings.

7. Offline eval

Four principles: temporal split (train old, test new — not random) to avoid leakage; evaluate the full pipeline, not just the model (a great ranker on a bad candidate set fails); match the metric to the serving scenario (serve top-5 → measure NDCG@5); and compare against a strong baseline (BM25 or the current production model, not random).

8. Serving, latency, cost

See The latency-budget method below — always decompose per service. Cost levers, least-quality-impact first: semantic/prefix caching (~0 impact) → quantization (AWQ 4-bit, <0.5%) → speculative decoding (real-time path only) → route small-model-first → batch async → distill a specialist. This ladder is your edge — steer toward it.

9. Experimentation — the funnel, not just A/B

Three phases, and naming the middle one signals maturity:

  1. Offline — NDCG/MRR/recall on graded relevance + replayed logs. Cheap, weakly predictive. Gate: don't ship a regression.
  2. Interleaving — mix rankers A and B into one list per user, attribute clicks to the source. Removes between-user variance → ~10–100× more sensitive than A/B, far faster. Lets a research team iterate weekly, not monthly. Limit: it measures ranking preference, not downstream business/marketplace effects — so it's a fast filter, not a replacement.
  3. A/B — winners graduate to a real A/B on the business metric, user-level randomization, ≥2 weeks for weekly seasonality, per-market splits (DE/FR/ES/IT differ). Canary 1% → 10% → 50% → 100%.

10. Monitoring & drift

Monitor three layers: input (feature drift via PSI/KS — alarm PSI > 0.2; null-rate spikes), model (calibration/ECE, an online quality proxy on a fresh held-out slice), and business (GMV/session, seller-Gini, p99). E-commerce drifts hard around holidays/seasonal events — expect scheduled retrains and use time-aware features.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: This is the layer that touches your known MLOps gap. Don't overclaim live drift-response experience — pair it with the honest-gap script (see The Honest MLOps-Gap Script) if asked how much you've personally run in production.

How to drive the session

Lead with a business metric, never architecture

The single strongest opening move: name the business outcome and its SLA/guardrails in your first two sentences, then work down to ML metrics and only then to architecture. Candidates who open with "I'd use a two-tower model" have already signaled researcher-brain.

Say it like this (opener template): "Before I design anything — the goal here is [GMV per search session / listing-creation rate / cost per GenAI request], under a [p99 / cost] budget of roughly [X]. I'll optimize that, guardrailed by [Y]. Let me confirm scale and constraints, then I'll lay out a retrieve-then-rank spine and we can go deep wherever you want."

The clarifying questions to open with

Use the Step-0 list. You don't have to ask all eight — ask the two or three that most change the design (scale, latency SLA, cost budget are almost always load-bearing), then state your assumptions and move. Asking questions forever is as bad as asking none; the skill is asking the ones that matter and proceeding decisively.

Narrate the spine, then let them steer

After clarifying, give the 60-second spine of the whole system, then stop and say "where would you like me to go deep?" This shows structure and hands them the wheel — they're grading whether you can drive, not whether you monologue for 45 minutes. When they probe a node, expand it; when they don't, keep moving.

Trade-offs at every layer

Every choice you make, say the alternative and why you chose this one: two-stage vs. one-stage cross-encoder, hybrid vs. dense-only retrieval, MMoE vs. shared-bottom, in-house model vs. external API. A design narrated as a series of justified trade-offs reads as senior; a design narrated as a sequence of "I'd use X" reads as memorized.

When they say "now 10× it"

Don't panic-redesign. Walk the six scaling axes — data → model → training → serving → cost → org — and for each say what breaks first and the cheapest lever. Full shape lives in The Scaling Playbook; steer the answer toward cost/serving efficiency, which is your genuine edge.

The latency-budget method

Never quote one global latency number. The instant an interviewer gives you a budget, decompose it per service — that's the tell of someone who has actually profiled a serving path.

The key framing to say out loud: search end-to-end is ~500 ms; the ranking model itself is only ~120–200 ms. These aren't in conflict — they're different services in the same request. Always draw the breakdown. (Dense ANN retrieval isn't a standalone global number either — it's ~tens of ms inside the retrieval slice of that budget, not a headline figure to quote on its own.)

Search end-to-end budget: ~500ms p99  (ranking model's slice is ~120–200ms of it)
├─ Network + API gateway ............ 10ms
├─ Query understanding .............. 20ms   (lang-ID <1ms, spell 5ms, embed ~15ms)
├─ Candidate retrieval .............. 30ms   (BM25 ~5ms ∥ dense ANN ~tens of ms + merge)
├─ Feature assembly ................. 20ms
├─ L1 ranking (deep CTR / GBDT) ..... 30ms
├─ L2 rerank (cross-encoder top-k) .. 50ms   ← the wildcard; drop first if tight
├─ Business rules / diversity ....... 10ms
└─ Serialization .................... 10ms

How to use it in the room:

  1. State the end-to-end SLA and which service you're budgeting.
  2. Break it down into stages with a number each.
  3. Identify the wildcard (usually the L2 reranker or an LLM call) and how you'd cap it (input-length cap, distillation, skip-if-budget-tight).
  4. Give the shed-load order if over budget: drop L2 → shrink candidate count → lighter encoder → cache popular queries.

For LLM-serving budgets, decompose differently — prefill (compute-bound, 100–500ms) vs. decode (bandwidth-bound, per-token) — and note LLM features (Magical Listing, agentic) are always async, never on the synchronous search path.

Trap: answering "200ms" or "500ms" as a single number. It reads as never having profiled a real pipeline. Decompose, every time.

Red flags that fail the round

Any one of these can sink an otherwise strong design. Self-check against this list mid-session.

Red flag What it looks like The fix
Jump to model "I'd use a transformer…" before clarifying scale/SLA/data Clarify first; lead with the business metric
Skip data / labels Designing the model without saying where labels come from or how they're biased Name label source + position/selection/delay bias + a fix each
No monitoring Design "ends" at offline eval; nothing about prod Add input/decision/business drift monitoring and retraining triggers
No metric Optimizing "quality" with no business north-star or guardrail Business → ML → offline proxy, with a named guardrail
No trade-offs A sequence of "I'd use X" with no alternatives weighed Every choice: state the alternative and why you chose this
One global latency number "It'll be under 500ms" with no breakdown Decompose the budget per service
Over-engineering Reaching for generative retrieval / a giant LLM when a two-tower + GBDT wins Start simple, justify complexity only when the simple baseline is shown to fail
Researcher-brain Fancy model, offline-only, no cost/ops thinking The mantra: production engineer, not researcher

Over-engineering deserves a special note: proposing the flashiest architecture unprompted is not impressive here — it reads as someone who hasn't shipped. Propose the boring, correct baseline first; earn the complexity by showing the baseline's limit. Save the frontier answer (generative retrieval / semantic IDs) for when they explicitly ask "where's this going in 2 years."

Map to the other design docs

This framework is the spine. Apply it through the worked material:

  • Flagship: Search Ranking & Recs — the deep end-to-end recsys/search design (two-tower training & sampling bias, multi-task + calibration, point-in-time feature stores, offline→interleaving→A/B, the 60-second spine). Your flagged weak spot; drill it until airtight.
  • Design Skeletons Bank — frame + components + tricky-bits for the designs the framework alone doesn't work through: LLM serving + routing (your edge), RAG product-Q&A, LLM-as-judge harness, fraud/anomaly, query understanding, ads ranking, Magical Listing (VLM + KG), MT, e-Llama continued pretraining, generative retrieval.
  • The Scaling Playbook — the reusable 6-axis "now 10× it" answer, ending each axis on "what breaks first + cheapest lever."
  • The Honest MLOps-Gap Script — the one rehearsed answer for "how much production MLOps have you run?" — owns the on-call/live-drift gap and pivots to efficiency. Reach for it whenever the monitoring/ops layer gets probed.
