STAR Stories — The Four Prompts

Your spoken story bank for the Stage-2 behavioral prompts the email named. Read it aloud in the days before; drill the P2 gap hardest. Each story is ~2–3 min spoken, grounded in verified numbers, with a one-line delivery cue and the trap to dodge.

How to use this doc

The recruiter's email flagged four behavioral prompts. For each you get a PRIMARY and a BACKUP story, each as a four-box STAR (Situation / Task / Action / Result), a one-line "how to say it," and a Trap. Then a leadership-style answer, a genuine-failure story, and a consistency note.

Three standing rules, same as the rest of the pack:

  • Number-caveats go FIRST, in the opening sentence of the Result ("no measured speedup," "in-distribution only," "no cumulative gain"). Leading with the caveat reads as rigor, not weakness.
  • Byline humility (3rd of 17, 4th of 8, co-first author) goes once, plainly, near the end — never as your opener. Don't start a story by deflating yourself.
  • > ⚠ [CONFIRM TONY] marks a human detail — a named person, a real reaction, an outcome — that is NOT in your written record. Fill each before the room. An honest "I'd have to check the specifics" beats a confident invention that collapses on the first probe.

Delivery reflex for every story: Situation + Task ≤ ~40s, whole answer ~2 min, then stop and let them cut in. Say "I," not "we," in the Action. If a number won't come: "I don't have that in front of me — directionally it was…"

P1 — A challenging project you delivered

PRIMARY · VR eye-tracking emotion dataset (the hardware wall)

How to say it: "The hardest thing I've shipped end-to-end was a VR eye-tracking emotion dataset — and what nearly sank it was a hardware limit, not the modeling."

Situation. As co-first author at TU Delft, I set out to read emotion from the eyes in VR using only the sensors a headset already carries — no external physiological rig. The wall hit early: our headset's built-in Tobii tracker only exposed derived gaze — processed numbers — while the raw periocular video the whole idea depended on was locked behind a commercial SDK license.

Task. Deliver a clean, publishable multimodal dataset plus a benchmark recognition model — spanning hardware integration, a labeling pipeline, human-subjects studies, and modeling — on one timeline, with a co-first author and a senior external collaborator I couldn't direct.

Action. Rather than buy our way out, I integrated a Pupil Labs add-on to capture the raw near-eye video directly. I built the synchronization and labeling pipeline around it, including an open-source Unity collection-and-labeling tool so the protocol was reproducible. I designed a seven-emotion stimulus protocol, ran two participant studies, coordinated the two collaborators as a peer rather than a boss, and built the fusion model — a ViViT video encoder fused with a time-series transformer over gaze and pupil.

Result. Caveat first: it's a modest 26-participant set, shared on request under an agreement rather than as an open download, and our fusion actually loses slightly at the shortest half-second window — which we reported rather than buried. With that said: seven emotions captured with raw high-frame-rate periocular video, the Unity tool released open-source, and the paper published at IMWUT 2025. As co-first author, my owned slice was the hardware, the data pipeline, and the model.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: one concrete coordination moment — a design call you and your co-first author (Bishwas Regmi) or the senior collaborator (Andreas Bulling) disagreed on, and how you steered it — if you want this to also fire for "leadership/coordination."

Trap: Don't call it "the first large-scale public dataset" or imply it's an open download — it's 26 participants, shared on request under an agreement. Lead with the win (seven emotions, raw periocular, IMWUT), put the size/sharing caveat in the Result, keep byline humility to one clean line.

BACKUP · Pruning nnU-Net into MIDL on a hard deadline

How to say it: "The cleanest fixed-deadline delivery I've done was getting my nnU-Net pruning work into MIDL — an external deadline that could not move, from a partially-explored problem."

Situation. As first author, I had a fixed, immovable MIDL Short Papers deadline to take a partly-explored question — how much of a trained nnU-Net, the standard medical-segmentation model, is actually redundant — all the way to a complete four-page contribution. And these benchmarks don't release test-set labels, so I had no ground truth to measure pruning damage against.

Task. Design the method, run every experiment across four datasets and five cross-validation folds, and write it to a finished short-paper standard — on a deadline that couldn't slip.

Action. I front-loaded the riskiest decisions. I scoped tight — one-shot magnitude pruning, no fine-tuning — so I was measuring the network's own redundancy, not a retraining trick. To score without labels I defined a proxy Dice (agreement between the pruned and unpruned model) and anchored it on ACDC, the one dataset with public labels, checking the two curves tracked together. I worked backward from the deadline with internal milestones and a buffer, front-loaded the heaviest compute runs to beat GPU-queue contention, and made sure I had baseline results I could publish regardless of whether the most aggressive result landed.

Result. Caveat first: this is unstructured pruning, so there is no measured speedup — the footprint stays dense. The finding: over 80% of weights come out while proxy Dice stays above 0.95, across four datasets, with an inverted-U pattern showing the deep, parameter-heavy layers are the most prunable. Submitted on time, accepted at MIDL 2025, first author. I frame real latency wins as the structured follow-up the redundancy map enables — not something I've delivered.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: your actual milestone schedule and cadence — the real timeline, whether you used explicit buffer/off-peak runs, and who you reported milestones to (e.g. advisor Qian Tao). Replace the directional version above with what you actually did.

Trap: Never let "99% sparsity" or "6× speedup" back into your mouth — both are wrong (99.1% is only one dataset at the most aggressive, high-degradation threshold; there is no speedup number in the paper). The defensible headline is ">80% removed at proxy Dice >0.95, unstructured, no measured speedup." Misquoting your own paper is the one truly costly mistake here.

P2 — A difficult interaction with a customer / vendor / stakeholder

⚠ This is your thinnest real material — prep it hardest. You have never managed a vendor or an end customer commercially, and there is no documented difficult interpersonal episode in your record. Below are two honest options — an academic-collaboration version and an industry-collaboration version. Pick the one that maps to a real memory and fill every [CONFIRM TONY] with what actually happened. Do NOT present invented interpersonal specifics as fact.

⚠ LOUD WARNING — read before answering P2. If you cannot fill a real Option-A or Option-B specific (an actual person + what they pushed for + how it resolved), do NOT fall back on "peer-review pushback." Answering a reviewer's comment is not a stakeholder relationship, and a tough interviewer will reject it as off-prompt. Instead, redirect to a named cross-discipline alignment moment — the strongest is Reverse Imaging (you as the ML voice aligning with the MR-physicist team when RI-T2S underperformed and the physics side vs. the ML side each could assume it was the other's bug). That is a real "difficult external party" you had to align without authority. Name the collaborator and how you reached agreement.

The honest bridge (say this first if framed as "customer/vendor"):

Say it like this: "I'll be upfront — I haven't managed external vendors or end customers in a commercial role; my one conventional industry role was support engineering at NXP. My closest honest analog is a research collaboration where the hard part wasn't a personality clash — it was holding the line on what the work could truthfully claim when a partner wanted a bigger promise. The discipline transfers: read their real goal, show the data behind the limit, and offer a narrower thing you can actually defend."

That reframe is both true and exactly the competency they're probing.

OPTION A · Academic collaboration — a disagreement on scope / direction / authorship

How to say it: "The most real 'difficult external party' I can point to is a research collaboration where a co-author and I genuinely disagreed on scope — and separately, peer review, where I had to win a skeptical reviewer on the merits, not by authority."

Situation. On a published paper I worked with collaborators who didn't report to me and whom I couldn't overrule. The tension I'd describe:

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: the real friction. Which collaboration, and what was the disagreement — a co-author or advisor pushing for a broader claim / a faster headline-number evaluation / a different authorship or scope call, while you wanted tighter rigor? Name the person and what they literally argued for. (Grounded anchors you can stand on: you published nnU-Net at MIDL and the eye-tracking paper at IMWUT, so you provably received real reviewer pushback and answered it in a rebuttal.)

Task. Resolve the disagreement without damaging a peer relationship, and land the paper on a footing I could defend under scrutiny.

Action. My move in these situations is to depersonalize the disagreement and let evidence settle it rather than seniority. The defensible technical anchor I can state, on nnU-Net: when the "your proxy just measures the model agreeing with itself" worry came up, I answered it by showing the real-Dice curve on ACDC tracking the proxy-Dice curve — so the proxy was calibrated against ground truth wherever it could be checked.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: what you actually did in the exchange — added an ablation, ran a control, tightened scope, proposed a parallel experiment to let the data decide? And how the human side landed.

Result. The honest, grounded landing: the rebuttal held and the paper published (MIDL / IMWUT 2025).

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: the specific outcome — did the reviewer or co-author concede, change a score, or ask for the exact control you ran? Name it, so this answers "a difficult interaction" and not just "I answered an anticipated objection."

Trap: Don't inflate a routine review comment into a dramatic clash, and don't claim a reviewer "conceded" if you can't recall it. Peer review is the safest grounded anchor precisely because the event is real — only the specifics need recall, not invention.

OPTION B · Industry collaboration — a partner's priorities vs. what the research needed

How to say it: "My most honest industry-stakeholder story is a pilot where the product side wanted a broader claim than the model could truthfully support — and the difficulty was managing that without damaging the relationship."

Situation. I ran an NDA pilot with an industry partner, fine-tuning a vision-language model to infer customer purchase intent — internally the "Human Intent World Model." The collaboration is real; the classic research-to-business tension was: I had a number I believed in, and a partner who understandably wanted the broadest possible claim from it.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: the actual friction. Did the product/business side push for a "works in any retail setting" claim, or for rule-based sales scripts, while you wanted to protect honest generalization? What did they literally say and ask for? (This applies whether the partner was MeetaVista, or a priority-misalignment with Gradient / Tencent wanting a marketing-ready result while the research needed a rigorous benchmark.)

Task. Deliver something technically sound, correct the scope of the claim, and set expectations that would survive real scrutiny — without burning the relationship.

Action. I held the line on what the result actually supported. My general play here is to show where the model holds up versus where it breaks and propose a narrower, defensible scope — the honest anchor is that the result is in-distribution only (measured against the same synthetic process it was trained on, with no agreed real-world ground truth), so any "works anywhere" claim would have been over-reach, and flagging exactly that is the honest move.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY] — the number itself. The "91.3%" figure is not in your verified written record. Do NOT speak it until you have confirmed it is a real logged metric with a definition you can defend. Safer default: tell this qualitatively — "a strong in-distribution number that did not generalize out of distribution" — and only quote a figure once you've verified it.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: what you actually did — walked them through in-distribution vs. out-of-distribution, proposed a "validated use cases" scope, ran a small real-world test? And what they literally pushed back with.

Result.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: how it resolved — did they accept the narrower scope, and what was their reaction / the human outcome? Do NOT use the "two investors praised the honest scoping" line — that detail is unverified and must not be stated as fact.

Trap: Any figure here (the "91.3%") is unverified — confirm it's a real logged number before you say it, and even then it's in-distribution only, so never let it travel as a general accuracy claim. Don't assert the model went live in a real sales setting unless you've confirmed it. And kill the "investors praised us" embellishment.

P3 — A time you used your technical expertise to advise

PRIMARY · TwinRouterBench — "measure it, don't argue it"

How to say it: "On our routing benchmark, the slice the team relied on me for was the evaluation methodology — and my core piece of advice was: build the measuring stick before you bet on any router."

Situation. TwinRouterBench scores LLM routing per step of a multi-step agent task. The measurement problem I owned: to label the cheapest model tier that still solves each step, the naive approach re-verifies every combination of tier choices across a trajectory — that's on the order of 4-to-the-N reruns for an N-step trajectory. You simply cannot build that corpus. I'm 3rd of 17 authors, a regular co-author, and I owned the labeling and cost-accounting.

Task. Make an impossible-to-build labeling space actually buildable, without fooling ourselves — and get the team to invest in rigorous evaluation instead of a quick headline number.

Action. I advised, and built, three things people often blur together. First, the labeling protocol — greedy sequential-locking: fix the verified tier at each step, lock it, move on, and accept a downgrade only if the full trajectory still passes end-to-end with the same step count. That collapses 4-to-the-N down to roughly 4-times-N, which is what made the corpus buildable at all — plus a ~10% manual audit to catch cross-step interactions greedy can miss. Second, failure-aware cost accounting, so a cheap router that breaks a step gets charged for the failure rather than rewarded for being cheap. The through-line of my advice was: a single router's number is unfalsifiable — you can't tell whether it won on the routing idea or the model pool — so measure comparatively before you commit.

Result. That protocol is what made the 970-step corpus buildable, and it's the slice the team leans on me for. To be precise on credit: I'm 3rd of 17, listed as an independent researcher — I own the labeling and cost-accounting, not the project framing or the headline experiments.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: name who actually received and adopted this advice — a co-author, one of the two equal-contribution leads (Pei Yang / Wanyi Chen)? — and what they changed, so this answers "advise a person," not just "I designed a method."

Trap: Don't lecture the interviewer about routing as if it's news to them — keep it to "here's the call I made and why," then hand the floor back. And keep the byline honest: 3rd of 17, methodology owner, not project lead.

BACKUP · The ML voice on a physics-led MRI team (Reverse Imaging)

How to say it: "A different flavor of advising-with-expertise is a cardiac-MRI project where I was the machine-learning voice on a team led by MR physicists — and my most useful contribution was refusing to hide where our method broke."

Situation. Reverse Imaging is a cardiac-MRI reconstruction project led by MR physicists, with NIH/NHLBI and Microsoft Research collaborators. I was 4th of eight, the CS/ML contributor advising across the ML-to-physics boundary, where the two sides genuinely speak different languages. One variant, RI-T2S, actually underperformed a plain baseline on short-echo-time gradient-echo data.

Task. Advise the team on why the method broke — in a cross-discipline setting where the physics side and the ML side can each quietly assume it's the other's bug — and steer toward something that would hold up.

Action. My advice was to treat the failure as signal, not embarrassment. The model was weakest exactly where the physics made the signal hardest — a very short echo time starves the inverse problem — so instead of forcing that method, we should invert the information-rich source data and synthesize training contrasts from it. That reasoning is what motivated the follow-up method, RI-Aug.

Result. It's an arXiv preprint with eight authors; my role was the ML advisory slice, not leading the physics. But naming precisely where the method broke, rather than burying it, is what redirected the design toward something that held up — RI-Aug ends up best on both evaluation sets.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: the specific advising exchange — which collaborator you persuaded or pushed back on, what they initially wanted, and how the conversation landed.

Trap: Its punchline is "our first method underperformed," so it under-sells as a pure "advise" story — use it for variety, and keep the byline (4th of 8, contributing co-author) near the end, not up front.

(Alternate if you want a non-ML-audience version: the MSCA wireless-sensing reframing — advising the team to move from a classification framing to a representation-learning / world-model framing before committing compute. Same "advise on the right framing before you build" shape; confirm the real advising exchange before telling it.)

P4 — A time you used data to influence a decision

PRIMARY · The ACDC ground-truth curve behind "80% removed"

How to say it: "The clearest time I used data to win an argument was making an aggressive compression claim believable — 'you removed 80% of the network and it still works' invites disbelief unless your measurement is airtight."

Situation. When I claimed over 80% of a trained nnU-Net could be removed, the natural objection is: "Your proxy Dice just measures the model agreeing with itself — that's circular." It's a fair objection, and it's the first thing a careful reviewer raises.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY] — NAME THE REAL PERSON (this is the crux of a "used data to influence" answer). Who actually voiced this doubt and then changed their mind — advisor Qian Tao, a specific co-author, or a reviewer? State (a) who they were, (b) what they believed before the anchor, and (c) what they did differently afterward (raised the score, dropped the objection, greenlit the framing, adopted proxy Dice). The data is grounded; the named, persuaded person is the gap that turns this from "I answered an anticipated objection" into "I changed a specific person's mind." Do not invent the name — confirm it.

Task. Make the compression claim credible to a skeptical audience — turn a suspicious-looking headline into a measured finding people would trust.

Action. I didn't lead with the proxy. I led with ACDC, the one dataset with public ground-truth labels. I put the real-Dice curve right alongside the proxy-Dice curve and showed they tracked each other — so I could say the proxy is calibrated against ground truth everywhere we can check it, which justifies trusting it where we can't. I also showed the inverted-U redundancy map, so the claim had a mechanism, not just a headline number.

Result. Caveat first: it's unstructured pruning, so no measured speedup. The win: that reframed it from a suspicious headline into a measured finding, and it's what carried the paper through MIDL review. The data did the persuading — I didn't argue the claim, I showed the two curves and let them land.

Trap: Proxy Dice is agreement with the unpruned model, not accuracy — only ACDC has a true-Dice curve. Never say "we kept 95% accuracy" or "<1% Dice drop across tasks"; that conflates proxy agreement with real accuracy and is the single biggest trap in this paper.

BACKUP · The accounting choice that flipped a router ranking (TwinRouterBench)

How to say it: "A sharper 'the data changed a decision' example: on our routing benchmark, one accounting choice flipped which router we treated as best."

Situation. We were ranking routers by cost savings. The subtlety: if you only count what a router saves and ignore the cost of the steps it gets wrong, a reckless cheap router looks like the winner.

Task. Get the team to rank routers on the correct metric rather than the flattering one — and back it with evidence, not just an assertion of principle.

Action. There are several ways to do that accounting. The naive variants rank a rule-based router, ClawRouter, on top. Only the failure-aware version — where a router that breaks the task is charged for the failed steps instead of credited for being cheap — flips it: SR-KNN at 56.18 over ClawRouter at 53.82. I argued the failure-aware version is the correct one on first principles: a router that saves money but breaks the task hasn't saved anything. I backed it with a second signal — a frontier model used as the router flagged only 7 of 147 verified-hard steps as high-difficulty, so a naive "just ask a smart model to route" baseline was quietly failing the work we'd assumed it did.

Result. That changed which router the team treated as the reference. Caveat I keep attached: it's a benchmark, and the labels are tied to a frozen model pool and a price snapshot, so the ranking is only valid under that snapshot. The fact that the ranking is metric-sensitive is itself a finding we chose to publish, not bury.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: name the colleague who initially favored the other ranking, to make this "influenced a person" rather than "influenced the team's decision."

Trap: If pushed with "variant E is cherry-picked — you picked the metric that makes your router win," answer on first principles, not on the ranking: charging failures is the correct accounting regardless of who wins, and the fact that the naive variants rank ClawRouter on top is itself the published finding. Don't improvise this one cold.

Leadership style (~150 words spoken)

How to say it: "I'll lead with the honest caveat — I've never had formal reports or line-managed anyone — so when I say 'leadership,' I mean three concrete things."

"First, leading without authority through co-first-authorship. On the eye-tracking paper I coordinated a co-first author and a senior external collaborator, people I couldn't direct, so the job was alignment, not instruction. Second, leading by example and by teaching — I was a TA and MSc mentor across about five courses at TU Delft, and the way I lead a mentee is to make the problem crisp and then let them own it. Third, self-direction: my independent program is a solo-prioritized backlog of ten to fifteen ideas, ranked by 'what would I learn?' and 'what's the smallest experiment that could prove me wrong?' If I compress it to one line: get to clarity fast, design the smallest falsifying experiment, and let the evidence — not seniority — settle it. I haven't managed people yet; that's a growth edge, not a claim."

Trap: Say the "no formal reports" frame once, early, calmly — it's a strength when you own it, not a weakness you're hiding. Don't over-apologize for it after.

Genuine failure — MERA's null result, and the metric that could have lied

How to say it: "The failure I'm most comfortable owning is the model-evolution half of MERA — it simply didn't work, and we chose to publish that rather than hide it. The deeper lesson changed how I measure things."

Situation. MERA is an agentic-routing framework with two halves: a routing half, and a model-evolution half where a small student model is meant to get cumulatively better as it sees more tasks. I'm 3rd of seven authors, listed as an independent researcher, and I was close to the results.

Task. Report both halves honestly — including the half that didn't deliver — and pull a durable lesson out of the failure rather than just filing it.

Action. The routing half held up: about 87.3% router-decision accuracy — I'm careful to say router accuracy, not task accuracy — at roughly 51.8% of always-large serving cost, with a 4.4% fallback. But the model-evolution half, a small student trained with reinforcement learning, showed no cumulative gain: its pass rate sat around 47%, essentially flat across cycles. The tempting move was to quietly drop that half for a cleaner story. We reported it instead, in the limitations. The systemic change I took from this line of work is to distrust aggregate metrics — an average can wash out a whole category quietly failing — so I now score per-slice and set a per-slice guardrail by default rather than trusting a single headline number.

Result. Caveat up front: that's a genuine null result — the evolution half doesn't yet earn its compute, and we say exactly that. We published it because a framework honest about which half works is more useful than a polished half-truth. The behavior change is the real payoff: measuring per-slice is now a default, not an afterthought.

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: the specific "averaging trap" episode — the concrete case where an aggregate metric hid a failing skill category and you switched to per-slice scoring. Anchor the told failure on the verified MERA null result (~47% flat, published); only add the per-slice-metric origin story as fact once you can name the real instance.

Trap: Say "router-decision accuracy," never "task accuracy" — 87.3% is the routing metric. End on the learning and the systemic change (per-slice scoring), not on the failure itself — the learning is the answer. And take a genuine 2–3s pause before answering a "what did you get wrong" question; an instant polished reply reads as unreflective.

Consistency note — don't tell one project two contradictory ways

Several projects legitimately serve more than one prompt. The risk isn't reuse — it's telling the same anecdote two contradictory ways in one hour. Lock these:

  • nnU-Net appears three times on purpose — P1 backup (delivering into MIDL against a measurement wall), P4 primary (the ACDC curve making "80% removed" credible), and folded into leadership/self-direction. Genuinely different facets. In one interview, pick the facet the question asks and don't tell two back-to-back. Constants every time: unstructured pruning, >80% of weights removed at proxy Dice >0.95, four datasets / five folds, ACDC = the labeled anchor, and NO measured speedup. Never say "99% sparsity" or "6× speedup."
  • TwinRouterBench also appears twice — P3 primary (the labeling / cost-accounting methodology you advise on) and P4 backup (the failure-aware accounting flip). Same project, different facet. If you've used it for P3, reach for the nnU-Net P4 primary first and keep the accounting story as the second example only if asked — don't run two TwinRouterBench stories back-to-back. Keep the byline constant: 3rd of 17, independent researcher, methodology owner.
  • The industry pilot (Human Intent World Model) underlies both P2-Option-B and any "regret" story — same single NDA pilot. The "91.3%" is NOT in your verified record — [CONFIRM TONY] it's a real logged figure before speaking it, and even then it's in-distribution only, so never let it travel as a general accuracy claim. Don't assert the model went live unless you've confirmed it. Do not use the unverified "investors praised the honest scoping" line anywhere.
  • Eye-tracking can serve P1 (hardware wall), leadership (co-first-author coordination), and a small honesty story (the half-second window where fusion loses). Pick one facet per question. Constants: 26 participants, shared on request under an agreement (not "first large-scale public dataset"), public artifact = the open-source Unity tool.

If any figure won't come cleanly, say "directionally…" rather than invent one. A confident wrong number is the single costliest mistake in the room.
