eBay Context (Stage-2 lens)

Your one-page map of eBay's AI stack — every number, and the one-line bridge from it to your own work. Read it early, then re-skim before each round so the facts and connections are loaded and ready to drop into conversation.

What this doc is for

Stage 1 was the research round (with Yannick Versley, who leads Foundation Models pretraining). Stage 2 is a different audience: an engineer/applied scientist (Round 1), a senior IC or lead (Round 2), and a hiring manager (Round 3). None of them wants a paper-by-paper deep dive. What they reward is you connecting your work to eBay's actual production problems — naturally, in a sentence, when the conversation opens the door.

So this doc is not a company dossier. It's the connective tissue: the stack, the numbers, and the bridge to your research. Drop these as bridges, not as a monologue.

Trap: don't recite the stack unprompted. These land when an interviewer's question opens the door ("how would you serve this cheaply?", "why not just use a big model?"). Then you have the bridge ready.

The one economic fact that frames everything

eBay is an asset-light marketplace — no inventory, no warehouses. ~$80B GMV in 2025 on ~$11.1B revenue, mostly transaction fees. So the unit economics are dominated by software and AI, not logistics: every percentage point of serving cost or listing quality flows straight to the bottom line.

That's why eBay's published AI work is obsessed with efficiency — and it's exactly your thesis: AI is moving from a scaling race to a systems race. eBay is one of the few places where that isn't a slogan, it's the business model.

The stack, and the bridge to your work

For each: what it is (with eBay's numbers), then Bridge: — how your work connects, in one line.

LiLiuM — eBay's in-house LLM family (eBay's published work)

Decoder-only, three sizes 1B / 7B / 13B, trained on 3T multilingual tokens, with a custom 65k e-commerce tokenizer. Because brand names, model numbers, and part numbers become single tokens instead of being split into sub-words, generation is up to ~34% faster than LLaMA-2 on eBay tasks. Built 100% in-house for data governance, licensing freedom, and serving efficiency.

Bridge: tokenizer efficiency and my routing attack the same cost from two different layers — they make each token cheaper; I make each query cheaper by sending the easy ones to a smaller model. They compose: route to a well-tokenized specialist and you multiply both wins.

e-Llama — domain-adapted Llama 3.1 (eBay's published work)

Continued pretraining of Llama-3.1 8B and 70B on 1T e-commerce tokens at LR = 10% of the original pretraining rate — the key knob for adapting a strong base model without catastrophic forgetting. A 50% e-com / 50% general data mix, plus model merging (simple parameter averaging) that gives a near-linear domain-vs-general dial at zero extra compute.

Bridge: this is cheap adaptation done right — the LR=10% call is the same instinct behind my Diffusion Weights work (~3.2× wall-clock fine-tune speedup): the win is in how you adapt, not how big you go.

The e-commerce VLM — small-finetuned beats big-zero-shot (eBay's published work) — flagship bridge

LLaVA-style VLMs for e-commerce. The headline result: a finetuned Gemma3-4B reaches F1 50.5, beating a zero-shot Gemma3-27B at 44.8 — higher quality — while running ~3.8× faster. Using a domain-adapted decoder (e-Llama) adds another 5–12% on e-commerce tasks.

Bridge: this is my entire thesis in one data point — a small, domain-adapted model dominates a big general one on quality and cost. It's the same economics as my LLM Router (>90% cost reduction at matched quality) and MERA (87.3% router-decision accuracy at 51.8% serving cost): stop paying for capacity the query doesn't need.

Mercury — agentic platform for LLM-powered recommendations (eBay's work)

An internal agentic framework that runs a fleet of models in parallel — fine-tuned Llama, Mistral, LiLiuM internally, plus external GCP/Azure/OpenAI APIs — with "prompts as code," modular agents, and chains. Today model selection is rule-based / priority-queue-based, not learned.

Bridge: Mercury is a learned router waiting to happen. It already serves many models by hand-written rules; my LLM Router / MERA replace those rules with a policy that picks the cheapest model still meeting the quality bar — the 51.8%-serving-cost lever, applied to their platform.

Magical Listing — the shipped agentic pipeline (eBay's product)

AI-native listing flow: seller photographs an item → VLM + LLM agents generate title, category, item specifics, and a price estimate. Impact as of Q1 2026: >25% less listing time, >50% higher new-listing creation rate, 500M listings created cumulatively; won an AI Breakthrough Award.

Bridge: it's a multi-step agentic pipeline (ingest → identify → extract → title → price), and each step is a model-choice decision — exactly the per-step routing problem MERA benchmarks. At 500M listings, a few points of per-step serving cost is real money.

Machine translation — cross-border trade as a revenue driver (eBay's published work)

Cross-border trade is a structural moat, and MT quality directly converts into international GMV. LiLiuM's 65k multilingual tokenizer (DE/ES/IT/FR emphasis) is built for it; a separate RAG title-translation line of work tackles the genuinely hard case — ~11-word product titles, jargon and model numbers, so sparse that chrF is chosen over BLEU.

Bridge: my Diffusion Weights paper is evaluated on WMT16, so MT is native ground for me — and the interesting eBay twist is that product-title MT is harder than sentence MT (sparse, jargon-heavy), which is still an open problem, not a solved one.

Recommendations, search & ads ranking (eBay's published work)

Near-real-time re-ranking under a hard latency budget (P99 ≤ 120 ms is the reported design constraint), on eBERT embeddings. A recurring pattern across search relevance and advertiser-keyphrase work: an LLM teacher/judge distilled into a cross-encoder or bi-encoder student for production-latency serving.

Bridge: "buy good labels from the big model, serve with the small one" is the same efficiency move as my pruning work (>80% of weights removed at proxy Dice >0.95 on nnU-Net; unstructured, so no measured speedup yet) and my routing — expensive signal, cheap serving. (The full end-to-end design of this system lives in Flagship: Search Ranking & Recs; don't rebuild it here.)

The product knowledge graph (eBay's proprietary asset)

A structured backbone — taxonomy, ontology, attribute-value structures over tens of millions of SKUs — that general LLMs simply don't have. It's what lets Magical Listing fill accurate item specifics.

Bridge (the honest one — no forced routing angle): the KG is what makes a small specialist viable at all. Structured domain grounding is why a 4B model can punch above a 27B one — it isn't reconstructing product knowledge from parameters. That's the substrate under the small-finetuned-beats-big result.

Why eBay — the authentic version

This is genuine, not flattery. My thesis is that AI is moving from a scaling race to a systems race — what matters is how you train, route, compress, deploy, and apply models under real constraints. eBay is the rare place where that's not a slogan but the business model: an asset-light marketplace where every efficiency point flows to the bottom line. So the team is already building my thesis into production — LiLiuM's tokenizer, the vocabulary-compression work, the small-finetuned-beats-big VLM result — and the routing problem I've published on is sitting, unsolved, inside Mercury. It's a team that both ships (Magical Listing, 500M listings) and publishes (ACL/EMNLP/EACL/SIGIR/ECAI), which is the research-that-deploys balance I want, and it's in Amsterdam, where I already am, next to UvA and CWI.

Say it like this:

"I'm not looking for a bigger GPU budget — I'm looking for the place where doing more with less is the whole game. eBay's an asset-light marketplace, so serving cost and listing quality flow straight to the bottom line, and the team is already building the thing I believe in: LiLiuM's tokenizer, the vocabulary compression, a finetuned 4B beating a zero-shot 27B. My routing and compression work is the same bet from a different angle — and honestly, the learned router that Mercury doesn't have yet is exactly what I've published on. Plus I'm already in Amsterdam, next to UvA."

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY — DO THIS, ~15 MIN, BEFORE THE ROUND]: Sit down at eBay.com (and open the Magical Listing flow — start listing an item from a photo) and lock in ONE true, specific observation you can say out loud. Options: (a) a real search/purchase of yours — a niche part, collectible, or recommerce find — name the item and what the experience was like; or (b) list a real item you own via Magical Listing and note ONE concrete thing (e.g., the title/category it auto-generated, whether the item specifics were right, how fast it was). Write the one sentence here once you have it. A real, first-hand observation makes "Customer Focus" land far harder than any stat — do NOT fabricate one; use only what you actually saw.
