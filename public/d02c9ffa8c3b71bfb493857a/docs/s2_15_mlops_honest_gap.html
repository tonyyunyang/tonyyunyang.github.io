The Honest MLOps-Gap Script

The one thing to memorize word-for-word. Read this last, the night before, and again on the walk in. When any interviewer probes "how much production MLOps have you actually done?" — this is the answer. Say it once, cleanly, the same way everywhere.

Why this matters

Interview 1's engineering half and Interview 2's design round both probe deep on production ops — canary releases, live drift monitoring, on-call. This is your known gap: your deployments have been research and shadow-eval scale, not a live on-call rotation. That's fine. What sinks candidates is bluffing it — an invented ops war-story unravels under a single follow-up, and eBay explicitly scores "Act With Integrity." The winning move is the opposite: know the machinery cold, own the gap in one clean clause, and pivot to the thing you genuinely own — efficiency and serving cost. Honest scores higher than flawless-sounding fabrication here, every time.

So you say it once, the same way, everywhere. Not a fresh improvisation each time it comes up — one rehearsed, precise answer. That consistency is itself a credibility signal.

⚠ Trap: the failure mode is drifting into "well, when I deployed to production I set up the canary..." — you didn't, at that scale, and the next question exposes it. Stay on the rehearsed script. One clause of gap, then pivot. Never embellish.

The canonical script — say it exactly like this

Say it like this (to "how much production MLOps have you done?"):

The efficiency side I've owned end to end — I've built and benchmarked routing, pruning, and generative training-acceleration systems, so the inference-optimization and cost levers are second nature to me. What I haven't personally run is a large-scale production on-call rotation with live canary releases and real-time drift monitoring — my deployments have been at research and shadow-eval scale. But I know exactly how that machinery works — shadow then canary then A/B with automated rollback, drift monitoring on both the input and the decision distributions, and proxy metrics while labels are delayed — and it's the fastest gap I'd close. I'd rather be precise about that than overstate it.

That's the whole thing. It concedes the gap in one clause ("what I haven't personally run is...") and spends the rest proving you know the machinery and where your real strength is. Don't add to it in the moment.

Your real hands-on stack (confirm once, then reuse)

Before the follow-ups, lock down the two sentences that draw the line between what you've personally run and what you know by design. Confirm these once here — the Serve & Monitor and Build & Train Q&A docs mirror these exact two sentences, so you rehearse them a single time and reuse them everywhere. Getting caught overstating either one is the same integrity trap as the ops war-story.

Serving stack (to "what have you served models with?"):

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: "I've actually run vLLM for inference serving hands-on, and I know SGLang, Triton Inference Server, and TensorRT-LLM by design — their tradeoffs and when I'd reach for each — but haven't operated them in production myself." (Default assumes vLLM is the one you've truly launched; adjust which is hands-on vs. by-design to match reality.)

Distributed-training stack (to "how have you trained at scale?"):

⚠ [CONFIRM TONY]: "I've personally launched multi-GPU training with PyTorch DDP, and I understand FSDP and ZeRO sharding for when the model won't fit on one device, though my hands-on runs were DDP-scale." (Default assumes DDP is what you've personally driven and FSDP/ZeRO is by-design knowledge; correct if you've actually launched sharded training.)

Say each the same way every time. One clause of what you've run, one clause of what you know by design — no drift into claiming the by-design tools as lived experience.

The two follow-up landmines

They will almost always push once more to check the machinery is real, not memorized. These two are the likely follow-ups. Answer with the mechanic and the threshold first — that's what proves it's genuine.

"OK — so what would you actually monitor?"

Say it like this:

Two distributions, not one. Input drift — PSI or a KS test on the feature distributions; the rule of thumb is PSI under 0.1 is stable, 0.1 to 0.2 is watch, above 0.2 is act, tuned per feature by importance. And the decision/output distribution — if the model's own predictions suddenly shift, that's an early warning before any label lands. The hard part is labels are delayed — a purchase or a chargeback lags by days — so while I wait I lean on proxy metrics: prediction drift immediately, business signals like click-through shortly after, and for generative outputs, LLM-as-judge on a sampled slice calibrated against a small golden set. Underneath all of it, the operational SLOs — TTFT, TPOT, p99 latency, and goodput, the fraction of requests hitting all their SLOs at once.

The beats, in order: drift on both input and decision distributions → PSI/KS with concrete thresholds → delayed-label proxy metrics → LLM-as-judge on a sample → latency/goodput SLOs. Hit them in that order; it reads as someone who's thought about the whole ladder, not just recited "monitor for drift."

Trap: don't stop at "I'd monitor for drift." That's the answer that sounds researcher-not-engineer. The distinction that earns the point is input drift versus decision/output drift, plus the delayed-label problem — those are the details a real operator knows.

"How would you roll back a bad model?"

Say it like this:

The fastest rollback is one you designed in before you shipped. Prerequisite is a versioned, immutable model registry — so "the previous good model" is an exact, reproducible artifact with its lineage, not something you hope to rebuild. Rollout is staged: shadow to check it's safe on real traffic, canary to ramp a small slice while watching SLOs, then A/B for the business decision. Rollback is then trivial — set the canary split back to zero, or with blue-green flip traffic to the known-good environment atomically — and I'd wire it to trigger automatically on an SLO or quality regression rather than waiting for a human to notice. Keep the previous version warm so the switch is instant. The subtle trap is state: if the new model changed a feature schema, a naive rollback leaves you inconsistent, so you version features and keep them backward-compatible.

The beats: versioned immutable registry → shadow → canary → A/B → automated rollback on SLO/quality regression → keep the previous version warm. The state/schema caveat at the end is the senior signal — it shows you've thought past the happy path.

Trap: if you claim you've operated automated rollback on-call at scale, you'll get drilled on the paging policy and the incident you handled — and you don't have one. Describe the machinery in the abstract ("I'd wire it to..."), not as lived on-call experience. The script already conceded that; stay consistent with it.

The one-line rule

Know the machinery cold. Own the gap in ONE clause. Pivot to efficiency. Never bluff.

Everything behind the machinery — the drift tests, rollout strategies, and SLOs — lives in your engineering Q&A prep on serving, deployment, and monitoring, with the same honesty framing baked into each answer. This doc is the compressed, memorize-it version of that framing; other prep docs point back here whenever the gap comes up. When it does: this script, once, the same way.
